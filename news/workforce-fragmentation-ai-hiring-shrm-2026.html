HR & Workforce

70% of Employers Cannot Fill Roles — Workforce Fragmentation Peaks as AI Bias and Talent Gaps Dominate SHRM 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

The human resources industry enters 2026 facing a paradox that defines the modern workforce: organizations have more tools than ever to find, assess, and manage talent, yet nearly 70 percent of them struggled to fill full-time positions in 2025. SHRM's State of the Workplace 2026 report and its annual trends forecast paint a picture of an industry at an inflection point — where AI adoption is accelerating but trust in AI hiring tools is declining, where workforce fragmentation is peaking, and where the CHROs steering corporate strategy are grappling with rising healthcare costs, multigenerational management, and the persistent skills gap. With the SHRM Annual Conference heading to Orlando June 16–19, HR Tech on the fall calendar, and Transform conferences continuing to push the innovation envelope, HR technology exhibitors face an audience that is simultaneously overwhelmed and underserved.

Workforce Fragmentation Reaches Its Peak

SHRM's research identifies workforce fragmentation as the defining structural challenge of 2026. The modern workforce is no longer a single entity that reports to a single location on a consistent schedule. It is a complex mix of full-time employees, contractors, gig workers, remote staff, hybrid participants, and globally distributed teams — each with different expectations, legal requirements, and management needs.

For HR technology exhibitors, fragmentation creates both a problem and a market opportunity. Companies need platforms that can manage diverse worker classifications, ensure compliance across jurisdictions, provide consistent employee experiences regardless of work arrangement, and generate unified workforce analytics. The products that solve fragmentation at scale will command attention at every HR trade show in 2026.

The Multigenerational Challenge

SHRM data shows that 47 percent of HR leaders expect managing multigenerational workforces to become a more significant challenge in 2026. For the first time, many organizations have five generations working simultaneously — from Baby Boomers delaying retirement to Gen Z entering mid-career roles. Each generation brings different communication preferences, technology comfort levels, benefits expectations, and career development models. HR tech companies that offer genuinely flexible, personalized platforms will resonate with buyers who are tired of one-size-fits-all solutions.

AI in Hiring: Promise Meets Scrutiny

Artificial intelligence dominated the HR technology conversation in 2025, and SHRM confirms it will stay center stage in 2026. But the conversation has matured significantly. While AI stole the spotlight as a transformative force, leaders are now confronting its true costs and hidden pitfalls. The focus in 2026 is on ROI and risk management — not just adoption for adoption's sake.

The most telling statistic from SHRM's research: 57 percent of HR leaders expect reducing bias in AI hiring tools to become more prevalent as a workplace issue in 2026. This is not a theoretical concern. Cities and states are passing AI transparency laws, the EEOC is scrutinizing automated hiring systems, and high-profile bias lawsuits are making headlines. For HR tech exhibitors selling AI-powered tools, the ability to demonstrate bias testing, algorithmic transparency, and compliance with emerging regulations is no longer optional — it is the price of admission to the conversation.

This creates an opportunity for HR tech exhibitors to position their products as enablers of organizational transparency — through analytics dashboards, employee feedback systems, and communication platforms.

The Top 5 Workplace Issues for 2026

SHRM's advocacy team has identified five critical workplace issues that will shape the regulatory and business landscape in 2026:

  • Rising healthcare expenses: New state mandates and evolving compliance requirements are making benefits programs more difficult to design and manage. Changes in tax credits and funding models add further uncertainty.
  • Talent shortages and skills gaps: Nearly 70 percent of organizations struggled to fill full-time positions in 2025, often for roles requiring new or rapidly evolving skills. This challenge is expected to grow as technology and business models continue to evolve.
  • AI governance and workplace AI policies: Organizations must develop clear policies for how AI is used in hiring, performance management, and employee monitoring — before regulators do it for them.
  • Workforce flexibility and remote work policies: The return-to-office debate is far from settled. Companies are still calibrating their hybrid models, and employees are pushing back on rigid mandates.
  • Compliance complexity: The patchwork of state and local employment regulations continues to expand, creating operational challenges for multi-state employers.

The 2026 HR Trade Show Calendar

The HR industry's trade show circuit offers exhibitors distinct venues for reaching different buyer segments:

  • SHRM Annual Conference (June 16–19, Orlando): The largest HR conference in the world. The audience spans generalists, specialists, CHROs, and HR business partners across every industry. Best for broad-market HR technology, benefits solutions, compliance tools, and workforce management platforms.
  • HR Tech Conference (Fall 2026): The premier technology-focused HR event. The audience skews toward HR technology buyers, IT decision-makers, and innovation leaders evaluating next-generation platforms. Ideal for AI-powered tools, analytics platforms, and emerging HR technology categories.
  • Transform: Innovation-forward and culture-focused. The audience includes people leaders, talent strategists, and organizational development professionals. Best for companies selling culture technology, employee experience platforms, and learning and development solutions.

Exhibitor Strategies for HR Trade Shows in 2026

  1. Address the AI trust gap directly. Do not just demonstrate what your AI can do — show how you ensure it is fair, transparent, and compliant. Bring bias audit reports, algorithmic explainability documentation, and case studies showing equitable outcomes. This is the conversation buyers will initiate at your booth.
  2. Show fragmentation solutions in action. Build demos around realistic scenarios: managing a workforce with full-time, contract, and gig workers across multiple states. Show how your platform handles compliance, communication, and analytics for the modern fragmented workforce.
  3. Lead with healthcare cost data. Rising benefits costs are the top workplace issue for 2026. If your product touches benefits administration, healthcare navigation, or employee wellness, lead with quantified cost savings and employee satisfaction data.
  4. Prepare for the multigenerational conversation. Buyers will ask how your platform serves employees aged 22 and employees aged 67 with equal effectiveness. Demonstrate personalization capabilities, accessibility features, and communication flexibility.
  5. Invest in systematic lead capture. SHRM Annual alone draws thousands of HR professionals. Use Scannly to capture every badge scan and conversation note digitally, enabling your sales team to follow up with personalized outreach segmented by company size, industry, and identified pain points.

The HR technology market in 2026 is defined by a single dynamic: the gap between what organizations need and what their current tools deliver has never been wider. Exhibitors who arrive at SHRM, HR Tech, and Transform with solutions that close that gap — responsibly, measurably, and at scale — will find an audience of buyers who are ready to invest.
