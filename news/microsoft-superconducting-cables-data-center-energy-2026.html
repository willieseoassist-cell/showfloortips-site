Microsoft Tests Superconducting Cables for Data Centers — Zero-Resistance Power Delivery and What It Means for Trade Shows in 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

On February 10, 2026, Microsoft disclosed that it is actively testing high-temperature superconductor cables inside its data center facilities — a development that could fundamentally reshape how power is delivered to the server racks consuming more electricity than some small nations. The pilot program, conducted in partnership with VEIR, a startup that recently closed a $75 million Series B funding round, has successfully delivered 3 megawatts of power through a single HTS cable. That cable is 10 times smaller than the conventional copper infrastructure it replaces. And it does so with zero electrical resistance, meaning virtually no energy is lost as heat during transmission.

This is not a laboratory curiosity. It is a working pilot inside a production data center environment, backed by the world's second-most-valuable company and funded at a scale that signals commercial intent. For exhibitors in the power delivery, cable manufacturing, energy management, and data center infrastructure sectors, Microsoft's announcement redraws the competitive landscape heading into the spring 2026 trade show season — particularly OFC, Data Centre World, Hannover Messe, and DistribuTECH.

Why Superconducting Cables Matter Now

The physics of superconductivity have been understood for over a century. When certain materials are cooled below a critical temperature, their electrical resistance drops to zero, allowing current to flow without any energy loss. The challenge has always been practical: traditional superconductors require cooling to near absolute zero, making them impractical for most commercial applications. High-temperature superconductors, which operate at the comparatively balmy temperature of liquid nitrogen (around minus 196 degrees Celsius), have been the subject of research for decades but have struggled to achieve cost-effective, reliable operation at commercial scale.

What changed is the data center power crisis. AI workloads have created such extreme power demands that the economics of superconducting power delivery have shifted dramatically. When a single AI training cluster can draw 100 megawatts and data center operators are spending billions on power infrastructure, the cost of cooling an HTS cable system becomes a rounding error compared to the efficiency gains it delivers. Microsoft's calculation is straightforward: if HTS cables can eliminate transmission losses, reduce physical footprint, and simplify power distribution architecture, the total cost of ownership drops even after accounting for the cryogenic cooling systems required.

The VEIR Partnership and the $75M Signal

VEIR, Microsoft's partner in this pilot, is not a university lab or a government research project. It is a venture-backed company that has raised $75 million in Series B funding specifically to commercialize high-temperature superconductor cable technology. VEIR's approach uses an evaporative cooling system that reduces the complexity and cost of maintaining HTS cables at operating temperature, making the technology viable for distributed deployment rather than only in controlled laboratory settings.

The $75 million funding round is significant because it represents investor confidence that HTS cables are approaching commercial readiness. Venture capital at this scale typically indicates a technology that is 18 to 36 months from revenue-generating deployments, not a decade-away research project. For trade show exhibitors, this timeline matters: the products and partnerships that emerge from HTS cable technology will begin appearing on show floors as early as late 2026 and throughout 2027.

Key TakeawayMicrosoft's HTS cable pilot is not a science experiment — it is a production-environment test backed by $75M in venture funding and the purchasing power of a company spending over $80 billion annually on data center infrastructure. The commercialization timeline is measured in quarters, not decades. Exhibitors in power delivery and cable manufacturing need to be tracking this technology now.

The 3MW Pilot: What It Proves

The headline number from Microsoft's announcement is that a single HTS cable delivered 3 megawatts of power. To put that in context, 3 megawatts is enough to power approximately 2,400 average American homes. Delivering that much power through a single cable that is 10 times smaller than its copper equivalent demonstrates several critical capabilities simultaneously.

  • Power density: HTS cables can carry dramatically more current per unit of cross-sectional area than copper, enabling higher power delivery in constrained physical spaces — exactly the conditions found inside data centers where every square foot of floor space has economic value.
  • Efficiency: Zero resistance means zero transmission losses. In a conventional data center, 5 to 8 percent of incoming power is lost in the distribution infrastructure before it ever reaches a server. At the scale of a 100-megawatt facility, those losses represent millions of dollars annually in wasted electricity.
  • Simplification: Fewer, smaller cables mean simpler routing, fewer connection points, and reduced maintenance complexity. Data center operators spend significant capital and operational effort managing cable infrastructure, and any technology that simplifies that infrastructure reduces both cost and failure risk.
  • Scalability: If a single cable can deliver 3 megawatts, the path to delivering 30, 100, or 500 megawatts through HTS systems becomes an engineering challenge rather than a physics problem.

Implications for the Broader Energy and Infrastructure Market

Microsoft's HTS cable pilot has implications that extend far beyond data centers. If superconducting cables prove commercially viable at data center scale, the technology will inevitably migrate to other high-power applications: urban power distribution grids where underground cable routing is constrained, industrial facilities where power density requirements are increasing, renewable energy installations where long-distance transmission losses erode economic viability, and military applications where compact, high-power delivery systems are operationally critical.

For exhibitors at energy-focused trade shows like DistribuTECH and Hannover Messe, the message is clear: superconducting cable technology is transitioning from the research poster session to the exhibition floor. Companies involved in cable manufacturing, cryogenic cooling systems, power electronics, and grid integration should be preparing their messaging and product strategies now.

"The data center industry's power crisis is accelerating the commercialization of technologies that would have taken another decade to reach market under normal conditions. Superconducting cables are a prime example — the economic case has become compelling specifically because AI power demands are so extreme." — Energy infrastructure analyst commentary, February 2026

Trade Show Strategy: Where This Technology Meets Buyers

OFC 2026 — San Francisco, March 30 – April 3

While OFC is primarily an optical fiber and photonics conference, the convergence of power and connectivity in modern data center design makes it increasingly relevant for power delivery exhibitors. Data center architects at OFC are designing facilities where power and connectivity are co-optimized, and HTS cable technology directly impacts those designs. Exhibitors with power delivery innovations will find a receptive audience among the engineers planning next-generation data center layouts.

Data Centre World — London, March 4–5

Data Centre World is where operators evaluate the full stack of data center infrastructure, from power input to rack output. Microsoft's HTS announcement will be a topic of conversation on the show floor, and exhibitors who can contextualize their products relative to this technology shift — whether as complementary solutions, competitive alternatives, or integration partners — will be better positioned than those who ignore it. European data center operators, facing both power constraints and sustainability regulations, are particularly receptive to efficiency-enhancing technologies.

Hannover Messe — Germany, April 20–24

Hannover Messe's energy technology halls are the natural home for superconducting cable technology on the European trade show circuit. With over 130,000 attendees spanning the full spectrum of industrial energy applications, Hannover Messe is where emerging power technologies find their first commercial audiences. Exhibitors in cable manufacturing, cryogenic systems, and power distribution should consider Hannover Messe as a primary launch platform for HTS-related products and partnerships.

DistribuTECH — 2026

DistribuTECH focuses specifically on electric utility transmission and distribution technology, making it the most directly relevant show for superconducting cable applications in grid infrastructure. As utilities explore HTS cables for urban power distribution and renewable energy interconnection, DistribuTECH attendees will be among the earliest adopters evaluating commercial deployments.

Exhibitor Strategies for the Superconducting Future

  1. Position relative to HTS, not in isolation. Whether you sell conventional copper cables, power distribution units, or cooling systems, Microsoft's announcement means your buyers are now asking how your product fits into a world where HTS cables exist. Address that question proactively in your booth materials and demonstrations, even if your product is not directly related to superconducting technology.
  2. Emphasize total cost of ownership. HTS cables will win adoption based on TCO, not unit cost. If your products contribute to lower total infrastructure costs — through efficiency, reliability, reduced maintenance, or space savings — frame your value proposition in the same TCO language that is driving HTS adoption. Data center operators are making 10-to-20-year infrastructure decisions, and they are evaluating every component through a long-horizon economic lens.
  3. Prepare for integration conversations. HTS cables do not replace every component in a power distribution system. They replace the cables but still require terminations, switchgear, monitoring systems, and cooling infrastructure. If your products interface with power cables in any way, prepare your engineering team to discuss how your solutions integrate with HTS cable systems. These conversations will happen at the show whether you are prepared or not.
  4. Target the right audience at each show. Data Centre World attracts data center operators. OFC attracts network architects. Hannover Messe attracts industrial energy engineers. DistribuTECH attracts utility executives. The same technology innovation requires different messaging for each audience. Do not use a single pitch deck across all four shows.

The Broader Shift: From Incremental to Transformational

For the past two decades, improvements in data center power delivery have been incremental: slightly more efficient UPS systems, marginally better power factor correction, modestly improved cable insulation. Microsoft's HTS cable pilot represents something qualitatively different — a step-function improvement in the fundamental physics of power delivery. Zero resistance is not a 5 percent improvement or a 10 percent improvement. It is a categorical change in how electricity moves from source to load.

The exhibitors who thrive during technology transitions are those who recognize the shift early, understand its implications for their product categories, and position themselves as enablers rather than obstacles. The spring 2026 trade show season — OFC, Data Centre World, Hannover Messe, and DistribuTECH — is where that positioning begins. The companies that show up prepared to discuss superconducting integration, zero-loss power distribution, and next-generation infrastructure architecture will be the ones that earn the trust and the orders of the buyers building tomorrow's data centers.
