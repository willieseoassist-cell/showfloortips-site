Tariffs at 13.5% and Climbing: The Real Cost to Your Trade Show Booth

The weighted average U.S. tariff rate on all imports now stands at 13.5%. The effective rate, accounting for behavioral shifts in sourcing and purchasing, is 9.9%. Both figures represent the highest levels since 1946, according to analysis published by the Tax Foundation on February 6, 2026. For the average American household, the tariff regime amounts to an additional $1,300 tax burden this year. For trade show exhibitors, the math is even less forgiving.

The tariff landscape has evolved rapidly. A 25% duty on Canadian and Mexican goods, including those previously qualifying under the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, now stands alongside a 20% tariff on Chinese imports. Bilateral deals have brought selective relief: the India reciprocal tariff was reduced from 25% to 18% on February 2, and a trade and investment agreement with Argentina was signed on February 5. But for the vast majority of goods that cross borders to build, furnish, and equip trade show exhibits, costs have gone up substantially and show no sign of coming back down.

This is not an abstract macroeconomic story. It is a line-item problem on your next exhibit budget.

Where Tariffs Hit the Exhibit Budget

Raw Materials and Fabrication

Aluminum and steel are the structural backbone of custom trade show exhibits. Extrusion systems, truss rigging, counters, shelving, and framework all rely on metals that carry tariff surcharges. The 25% tariff on steel and 10% tariff on aluminum imports, first imposed in 2018 and maintained through the current administration, have already added an estimated 8-12% to the cost of custom metalwork in exhibit fabrication. With the broader tariff regime now layering additional costs onto components sourced from Canada, Mexico, and China, exhibit builders report that material costs have risen 15-20% year over year for 2026 builds.

This hits hardest for exhibitors commissioning custom island exhibits, double-deck structures, and large-format installations. A custom booth that cost $150,000 to fabricate two years ago may now run $175,000 to $180,000 for equivalent specifications.

AV Equipment and Electronics

LED walls, touchscreen kiosks, interactive displays, and AV control systems are overwhelmingly manufactured in Asia. The 20% tariff on Chinese imports applies directly to monitors, LED panels, projection equipment, and the specialized controllers that drive interactive booth experiences. Exhibitors who rely on cutting-edge AV technology to differentiate their booths are absorbing cost increases of 12-18% on equipment purchases. Rental rates have followed, as AV providers pass through higher replacement costs.

Printed Graphics and Signage

Large-format printing substrates, vinyl, fabric backdrops, and specialty signage materials frequently originate from overseas suppliers. While domestic printing capacity has grown, the raw materials, including inks, substrates, and specialty coatings, remain import-dependent. Exhibitors report 8-10% increases in graphics production costs, with premium materials like backlit SEG fabric experiencing even steeper hikes.

Freight and Logistics

Tariffs do not just increase the cost of goods. They complicate the logistics of moving them. Customs processing times have lengthened as importers navigate an increasingly complex tariff schedule. For exhibitors shipping materials or products to a U.S. trade show from overseas, clearance delays can mean the difference between a booth that is ready on move-in day and one that arrives half-assembled. International exhibitors shipping display goods into the U.S. now face both the tariff itself and the administrative burden of classifying goods under the correct harmonized tariff codes, a process that has become significantly more complex as new duties layer atop existing ones.

International Exhibitors Are Reconsidering

The tariff environment is creating a chilling effect on international participation at U.S. trade shows. When an exhibitor from Germany, Japan, or South Korea calculates the cost of shipping booth materials, product samples, and display units into the United States, the tariff surcharge now represents a meaningful addition to an already expensive logistical operation.

Some international galleries and exhibitors are reconsidering their plans to attend U.S. events entirely due to the compounding costs of tariffs, shipping, and an unfavorable exchange rate environment.

This matters for show organizers and domestic exhibitors alike. International pavilions are a draw for attendees. They bring diverse products, buyers from new markets, and the global perspective that makes major trade shows valuable. If tariffs thin the international exhibitor ranks, the shows themselves become less attractive, reducing foot traffic for everyone on the floor.

The data supports the concern. Trade Show Executive reported that booth construction, signage, AV, and catering costs are all climbing, while international exhibitors are actively evaluating whether the ROI of a U.S. show justifies the increased expense. For shows that depend on strong international participation, like CES, PACK EXPO, IMTS, and Hannover Messe USA, this is a strategic vulnerability.

The Supreme Court Factor

Adding legal uncertainty to the equation, the U.S. Supreme Court is expected to rule in early 2026 on the constitutionality of the president's emergency tariff authority. If the court upholds the current framework, the Tax Foundation estimates U.S. importers will pay at least $2.3 trillion in tariffs over the next decade. If the court strikes down or limits the authority, a portion of the current tariff structure could unravel, potentially reducing costs but also creating a period of confusion as businesses adapt to a new set of rules.

For exhibitors planning their 2026 show calendars, this uncertainty is itself a cost. Budgets must account for the possibility that tariffs remain at current levels, increase further, or partially reverse. Planning under three scenarios is more expensive than planning under one.

Seven Ways Exhibitors Can Fight Back

  1. Source domestically where possible. The tariff premium on imported aluminum, steel, and electronics has narrowed the cost gap with domestic suppliers. Request quotes from U.S.-based exhibit fabricators and material suppliers. In many cases, domestic sourcing now offers competitive pricing with the added benefit of shorter lead times and no customs risk.
  2. Rent instead of buying AV equipment. Rather than purchasing LED walls and touchscreens at tariff-inflated prices, work with AV rental houses that amortize equipment costs across multiple clients. Rental rates have increased, but the per-show cost is still lower than purchasing new equipment at current import prices.
  3. Negotiate multi-show contracts with exhibit houses. Exhibit builders facing higher material costs are eager to lock in volume commitments. Negotiate contracts covering two or three shows rather than pricing each event individually. The volume commitment gives the builder purchasing predictability, and you get a lower per-show rate.
  4. Simplify your booth design. This is not a concession. It is a trend. The most effective booths at 2025 and 2026 shows have been cleaner, more open, and less reliant on expensive custom fabrication. Modular systems, rental exhibits, and lightweight fabric structures reduce material costs while often producing a more modern, inviting look than the over-engineered builds of previous years.
  5. Ship early and ship smart. Build extra lead time into your logistics schedule to account for customs delays. If you are an international exhibitor, work with a freight forwarder experienced in trade show logistics and familiar with the current tariff classification landscape. Misclassifying goods can result in unexpected duties or clearance holds.
  6. Apply for temporary import bonds (TIBs) or use ATA Carnets. International exhibitors bringing display goods into the U.S. for exhibition purposes, not for sale, can often use ATA Carnets to avoid paying import duties entirely. This instrument covers goods that will be re-exported after the show. If you are not already using Carnets, this is the single fastest way to reduce your tariff exposure.
  7. Advocate through your industry association. The Exhibition Services and Contractors Association (ESCA), the International Association of Exhibitions and Events (IAEE), and other industry groups are actively monitoring the tariff landscape and engaging with policymakers. Add your voice. The more data these organizations have about tariff impacts on the exhibition industry, the stronger their advocacy.

The Silver Lining: Trade Shows Become More Essential

Here is the counterintuitive truth about tariffs and trade shows. When global trade becomes more complex, more expensive, and more uncertain, the value of face-to-face business increases. Trade shows are where buyers and sellers navigate complexity together. They are where an international manufacturer can meet a domestic distributor and negotiate a relationship that bypasses the most painful tariff exposures. They are where supply chain managers discover alternative sourcing options they would never find through a database search.

The tariff environment makes attending trade shows harder. It also makes attending them more necessary. Exhibitors who internalize this paradox and position their booths as solutions to tariff-related challenges, whether that means sourcing alternatives, compliance expertise, or domestic manufacturing capabilities, will find an audience that is more motivated and more qualified than in any recent year.

The 13.5% average tariff rate is a headwind. But for exhibitors who plan intelligently, source creatively, and message strategically, it is also a tailwind in disguise.
