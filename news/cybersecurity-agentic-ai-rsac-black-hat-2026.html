$10.5 Trillion in Cybercrime Costs and the Rise of Agentic AI — What Exhibitors Need for RSAC and Black Hat USA 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

Cybercrime now costs the global economy $10.5 trillion annually — more than the GDP of every country on Earth except the United States and China. Over 7.5 million cyber incidents were recorded in 2025. The average cost of a data breach has reached $4.45 million, the highest on record. Ransomware incidents now average $5.08 million per event. And 87 percent of organizations rank AI-related vulnerabilities as their fastest-growing cyber risk. These numbers are not projections — they are the reality driving a cybersecurity market that will exceed $240 billion in annual spending in 2026, with 74 percent of large enterprises increasing their security budgets year over year.

Against this backdrop, the cybersecurity industry's two most consequential events return in 2026: RSAC Conference (March 23–26, Moscone Center, San Francisco) and Black Hat USA (August 1–6, Mandalay Bay Convention Center, Las Vegas). RSAC drew nearly 44,000 attendees in 2025 with its "Power of Community" theme continuing into 2026, while Black Hat USA delivers the deeply technical briefings and training that shape the industry's research and defensive agenda. For cybersecurity exhibitors, these two events represent the industry's primary commercial stages — and 2026's threat landscape makes exhibiting more strategically important than ever.

Agentic AI: The Defining Theme of 2026

If 2025 was the year generative AI entered the cybersecurity conversation, 2026 is the year agentic AI dominates it. Agentic AI refers to autonomous security agents that can independently detect, investigate, and respond to threats without constant human supervision. Unlike traditional AI-assisted tools that flag anomalies for human review, agentic AI systems can take defensive action — isolating compromised endpoints, revoking credentials, blocking malicious traffic — in real time.

The promise is compelling: as attack volumes scale beyond human analysts' capacity to respond, autonomous security agents can process thousands of alerts simultaneously and take immediate protective action. The risk is equally significant: autonomous systems making security decisions without human oversight raise questions about false positives, collateral damage, and accountability. Almost every keynote at RSAC 2026 is expected to address how to secure AI-driven supply chains and manage the explosion of machine identities that autonomous agents create.

For exhibitors, agentic AI is both an opportunity and a positioning challenge. If your product incorporates autonomous capabilities, you need clear messaging about governance, transparency, and human-in-the-loop safeguards. If your product helps organizations manage and monitor autonomous AI agents, you have one of the most timely pitches in cybersecurity.

Key InsightThe agentic AI conversation at RSAC and Black Hat will split into two camps: vendors selling autonomous security capabilities and vendors selling tools to govern autonomous security. Both represent enormous market opportunities, but the messaging strategies are fundamentally different. Know which camp you are in and build your booth experience accordingly.

Post-Quantum Cryptography Readiness

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) readiness has moved from an academic exercise to an operational priority in 2026. NIST has standardized several post-quantum algorithms, and government agencies, financial institutions, and defense contractors are actively evaluating and beginning to implement PQC solutions. The timeline pressure comes from "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks — adversaries capturing encrypted data today with the expectation of decrypting it once quantum computers become powerful enough.

For cybersecurity exhibitors, PQC represents a multi-year transition that creates sustained demand for cryptographic assessment tools, encryption migration platforms, key management systems, and consulting services. Organizations need to inventory their cryptographic assets, identify vulnerable systems, develop migration roadmaps, and begin implementing quantum-resistant algorithms — a process that will generate significant purchasing activity across the 2026 conference circuit.

Operational Resilience Under New Regulations

The regulatory landscape for cybersecurity continues to intensify. NIS2 (the EU's Network and Information Security Directive) and DORA (the Digital Operational Resilience Act) are driving new compliance requirements across multiple sectors. These regulations mandate not just preventive security measures but operational resilience — the ability to maintain critical functions during and after a cyber attack.

This shift from prevention to resilience creates demand for business continuity tools, incident response platforms, crisis communication systems, and recovery-focused technologies. Exhibitors whose products help organizations meet NIS2 and DORA requirements have a regulatory tailwind that will persist for years.

The Threat Landscape in Numbers

  • 91% of successful breaches started with a phishing attack, making email security and user awareness training perennial needs.
  • Over 305,000 recorded CVEs in the vulnerability database, with projections of 30,000+ new disclosures in 2026 alone, driving demand for vulnerability management and patch automation.
  • Ransomware drove over half of all global cyberattacks, sustaining the market for ransomware prevention, detection, and recovery solutions.
  • The average ransomware incident now costs $5.08 million, including ransom payments, recovery costs, business disruption, and reputational damage.

RSAC vs. Black Hat: Choosing Your Stage

  • RSAC Conference (March 23–26, Moscone Center, San Francisco): The larger, more commercially focused event. RSAC draws nearly 44,000 attendees including CISOs, security directors, IT leaders, compliance officers, and security analysts. The exhibit floor is massive, with hundreds of vendors ranging from startups to global security brands. RSAC is where budgets get allocated and vendor shortlists get built. Registration starts at approximately $2,500 for standard briefings. The "Power of Community" theme for 2026 emphasizes collaboration and shared defense.
  • Black Hat USA (August 1–6, Mandalay Bay, Las Vegas): The more technically focused event, beginning with four days of hands-on training followed by two days of briefings. Black Hat attracts security researchers, penetration testers, red team operators, and technical leaders who value deep-dive research presentations and exploit demonstrations. The exhibit floor is smaller but highly engaged, with an audience that evaluates products based on technical merit rather than marketing claims.

Exhibitor Strategies for Cybersecurity Shows in 2026

  1. Address the AI security conversation explicitly. Whether your product uses AI, secures AI, or helps organizations govern AI, you need a clear AI narrative. In 2026, every CISO is grappling with AI risk, and exhibitors who can provide clarity and actionable solutions will command attention. If your product does not involve AI, be prepared to explain how it fits into an AI-augmented security architecture.
  2. Show detection-to-response timelines. With breach costs averaging $4.45 million and ransomware incidents reaching $5.08 million, the financial case for faster detection and response has never been stronger. Build your booth demonstrations around time-to-detect and time-to-respond metrics. Show exactly how your product compresses the window between initial compromise and containment.
  3. Differentiate your RSAC and Black Hat approaches. If you exhibit at both events, adjust your booth strategy. RSAC demands business-oriented messaging that speaks to CISOs and security directors making procurement decisions. Black Hat demands technical depth that earns credibility with researchers and practitioners who influence vendor selection through technical evaluation.
  4. Leverage the regulatory compliance angle. NIS2, DORA, and evolving U.S. regulations create urgency for compliance-oriented security products. Frame your product around specific regulatory requirements and demonstrate how it simplifies audit preparation and reporting. Compliance-driven buyers often have committed budgets and shorter sales cycles.
