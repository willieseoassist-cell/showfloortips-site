DHS Just Shut Down — If You Exhibit at ISC West, Here’s What It Means for Your Pipeline

ShowFloorTips Research Team February 14, 2026 9 min read
Share:

At 12:01 a.m. on Saturday, February 14, 2026, the Department of Homeland Security ran out of money. DHS funding lapsed—a partial government shutdown triggered by a bipartisan impasse over ICE reform after two U.S. citizens were shot dead by immigration officers in Minneapolis. Congress left Washington for a week-long recess with no continuing resolution in sight. The lapse could extend for weeks. If you sell security technology, border surveillance systems, maritime defense equipment, or any product that flows through the DHS procurement pipeline, your spring trade show strategy just changed overnight.

What Exactly Shut Down—and What Didn’t

This is not a full federal government shutdown—it is a DHS-specific funding lapse, which makes it simultaneously narrower in scope and surgically devastating to the security industry. The entire apparatus of homeland security is running on fumes, administrative inertia, and the legal fiction of “essential” versus “non-essential” designations.

What continues: 93% of ICE and CBP personnel remain on the job, funded by carry-over allocations from last year’s massive appropriations—$75 billion for ICE and $65 billion for CBP. TSA screeners continue working at airports across the country, though without pay. The Secret Service maintains protective details. FEMA keeps responding to active disasters.

What stops: Government contractors do not get paid. No invoices are processed. No new contracts are awarded. No modifications to existing contracts are executed. No purchase orders flow. The procurement machinery of the third-largest federal department—responsible for billions in annual technology acquisitions—has ground to a halt. Aircraft and boat maintenance has been deferred. The Coast Guard has suspended routine patrols. Non-essential employees, including the administrative staff who process contracts and manage vendor relationships, have been furloughed.

“The people who buy security technology for the federal government are sitting at home. The people who approve contracts are furloughed. The people who process payments are offline. If your pipeline depends on DHS dollars, your pipeline is frozen.”

The Minneapolis Catalyst and the Political Timeline

The proximate cause matters because it determines how long this lasts. The shooting deaths of two U.S. citizens by ICE officers ignited a political firestorm that transcended usual partisan lines. Democrats demanded sweeping reforms to ICE use-of-force protocols and independent oversight. A surprising number of Republicans, facing constituent outrage in swing districts, signaled openness to reform measures that would have been unthinkable months ago. But the specifics proved impossible to negotiate before the DHS funding authorization expired.

Unlike previous shutdowns resolved through clean continuing resolutions, this one is anchored to a substantive policy dispute with deep emotional resonance. Congress did not leave for recess because they were close to a deal—they left because they were far apart. Multiple congressional sources told NPR, Axios, and the Washington Post that the lapse could extend for weeks, potentially stretching into early March—dangerously close to spring trade show season.

ISC West opens in Las Vegas on March 23. Border Security Expo follows shortly after. Sea-Air-Space brings the maritime defense community together. AFCEA TechNet convenes the defense and intelligence IT ecosystem. Every one of these events depends on government buyers being present, funded, and authorized to make procurement commitments.

Key Takeaway

This is not a procedural shutdown that resolves in 48 hours. The underlying policy dispute—ICE reform after the Minneapolis shootings—has no obvious compromise path while Congress is in recess. Exhibitors should plan for a lapse lasting through at least early March, with residual procurement disruptions extending well beyond.

ISC West: The Show Most Directly in the Crosshairs

ISC West—the International Security Conference & Exposition, March 23–27 at the Venetian Expo in Las Vegas—is the largest security trade show in the Western Hemisphere. More than 30,000 attendees and nearly 1,000 exhibitors across physical security, access control, video surveillance, cybersecurity, and fire and life safety. In a normal year, the floor buzzes with uniformed DHS officials, procurement officers from CBP and TSA, and representatives from dozens of agencies under the DHS umbrella. These are not casual attendees—they are buyers with budgets and authority.

A prolonged DHS shutdown changes ISC West in three critical ways. First, government attendance will decline. Furloughed employees cannot attend trade shows. Even essential personnel are operating under emergency protocols that do not include conference travel. Expect a 30–50% decline in DHS-affiliated badge scans if the shutdown persists into mid-March. Second, procurement conversations hit a wall. Even if the shutdown ends before ISC West opens, contracting officers return to backlogs. Government buyers may be in “listening mode” rather than “buying mode.” Third, contractor exhibitors face cash flow pressure. Companies expecting February payments from completed DHS contracts are sitting on unpaid invoices with no timeline for resolution. For smaller contractors, this can be existential—and some may pull back their ISC West investment to preserve cash.

“Every week the shutdown continues, the pipeline damage compounds. It is not just about the contracts that freeze during the lapse—it is about the six-month procurement cycle that gets pushed back because every contracting officer comes back to a desk full of catch-up work.”

Cybersecurity companies that sell to both DoD and DHS will find half their customer base operating normally while the other half is dark. TechNet messaging needs to emphasize DoD and IC use cases while acknowledging the DHS disruption.

The Contractor Payment Crisis: Follow the Money

The single most damaging aspect for the trade show ecosystem is the complete cessation of contractor payments. DHS component agencies—CBP, ICE, TSA, Coast Guard, CISA, Secret Service, FEMA—collectively spend tens of billions annually on security technology. When payments stop, the cash flow crisis cascades through the entire supply chain.

  • Large defense primes (L3Harris, Leidos, SAIC, Booz Allen Hamilton) have balance sheet reserves to absorb weeks of delayed payments. They will maintain their trade show presence. But their subcontractors—often small and mid-size companies—may not be so fortunate.
  • Mid-tier contractors with $50M–$500M in annual DHS revenue face a difficult decision: spend $80,000–$200,000 on an ISC West booth while government receivables are frozen? The show floor may feel noticeably thinner in this segment.
  • Small businesses and startups are the most vulnerable. Many operate on thin margins with limited credit lines. A two-week delay in payments can trigger payroll problems. A four-week delay can threaten viability. The ISC West booth may become an unaffordable luxury precisely when they most need the pipeline it generates.

The irony is cruel: companies most dependent on DHS contracts are least able to afford trade show participation during a shutdown, yet trade shows are where they build the relationships that sustain their government business.

TSA Without Pay: When Airport Disruptions Hit the Show Floor

TSA workers screening passengers without paychecks is the most politically visible consequence of the lapse. During the 2018–2019 shutdown, TSA sick-out rates spiked to 10% at some airports, leading to checkpoint closures and multi-hour security lines. If similar dynamics emerge in March 2026, getting to ISC West in Las Vegas becomes logistically harder—compounding an already difficult business environment for exhibitors.

More broadly, companies that sell screening equipment, credential authentication systems, and passenger processing technology to TSA face a double hit: payments are frozen and the agency is operating under emergency conditions that make any new procurement or pilot program impossible.

Exhibitor Action Item

If you are a DHS contractor exhibiting at ISC West, reassess your booth staffing and travel budget now. Consider a smaller, more targeted presence rather than a full-scale build-out. Preserve cash for operations while maintaining enough visibility to protect long-term relationships. The shutdown will end—your reputation should not be what suffers in the meantime.

What Smart Exhibitors Are Doing Right Now

Experienced exhibitors know that crisis creates opportunity for those who respond strategically. Here is what the most sophisticated security companies are doing in the hours after the shutdown took effect:

1. Pivoting to Commercial and International Buyers

The DHS pipeline is frozen, but the commercial security market is not. Nor are international government buyers. Smart exhibitors are recalibrating ISC West messaging to emphasize commercial applications, enterprise security, and allied government markets. The surveillance system you were positioning for CBP border deployment is also relevant to critical infrastructure operators, multinational corporations, and international defense agencies. The companies that pivot fastest to non-DHS buyers will suffer the least pipeline damage.

2. Pre-Positioning for the Post-Shutdown Surge

Every government shutdown ends with a predictable pattern: a surge of pent-up procurement as contracting officers rush to deploy frozen budgets. Smart exhibitors are using the shutdown to refine proposals, update GSA schedule pricing, and prepare compliant documentation to move immediately when funding resumes. ISC West establishes the relationship; the post-shutdown wave closes the deal.

3. Prioritizing Government Attendees Who Do Show Up

Not every government buyer will be absent. Some DHS functions continue, and employees of DoD, DOJ, and the Intelligence Community are unaffected. Government attendees who make the effort to come to ISC West during a shutdown are, by definition, the most committed buyers in the ecosystem. They deserve your best booth experience, your most senior staff, and your most substantive demonstrations.

4. Documenting the Disruption

Companies that carefully document shutdown-related costs—trade show expenses for events with reduced government attendance, carrying costs on unpaid invoices, opportunity costs from deferred procurements—will be better positioned to negotiate favorable terms when contracts resume. This is not about seeking sympathy; it is about having data to support your pricing in a disrupted market.

Timeline Scenarios: When Does This End?

The duration of the lapse determines the severity of its impact on spring trade show season:

Scenario 1: Resolution by March 1

Congress returns and quickly passes a continuing resolution. Government buyers return to work, contractor payments resume within 1–2 weeks, and the spring season proceeds largely as planned. Residual impact is limited to a compressed procurement timeline. This scenario requires a political breakthrough that does not appear imminent.

Scenario 2: Extended Lapse Through Mid-March

Negotiations drag on through mid-March. ISC West is directly impacted: fewer uniforms on the floor, fewer procurement conversations, more of a commercial and international orientation. This is the scenario most analysts consider most likely—and the one exhibitors should plan for.

Scenario 3: Prolonged Shutdown Into April

If the ICE reform debate becomes a prolonged standoff, the shutdown extends past ISC West and begins affecting Border Security Expo, Sea-Air-Space, and the broader spring calendar. Exhibitors face months of frozen procurement and accumulated cash flow pressure. This is the worst case, but given the depth of the political divide, it is not implausible.

Historical Precedent: What Past Shutdowns Tell Us

The 2018–2019 government shutdown—the longest in U.S. history at 35 days—is the most relevant precedent. Multiple trade shows saw significant declines in government attendance. Federal IT events and the SHOT Show, which depends on law enforcement and military buyers, reported reduced government presence. The recovery period was marked by a procurement surge that overwhelmed contracting offices, delaying new awards for months.

The 2013 shutdown, lasting 16 days, showed a similar pattern: immediate disruption followed by chaotic recovery as agencies raced to catch up before fiscal year end. Trade shows during or after the lapse reported reduced government attendance but intensified engagement from government buyers who did attend.

The lesson: shutdowns compress and distort the procurement cycle without eliminating it. The money DHS was going to spend does not disappear—it gets delayed. The contracts get pushed back. The buyers still need your products—on a different timeline. Exhibitors who maintain their presence through the disruption capture the pent-up demand when it releases.

The Bottom Line

The DHS shutdown that began at 12:01 a.m. on February 14, 2026, is a stress test for the security industry’s trade show ecosystem. The immediate effects are painful: frozen payments, furloughed buyers, deferred procurements. But the industry has weathered shutdowns before, and the fundamental dynamics that make security trade shows essential—concentrated buyer access, hands-on technology demonstrations, relationship-building in a trust-dependent market—do not change because Congress cannot agree on an appropriations bill.

The shows will go on. The money will eventually flow. The question is whether your strategy is ready for the show that actually happens—not the show you planned for six months ago. Exhibitors who diversify their buyer targets, preserve cash intelligently, and position for the post-shutdown procurement surge will emerge in the strongest competitive position. The shutdown is a pipeline delay, not a pipeline destruction. Plan accordingly.
