Global Energy Storage Market Passes 100 GW Annual Installations as Sodium-Ion Scales Up for 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

The global battery energy storage system (BESS) market has crossed a threshold that seemed improbable just five years ago. In 2025, annual installations surpassed 100 GW of power capacity for the first time, with approximately 315 GWh of energy capacity deployed worldwide — representing 51 percent year-over-year growth. Grid-scale projects alone accounted for nearly 240 GWh of that total, driven by the accelerating buildout of renewable energy infrastructure, persistent grid reliability challenges, and an increasingly compelling economic case for storage as wholesale electricity markets evolve toward real-time pricing and capacity auctions. The trajectory for 2026 is even more aggressive: industry forecasts project over 450 GWh of new operational capacity coming online this year, a pace that would double the cumulative global installed base in a single calendar year.

This is not merely a story about lithium-ion batteries getting cheaper, although that remains a powerful force. The 100 GW milestone marks a structural transformation in how the world generates, transmits, distributes, and consumes electricity. Storage is no longer an accessory to the renewable energy transition — it is the enabling infrastructure without which that transition cannot proceed. And the technology landscape itself is diversifying at a pace that is creating new market segments, new supply chains, and new exhibition opportunities at the trade shows that serve the energy storage industry. Sodium-ion batteries are moving from laboratory curiosities to gigawatt-scale supply agreements. Flow batteries are finding their economic niche in long-duration applications. Iron-air technology is attracting hundreds of millions of dollars in investment. The era of "storage equals lithium-ion" is ending, and what replaces it will reshape the competitive landscape for years to come.

Understanding the 100 GW Milestone: What the Numbers Actually Mean

To appreciate the significance of the 100 GW annual installation threshold, it helps to understand the distinction between power capacity (measured in gigawatts) and energy capacity (measured in gigawatt-hours), and why both metrics matter. Power capacity describes the maximum rate at which a storage system can charge or discharge — essentially, how much electricity it can inject into or absorb from the grid at any given moment. Energy capacity describes the total amount of electricity the system can store, which determines how long it can sustain that maximum output.

The 100 GW power capacity milestone means that the world installed enough new storage in a single year to provide instantaneous power equivalent to roughly 100 large nuclear reactors. The 315 GWh energy capacity figure means that these systems can collectively sustain that output for approximately three hours at full power — reflecting the dominance of two-to-four-hour lithium-ion systems in the current deployment mix. As the industry shifts toward longer-duration storage, the ratio of energy capacity to power capacity will increase, meaning future installation years may show even more dramatic GWh growth relative to GW growth.

The 51 percent year-over-year growth rate is particularly notable because it represents acceleration on an already large base. In 2024, global installations were approximately 209 GWh, which was itself a record. Growing 51 percent on top of that level required not just incremental demand increases but structural expansions in manufacturing capacity, supply chain throughput, grid interconnection processing, and project development pipelines. The fact that the industry delivered this growth without catastrophic bottlenecks suggests that the supply chain has matured to a point where sustained high growth rates are achievable — a critical signal for investors, policymakers, and exhibitors evaluating the market's long-term trajectory.

Grid-Scale Dominance and the Utility Business Model

Grid-scale projects — utility-owned or independently developed storage facilities connected directly to transmission or distribution networks — accounted for nearly 240 GWh of the 315 GWh installed in 2025. This dominance reflects the economic logic of scale: larger projects achieve lower per-unit costs through procurement efficiencies, reduced balance-of-system expenses as a percentage of total cost, and more favorable financing terms. A 200 MW / 800 MWh grid-scale project can negotiate battery cell prices, EPC contracts, and interconnection agreements that are simply not available to a 10 kWh residential system or even a 1 MWh commercial installation.

The utility business model for grid-scale storage has also matured significantly. Five years ago, most grid-scale storage projects were justified primarily by ancillary service revenues — frequency regulation, spinning reserve, and voltage support. These services are valuable but represent relatively small market pools that saturate quickly as more storage capacity comes online. Today's grid-scale projects are increasingly justified by energy arbitrage (buying low, selling high in wholesale markets), capacity market participation (receiving payments for being available to generate during peak demand periods), and renewable energy firming (co-locating storage with solar or wind generation to provide dispatchable clean power).

This diversification of revenue streams has made grid-scale storage bankable in a way that was not possible even three years ago. Project finance structures that combine multiple revenue streams can achieve debt-service coverage ratios that satisfy conservative lenders, enabling higher leverage and lower equity requirements. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle: better project economics attract more capital, which funds more projects, which creates larger markets for storage equipment and services, which drives costs down further.

"The 100 GW milestone is significant not because of the number itself, but because of what it proves: the global energy storage supply chain can scale at the pace required to meet decarbonization targets. Three years ago, serious analysts questioned whether manufacturing, installation, and grid interconnection capacity could grow fast enough. That question has been answered."

— BloombergNEF, Global Energy Storage Outlook 2026

The 2026 Forecast: 450 GWh and the Road to Terawatt-Scale Storage

Industry forecasts for 2026 project over 450 GWh of new operational capacity, which would represent approximately 43 percent growth over the 2025 figure. While this growth rate is lower than the 51 percent achieved in 2025, the absolute volume increase — roughly 135 GWh of additional deployment compared to the prior year — would be the largest single-year increment in the industry's history. The market is transitioning from percentage-growth-driven narratives to volume-driven narratives, a maturation pattern familiar from the solar PV and wind energy sectors.

Several factors underpin the 450 GWh forecast. First, the project pipeline is visible: storage projects typically require 18 to 36 months from development initiation to commercial operation, and the projects expected to come online in 2026 were largely committed by mid-2024 or earlier. Grid interconnection queues in the United States, Europe, Australia, and China contain hundreds of gigawatts of storage capacity in various stages of development. While not all queued projects will reach operation — attrition rates of 40 to 60 percent are common in interconnection queues — the volume of committed projects provides a high degree of confidence in the near-term deployment trajectory.

Second, manufacturing capacity has expanded dramatically. Chinese battery manufacturers — led by CATL, BYD, EVE Energy, and REPT Battero — have invested tens of billions of dollars in new production lines specifically designed for energy storage applications. These production lines use lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry, which has become the dominant technology for stationary storage due to its superior cycle life, thermal stability, and lower cost compared to nickel-manganese-cobalt (NMC) alternatives. Global LFP production capacity now exceeds 1 TWh annually, providing ample supply for the projected 450 GWh of storage deployment plus the growing LFP EV market.

Third, policy support continues to strengthen. The U.S. Inflation Reduction Act's standalone storage investment tax credit, which provides a 30 percent ITC for storage projects regardless of whether they are co-located with renewable generation, has transformed the economics of grid-scale storage in the United States. The European Union's revised Electricity Market Design, adopted in late 2025, explicitly recognizes storage as a distinct asset class and removes several regulatory barriers that previously hindered deployment. Australia's Capacity Investment Scheme is directly contracting storage capacity to ensure grid reliability as coal plants retire. India's ambitious renewable energy targets increasingly include storage mandates that are creating a rapidly growing market.

Europe's Storage Surge: 160 Percent Growth and the Negative Pricing Signal

While China continues to dominate global storage deployment in absolute terms, accounting for roughly 60 percent of worldwide installations, Europe's trajectory in 2025 was remarkable. European BESS installations grew 160 percent year-over-year, driven by a combination of accelerating renewable energy deployment, retiring thermal generation capacity, and increasingly dysfunctional wholesale electricity markets that make the economic case for storage impossible to ignore.

The European storage market has two distinct segments with very different characteristics. Germany leads the distributed storage segment, where residential and commercial batteries paired with rooftop solar PV have become mainstream products. Germany's high retail electricity prices, strong solar irradiance by Northern European standards, and well-established installer networks have created a market where more than 70 percent of new residential solar installations include battery storage. The typical German residential system — a 10 kWp solar array with a 10-15 kWh battery — can reduce grid electricity purchases by 70 to 80 percent, providing a payback period of six to eight years even without subsidies.

The United Kingdom dominates Europe's utility-scale storage segment. The UK's capacity market, which pays generators and storage operators for making capacity available to the grid, provides a reliable revenue floor that de-risks project finance. Combined with substantial energy arbitrage opportunities created by the UK's volatile wholesale market and growing frequency response requirements as synchronous generation retires, the UK has attracted more utility-scale storage investment per capita than any other European market. Projects exceeding 500 MW are now in development, scales that would have been considered extraordinary for any market just three years ago.

The Negative Pricing Phenomenon

Perhaps the most powerful market signal driving European storage investment is the proliferation of negative wholesale electricity prices. In 2025, some European regions experienced more than 500 hours annually of negative power prices — periods when electricity generators must pay the grid to accept their output rather than being paid for it. These negative pricing events occur when renewable generation exceeds demand and there is insufficient flexibility (storage, demand response, or export capacity) to absorb the surplus.

Negative pricing creates a remarkable arbitrage opportunity for storage operators. During negative price hours, storage systems are effectively paid to charge: they receive electricity and money simultaneously. They then discharge during peak demand hours when prices are highest. The spread between negative charging prices and positive discharge prices can exceed 200 euros per MWh in extreme cases, generating revenue that accelerates project payback dramatically. As more renewable capacity comes online and negative pricing events become more frequent and severe, the economic case for storage becomes self-reinforcing.

"When wholesale electricity prices go negative for 500 hours per year, the market is sending an unmistakable signal: we have a structural oversupply of intermittent generation and a structural undersupply of flexibility. Storage is the most scalable, most rapidly deployable source of that flexibility. The investment response has been proportional to the urgency of the signal."

— European Energy Storage Association, Market Analysis 2025-2026

For exhibitors at ees Europe and Intersolar, the negative pricing phenomenon is not an abstract market dynamic — it is the primary economic driver that their customers are responding to. Every product, service, or technology that helps storage operators capture arbitrage value more efficiently, predict pricing patterns more accurately, or optimize charge-discharge cycles more effectively has an immediately quantifiable value proposition. The exhibitors who can translate market data into equipment ROI calculations on the show floor will command the most attention from buyers who are evaluating multi-million-euro investment decisions.

Sodium-Ion Batteries: From Laboratory Promise to Gigawatt-Scale Reality

The most significant technology story in energy storage for 2026 is the commercial scaling of sodium-ion batteries. After years of development as a promising but perpetually "five years away" technology, sodium-ion is now entering the market at scales that demand serious attention from project developers, investors, and the broader energy storage ecosystem. Two developments in particular have transformed sodium-ion from a research curiosity into a commercial force.

First, Peak Energy has secured a 4.75 GWh sodium-ion supply deal — one of the largest non-lithium battery supply agreements in history. This contract represents a decisive market validation of sodium-ion technology for stationary storage applications. A 4.75 GWh order is not a pilot program or a technology demonstration; it is a commercial deployment at a scale that requires industrial manufacturing capacity, established quality control processes, and bankable performance guarantees. The fact that a project developer has committed to this volume indicates that sodium-ion cells have passed the technical due diligence required to secure project financing, a critical milestone for any new battery chemistry.

Second, CATL — the world's largest battery manufacturer by capacity — has announced it will launch sodium-ion batteries for energy storage applications in 2026. When the industry's dominant manufacturer commits to a new chemistry, the impact ripples through every level of the supply chain. CATL's entry validates the technology for customers who might hesitate to adopt products from smaller, less established manufacturers. It ensures that sodium-ion will benefit from CATL's massive manufacturing scale, procurement leverage, and quality control infrastructure. And it signals to other battery manufacturers that sodium-ion is a competitive necessity, not an optional research initiative.

Why Sodium-Ion Matters: The Supply Chain Argument

The appeal of sodium-ion batteries extends well beyond their technical characteristics. Sodium is one of the most abundant elements on Earth, available from common salt (sodium chloride) that is effectively unlimited in supply and geographically distributed across every continent. This contrasts sharply with the lithium supply chain, which is concentrated in a small number of countries (primarily Australia, Chile, Argentina, and China for processing) and subject to the price volatility, geopolitical risks, and environmental concerns that accompany any concentrated commodity supply chain.

For energy storage project developers and their financial backers, supply chain diversification is not an abstract benefit — it is a risk management imperative. The lithium price spike of 2022-2023, when lithium carbonate prices increased approximately sixfold before subsequently declining, demonstrated the vulnerability of a supply chain dependent on a single critical mineral. While lithium prices have since stabilized at more moderate levels, the memory of that spike — and its impact on project economics, equipment delivery timelines, and customer confidence — has permanently altered the industry's attitude toward supply chain concentration.

Sodium-ion batteries also eliminate the need for cobalt and nickel, two metals with their own supply chain challenges. Cobalt extraction is concentrated in the Democratic Republic of Congo and carries significant human rights concerns. Nickel supply is subject to Indonesian export policy decisions and Russian geopolitical dynamics. By replacing all three of these concentrated, volatile, and in some cases ethically problematic supply chains with abundant, widely available sodium, sodium-ion technology offers a fundamentally different risk profile that is increasingly attractive to institutional investors, sovereign wealth funds, and development finance institutions.

Technical Performance: Where Sodium-Ion Fits

Sodium-ion batteries are not a universal replacement for lithium-ion. Their energy density is approximately 20 to 30 percent lower than LFP lithium-ion, which means they require more physical space and weight for the same energy capacity. This makes them less suitable for applications where space and weight are critical constraints, such as electric vehicles. However, for stationary energy storage — where systems are installed in fixed locations with ample footprint — the lower energy density is a manageable trade-off, particularly given sodium-ion's other advantages.

Sodium-ion cells demonstrate excellent performance at low temperatures, maintaining higher capacity retention than lithium-ion at temperatures below minus 20 degrees Celsius. This characteristic makes them particularly attractive for storage installations in cold climates — Northern Europe, Canada, northern China, and mountain regions — where lithium-ion systems require energy-intensive heating systems to maintain performance. The reduced or eliminated need for active thermal management at low temperatures improves round-trip efficiency and reduces auxiliary power consumption, both of which contribute to better project economics in cold-climate applications.

Cycle life projections for current-generation sodium-ion cells are competitive with LFP lithium-ion, with manufacturers claiming 4,000 to 6,000 full equivalent cycles at 80 percent depth of discharge. If validated in field operation, these cycle life figures would support project lifetimes of 15 to 20 years in daily cycling applications, which is broadly comparable to the warranties offered on current LFP systems. The safety profile is also favorable: sodium-ion cells are less prone to thermal runaway than lithium-ion alternatives and can be safely discharged to zero volts for transportation and storage, eliminating the complex state-of-charge management requirements associated with lithium-ion shipping.

"The question is no longer whether sodium-ion can work for grid-scale storage. Peak Energy's 4.75 GWh deal and CATL's market entry have answered that definitively. The question now is how quickly sodium-ion can scale to capture its natural market share in stationary applications — and whether incumbents will adapt their supply chains fast enough to compete."

— Wood Mackenzie, Battery Technology Outlook 2026
Key TakeawaySodium-ion is not replacing lithium-ion — it is expanding the total addressable market for energy storage by offering a chemistry with different supply chain characteristics, cost structures, and performance profiles. Exhibitors at ees Europe and RE+ should understand that buyers are now evaluating multi-chemistry procurement strategies, not making binary technology choices.

Beyond Sodium-Ion: Flow Batteries and Iron-Air Gain Cost-Competitiveness

The diversification of energy storage technology extends beyond sodium-ion to encompass flow batteries and iron-air systems, both of which are reaching cost-competitiveness milestones that are opening new market segments. While lithium-ion and sodium-ion are fundamentally short-to-medium-duration technologies (optimized for two to eight hours of discharge), flow batteries and iron-air systems are designed for long-duration energy storage (LDES) applications requiring eight to 100+ hours of discharge capacity.

Flow batteries store energy in liquid electrolytes contained in external tanks, with the power output determined by the size of the electrochemical stack and the energy capacity determined by the volume of electrolyte. This decoupling of power and energy is the fundamental architectural advantage of flow batteries: adding more hours of storage requires only larger tanks and more electrolyte, not more expensive electrochemical cells. For applications requiring 8, 12, or 24 hours of storage, flow batteries achieve a cost crossover with lithium-ion that makes them the more economic choice.

Vanadium redox flow batteries (VRFBs) are the most commercially mature flow battery technology, with several hundred MWh of deployed capacity worldwide. The electrolyte is a solution of vanadium ions in sulfuric acid, which does not degrade over time — giving VRFBs an effectively unlimited cycle life and a system lifetime of 25+ years. The electrolyte itself retains its value at end of life and can be reused or recycled, creating a circular economy model that is attracting attention from ESG-focused investors. The primary limitation of VRFBs has been cost, which has been tied to the volatile price of vanadium. However, zinc-bromine and organic flow battery variants are emerging that use lower-cost, more abundant materials, broadening the flow battery market beyond vanadium.

Iron-Air: The Hundred-Hour Battery

Iron-air battery technology represents perhaps the most ambitious approach to long-duration storage. Companies like Form Energy have developed iron-air systems that use reversible rusting — iron oxidation and reduction — to store and release energy at costs projected to reach one-tenth that of lithium-ion on a per-kWh basis. The raw materials are iron (the most abundant metal on Earth), water, and air. The technology is inherently safe, uses no toxic or scarce materials, and is designed for discharge durations of 100 hours or more.

Form Energy's first commercial project, a 10 MW / 1,000 MWh installation being developed with Great River Energy in Minnesota, represents a 100-hour duration system — capable of providing 10 MW of continuous power for more than four days. This duration addresses a fundamentally different grid challenge than two-to-four-hour lithium-ion systems: not the daily solar peak-to-evening-demand shift, but multi-day weather events (extended cloud cover, wind droughts, polar vortexes) during which renewable generation drops to near zero for days at a time. These events are the most challenging scenarios for grid reliability in a high-renewable future, and addressing them with lithium-ion batteries would require enormously expensive oversizing that iron-air technology can potentially avoid.

The commercial readiness of iron-air technology is still behind lithium-ion and even sodium-ion. Manufacturing scale is limited, and field performance data from large installations does not yet exist. However, the technology has attracted substantial investment — Form Energy has raised over $800 million in funding — and the first commercial deployments expected in 2026 and 2027 will provide the operational data needed to validate performance claims and unlock project finance for larger installations. For exhibitors at energy storage trade shows, iron-air represents a conversation-starting technology that signals forward thinking and long-duration market awareness.

Hybrid Storage Configurations: The New Project Template

One of the most significant trends reshaping energy storage project development is the rise of hybrid configurations that combine storage with renewable generation, conventional generation, or other storage technologies in integrated systems. Over 50 percent of 2026 storage projects in Australia and India are hybrid configurations — typically solar-plus-storage or wind-plus-storage systems that co-locate generation and storage at a single grid connection point.

The economic logic of hybrid projects is straightforward. A solar farm that can only export power when the sun shines has limited value to the grid during evening peak demand, when electricity is most expensive and most needed. Adding storage allows the solar farm to shift its output to peak demand hours, capturing higher wholesale prices and providing greater grid reliability value. The shared grid interconnection reduces balance-of-system costs. The co-located configuration simplifies operations and maintenance. And the combined project may qualify for additional policy incentives — such as the IRA's production tax credit or India's hybrid project incentive schemes — that improve overall economics.

In Australia, the hybrid trend is particularly pronounced. The Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) has identified the need for approximately 46 GW of new dispatchable capacity by 2050 to replace retiring coal-fired generation and meet growing demand. Much of this capacity is expected to come from hybrid renewable-plus-storage projects, which can provide the firm, dispatchable power that the grid requires while maintaining Australia's commitment to emissions reduction. The Capacity Investment Scheme, which conducts competitive auctions for new dispatchable capacity, has awarded contracts to numerous hybrid projects, providing the long-term revenue certainty that enables project finance.

India's hybrid storage market is driven by different but equally powerful dynamics. India's solar resource is among the best in the world, but its transmission network is severely constrained in many regions, making it difficult to transport power from solar-rich areas (Rajasthan, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu) to demand centers (Delhi, Mumbai, Bangalore). Hybrid solar-plus-storage projects can mitigate transmission constraints by storing surplus solar generation and discharging it during periods when the transmission network has available capacity. India's Solar Energy Corporation (SECI) has issued multiple tenders for hybrid projects, creating a pipeline of several GW that is expected to drive storage deployment in 2026 and beyond.

Multi-Technology Hybrid: Lithium-Ion Plus Long-Duration

An emerging variant of the hybrid concept combines different storage technologies in a single project. A system might use lithium-ion batteries for fast-response, short-duration applications (frequency regulation, ramp rate control) while using flow batteries or compressed air storage for longer-duration energy shifting. This multi-technology approach optimizes the cost-performance trade-off by deploying each technology where its characteristics are most valuable, rather than asking a single technology to serve all functions.

Several developers are exploring lithium-ion plus iron-air hybrids, where the lithium-ion component handles the daily solar shift (four hours of discharge) and the iron-air component provides multi-day resilience (100+ hours of discharge). This combination addresses both the daily and the seasonal storage challenges, providing a comprehensive flexibility solution that neither technology could offer alone at a competitive cost. While commercial deployments of multi-technology hybrids are still in early stages, the concept is generating significant interest among grid operators and regulators who are planning for the long-term reliability needs of high-renewable grids.

Key TakeawayThe shift toward hybrid configurations is changing how storage projects are evaluated, designed, and financed. Exhibitors who can demonstrate experience with integrated hybrid systems — whether through equipment, software, or engineering services — will differentiate themselves in a market that is moving beyond standalone storage toward comprehensive flexibility solutions.

Storage Meets Data Centers: The Convergence No One Expected

Perhaps the most surprising development in the energy storage market is the emergence of battery storage as a primary power solution for data center developers. Storage is now the second most common onsite power solution — behind only natural gas turbines — for data center facilities, a position that would have been unthinkable just three years ago when backup diesel generators dominated the data center power landscape.

The drivers of this convergence are multiple. First, data centers require extraordinarily reliable power, typically specifying 99.999 percent availability (the "five nines" standard). Battery storage systems can provide seamless, instantaneous backup power with zero transfer time — unlike diesel generators, which require several seconds to start and synchronize. The elimination of even a brief power interruption during the generator transfer period can prevent the data corruption, service disruptions, and financial losses that result from momentary outages.

Second, many jurisdictions are imposing restrictions on diesel generator use, driven by air quality concerns and emissions regulations. California, several northeastern U.S. states, and multiple European jurisdictions have tightened permitting requirements for diesel backup generators, making battery storage an increasingly attractive alternative that avoids regulatory friction. Data center developers who specify battery storage instead of diesel generators face simpler permitting processes, shorter project timelines, and reduced community opposition.

Third, battery storage systems at data centers can generate revenue when they are not needed for backup power — which is the vast majority of the time. A data center battery system that sits idle waiting for a grid outage is a stranded asset. A battery system that participates in wholesale energy markets, provides frequency regulation, or reduces demand charges during peak periods while maintaining its backup power function generates revenue that offsets its capital cost. This dual-use model fundamentally changes the economics of data center power infrastructure, transforming a cost center (backup power) into a potential profit center (grid services).

"We are seeing a fundamental reconceptualization of data center power architecture. The battery system is no longer just a UPS — it is a grid asset, a revenue generator, and a sustainability credential, all while providing the same backup function that was its original purpose. This multi-value proposition is why storage has become the default specification for new hyperscale facilities."

— Uptime Institute, Data Center Power Trends 2026

The Hyperscale Effect on Storage Demand

Hyperscale data center operators — Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Meta, and Apple — are among the largest procurers of energy storage globally. A single hyperscale campus may deploy 200 to 500 MWh of battery storage, combining UPS functionality with grid services and renewable energy integration. When multiple campuses are being developed simultaneously across multiple regions, the aggregate storage procurement volumes rival those of major utility-scale storage developers.

This demand is creating a new customer segment for storage equipment manufacturers and system integrators. Hyperscale data center operators have unique requirements: they demand enterprise-grade reliability, sophisticated monitoring and control systems that integrate with their existing facility management platforms, and the ability to scale rapidly across multiple sites with standardized designs. Storage manufacturers who can meet these requirements are accessing a high-margin, high-volume market segment that is growing in parallel with the broader data center construction boom.

For exhibitors at energy storage trade shows, the data center convergence opens a new audience of buyers who may not traditionally attend energy industry events. Forward-thinking storage companies are now exhibiting at data center-focused shows like Data Centre World and DCD Connect in addition to their presence at ees Europe and RE+, recognizing that the data center buyer requires different sales approaches, different technical specifications, and different commercial terms than the traditional utility or IPP customer.

Trade Show Landscape: Where the Energy Storage Industry Convenes

The energy storage industry's rapid growth and increasing technological complexity are reflected in the trade show landscape that serves it. Several events have emerged as essential gathering points for the companies, investors, policymakers, and researchers who are building the storage industry. For exhibitors and attendees evaluating their 2026 trade show strategies, the following events represent the most significant opportunities.

RE+ / Energy Storage North America (September, Anaheim)

RE+ (formerly Solar Power International and Energy Storage International) is the largest clean energy trade show in North America, and its Energy Storage North America (ESNA) track has become the continent's premier exhibition for storage technology, project development, and market analysis. The 2026 edition in Anaheim, California, is expected to draw more than 40,000 attendees and over 1,400 exhibitors, making it the largest RE+ event in the show's history.

For storage exhibitors, RE+ offers several distinct advantages. The show's timing in September provides a natural platform for launching products and partnerships for the following year's deployment season. The co-location with the solar industry creates cross-selling opportunities for companies offering integrated solar-plus-storage solutions. And the concentration of U.S. utility executives, project developers, and financial institutions at RE+ makes it the most efficient venue for business development targeting the North American market.

The ESNA track within RE+ features dedicated programming on storage technology, policy, and market dynamics, including sessions on sodium-ion commercialization, long-duration storage economics, and hybrid project development. Exhibitors who secure speaking slots in these sessions gain credibility and visibility that extends well beyond the show floor. The exhibit hall itself is organized to facilitate easy navigation between storage and solar exhibitors, supporting the hybrid project narrative that is increasingly central to the industry's commercial development.

ees Europe / Intersolar (June, Munich)

ees Europe, held in conjunction with Intersolar Europe at Messe München, is Europe's largest battery and energy storage exhibition. The June 2026 edition is positioned at a critical moment for the European market, which is absorbing the implications of its 160 percent installation growth and grappling with the infrastructure, policy, and market design changes needed to sustain that trajectory.

The ees Europe exhibition floor has expanded significantly in recent years, reflecting the European market's growth and the influx of Asian manufacturers establishing European market presence. Chinese battery manufacturers, including CATL, BYD, EVE Energy, and Hithium, have substantially increased their ees Europe footprints, competing for the attention of European project developers, EPCs, and utilities. European manufacturers, including Sonnen (residential), Fluence (grid-scale), and Northvolt (cell manufacturing), are using ees Europe to demonstrate their competitive positioning against Asian competitors.

For exhibitors, ees Europe offers direct access to the European market's decision-makers at a time when procurement activity is at historic levels. The negative pricing phenomenon, the UK's utility-scale boom, Germany's distributed storage leadership, and the emerging markets of Southern and Eastern Europe all create specific opportunities that require market-specific messaging and sales strategies. Exhibitors who arrive in Munich with pan-European market knowledge and localized product offerings will outperform those who treat Europe as a monolithic market.

The Intersolar Europe co-location is particularly valuable for companies offering solar-plus-storage solutions. The overlap between the solar and storage buyer communities is substantial, and the physical proximity of the exhibition halls encourages cross-pollination between attendees who might not otherwise encounter storage products. Exhibitors should design booth messaging that speaks to both standalone storage and integrated solar-plus-storage applications, reflecting the market reality that an increasing share of European storage capacity is deployed in hybrid configurations.

Energy Storage Summit (London)

The Energy Storage Summit in London serves a more targeted audience than the large Munich or Anaheim exhibitions. Focused specifically on the UK and European storage markets, the Summit brings together a curated audience of project developers, investors, utilities, and policymakers who are directly involved in storage project financing, development, and operation. The attendee profile skews toward senior decision-makers, making the Summit an exceptionally efficient venue for business development conversations that would require multiple meetings to replicate at larger shows.

The Summit's programming is notably sophisticated, reflecting the maturity of the UK storage market and the complexity of the revenue stacking models that underpin UK project economics. Sessions on capacity market strategy, ancillary service optimization, wholesale market trading, and project finance structures provide substantive content that goes well beyond the introductory-level programming found at many energy events. For exhibitors, this programming creates a highly educated audience that is prepared to evaluate products and services on their merits rather than requiring basic market education.

The London location also provides natural access to the UK's financial community, which is among the world's most active in energy storage investment. Private equity firms, infrastructure funds, and commercial banks with energy storage lending programs are well-represented at the Summit, creating networking opportunities that can directly facilitate project financing discussions.

Solar & Storage Live

Solar & Storage Live, with editions in the UK, Africa, Southeast Asia, and other regions, serves the installer and distributor community that drives distributed storage deployment. While the large exhibitions focus primarily on grid-scale projects and utility-scale technology, Solar & Storage Live provides a platform for residential and commercial storage products, installation services, and the software platforms that manage distributed energy resources.

The distributed storage market has different buying dynamics than the grid-scale segment. Purchasing decisions are made by installers, electricians, and small business owners rather than by utility procurement teams and project finance professionals. Sales cycles are shorter. Product demonstrations and hands-on experience are more influential than technical specifications and financial models. Solar & Storage Live's format — which emphasizes product demonstrations, training sessions, and installer networking — is designed to serve these dynamics.

For exhibitors targeting the distributed storage market, Solar & Storage Live offers exceptional ROI per attendee because the audience is almost entirely composed of active buyers and influencers rather than the broader mix of researchers, students, policymakers, and general interest attendees found at larger events. The regional editions also provide access to emerging markets where distributed storage is growing rapidly, including sub-Saharan Africa (where off-grid and weak-grid applications drive demand) and Southeast Asia (where rapid solar adoption is creating residential storage opportunities).

Exhibitor AlertThe energy storage trade show calendar now requires a multi-event strategy. RE+ serves the North American market, ees Europe covers the European market, the Energy Storage Summit targets senior decision-makers, and Solar & Storage Live reaches the distributed installer community. Companies with global ambitions should evaluate a coordinated presence across at least two of these events to capture the full breadth of the storage market opportunity.

Exhibitor Strategy for Energy Storage Trade Shows in 2026

The energy storage market's growth is attracting an unprecedented number of new exhibitors to storage-focused trade shows, intensifying competition for attendee attention and making strategic exhibition planning more important than ever. The following framework addresses the key strategic considerations for exhibitors planning their 2026 trade show presence.

1. Lead with Economics, Not Just Technology

The energy storage buyer in 2026 is not evaluating technologies in the abstract — they are evaluating investment returns. Every product, service, or technology on the exhibition floor should be presented in the context of its economic impact: how does it reduce LCOS (levelized cost of storage)? How does it improve revenue capture? How does it extend asset life? How does it reduce operating expenses? Exhibitors who can quantify their value proposition in financial terms will outperform those who lead with technical specifications.

This economic framing is particularly important for companies introducing new technologies like sodium-ion batteries, flow batteries, or AI-powered optimization platforms. Buyers are naturally cautious about unproven technologies, and the most effective way to overcome that caution is to present a credible financial case supported by real-world data, third-party validation, and reference installations. Exhibitors should prepare detailed TCO (total cost of ownership) comparisons, ROI calculators, and case studies that allow buyers to evaluate the economic impact of adopting a new technology.

2. Address the Multi-Chemistry Future

The days of "one chemistry fits all" are ending. Project developers and utilities are increasingly evaluating multi-chemistry procurement strategies that match different technologies to different applications within their portfolios. A utility might deploy LFP lithium-ion for daily cycling applications, sodium-ion for cold-climate installations, vanadium flow batteries for eight-hour duration requirements, and iron-air for seasonal resilience. Exhibitors who understand this multi-chemistry reality and can position their products within a broader portfolio context will resonate with buyers who are thinking beyond individual project procurements.

  • Lithium-ion manufacturers: Acknowledge the emerging competition from sodium-ion and flow batteries, and emphasize the areas where lithium-ion retains clear advantages: energy density, round-trip efficiency, and supply chain maturity. Do not dismiss alternative chemistries — buyers will view this as either ignorance or defensiveness.
  • Sodium-ion companies: Provide transparent, detailed comparisons with LFP lithium-ion, acknowledging trade-offs (lower energy density, earlier-stage manufacturing) while quantifying advantages (supply chain resilience, cold-climate performance, cost trajectory). The Peak Energy 4.75 GWh deal and CATL's market entry are powerful proof points — use them prominently.
  • Flow battery and LDES providers: Focus on the applications where your technology is uniquely suited. Do not compete with lithium-ion on four-hour duration — compete on eight-plus hours where the economic crossover favors your architecture. Provide LCOS comparisons at various durations to demonstrate where the value proposition emerges.
  • System integrators and EPC firms: Position yourself as chemistry-agnostic, capable of designing and deploying projects using whatever technology best serves the application. This neutrality is increasingly valued by buyers who want a single integration partner across their multi-chemistry portfolio.

3. Showcase Software and Digital Capabilities

The energy storage market is maturing beyond hardware procurement into a software-defined operations model. Battery management systems, energy trading platforms, predictive maintenance algorithms, and fleet optimization tools are increasingly the differentiating factor between storage assets that achieve their target returns and those that underperform. Exhibitors should invest in software demonstrations, data visualizations, and live dashboard displays that show attendees how their digital tools translate into tangible operational and financial outcomes.

AI-powered optimization is particularly topical for 2026. Machine learning algorithms that predict wholesale price movements, optimize charge-discharge strategies, and forecast maintenance needs are moving from research to commercial deployment. Exhibitors offering these capabilities should provide quantified performance comparisons between AI-optimized and rules-based operations, demonstrating the incremental revenue that AI-driven trading and scheduling generate.

4. Prepare for the Hybrid Conversation

With over 50 percent of 2026 storage projects in Australia and India being hybrid configurations, and the hybrid trend accelerating in Europe and North America, every exhibitor must be prepared to discuss how their products function within hybrid systems. This means understanding the technical integration challenges (shared inverters, coordinated control systems, grid connection management), the commercial structures (single-entity ownership versus tolling agreements), and the regulatory frameworks (how hybrid projects are classified and compensated in different markets).

Exhibitors who can demonstrate hybrid system expertise — through case studies, simulation tools, or reference installations — will access a larger share of the market than those who can only discuss standalone storage applications. The hybrid conversation is also an entry point for cross-selling: a storage equipment manufacturer who can discuss solar panel compatibility, a software provider who can demonstrate integrated solar-plus-storage optimization, or an EPC firm that can design and build complete hybrid projects all benefit from the market's structural shift toward integrated systems.

Policy and Regulatory Landscape: What Exhibitors Need to Know

Energy storage policy is evolving rapidly across every major market, creating both opportunities and compliance requirements that exhibitors must understand to serve their customers effectively. The following overview covers the most significant policy developments affecting storage markets in 2026.

United States: The ITC Effect

The Inflation Reduction Act's standalone storage investment tax credit (ITC) has been the single most impactful policy development for the U.S. storage market. Prior to the IRA, storage systems only qualified for the ITC if they were charged primarily from solar generation, creating an artificial coupling between the two technologies. The standalone ITC eliminated this requirement, making any storage project — regardless of its charging source or application — eligible for a 30 percent federal tax credit. This policy change unlocked standalone storage projects in markets where solar-plus-storage was not the optimal configuration, including regions with strong wind resources, areas with transmission constraints, and applications focused on grid reliability rather than renewable integration.

The ITC's domestic content bonus, which provides an additional 10 percent credit for projects using domestically manufactured components, is creating new dynamics in the storage supply chain. U.S. manufacturers of battery cells, inverters, and balance-of-system components are marketing their domestic content credentials as a competitive differentiator against imported alternatives. Exhibitors at RE+ should be prepared to discuss their domestic content strategy, including which components qualify for the bonus and how their supply chain is evolving to increase domestic content over time.

European Union: The Revised Electricity Market Design

The EU's revised Electricity Market Design, adopted in late 2025, includes several provisions directly relevant to energy storage. The recognition of storage as a distinct asset class — rather than classifying it as either generation or consumption depending on the context — resolves a regulatory ambiguity that has hindered storage deployment across the EU for years. Storage assets can now participate in all electricity markets (wholesale, balancing, capacity) under clear, consistent rules that apply uniformly across member states.

The revised market design also strengthens the economic signals that drive storage investment. The extension of long-term contracts for capacity (reliability options and capacity mechanisms) provides revenue certainty for storage projects that supports project finance. The requirement for more granular price signals (moving toward 15-minute settlement periods across all markets) increases the value of storage's ability to respond rapidly to price fluctuations. And the provisions for cross-border flexibility trading create opportunities for storage assets to provide services across national boundaries, expanding the available market for storage operators.

Asia-Pacific: Mandates and Market Reform

China's storage market, the world's largest, is shaped by provincial-level mandates that require new renewable energy projects to include a specified percentage of storage capacity (typically 10 to 20 percent of the renewable project's rated capacity for two to four hours of duration). These mandates ensure a floor level of storage deployment but have also created concerns about quality, as some developers have installed low-cost storage systems that satisfy the mandate technically but provide limited operational value to the grid.

In response, Chinese regulators are tightening performance standards for storage systems and introducing market-based mechanisms that reward storage assets for providing real grid services rather than simply existing. This policy evolution is positive for high-quality storage equipment manufacturers and negative for suppliers of low-cost, low-performance systems. Exhibitors targeting the Chinese market should emphasize performance, reliability, and cycle life rather than competing solely on price.

India's energy storage policy is centered on its ambitious renewable energy targets and the recognition that achieving 500 GW of non-fossil generation capacity by 2030 requires substantial storage to manage intermittency. The Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI) has issued tenders totaling several GW of storage capacity, and India's planned Green Energy Corridor transmission expansion includes storage at strategic locations. For exhibitors, the Indian market offers enormous volume potential but requires navigation of complex procurement processes, local content requirements, and project development partnerships.

Australia's Capacity Investment Scheme (CIS) is arguably the most directly impactful storage policy in the Asia-Pacific region. The CIS conducts periodic auctions for new dispatchable capacity, providing successful projects with long-term revenue contracts (typically 10 to 15 years) that de-risk project finance. The CIS has awarded contracts to battery storage projects, pumped hydro projects, and hybrid renewable-plus-storage systems, creating a diverse portfolio of new flexibility assets. The certainty provided by CIS contracts has made Australia one of the most attractive markets globally for storage investment, attracting international developers and equipment suppliers to a market that was previously dominated by a small number of domestic players.

The Investment Landscape: Who Is Funding the Storage Boom

The energy storage market's growth is supported by an increasingly diverse and sophisticated investor community. Understanding who is funding storage projects and what they prioritize helps exhibitors tailor their messaging and sales approaches to the financial decision-makers who influence equipment procurement.

Infrastructure Funds and Institutional Capital

Large infrastructure funds — Brookfield, Macquarie, Global Infrastructure Partners, IFM Investors — have established dedicated energy storage investment programs that are deploying billions of dollars annually. These investors view storage as critical infrastructure with characteristics (long asset life, contracted revenues, essential service provision) that align with their mandates. They are sophisticated buyers who evaluate storage assets on risk-adjusted returns, asset quality, and long-term revenue certainty rather than headline growth narratives.

For exhibitors, the presence of institutional capital in the storage market means that product quality, warranty terms, and long-term reliability are increasingly important purchase criteria. An infrastructure fund acquiring a 200 MW storage project expects a 25-year asset life with predictable degradation and maintenance costs. Equipment manufacturers who can demonstrate track records, provide bankable warranties, and offer long-term service agreements are favored by the institutional investment community.

Venture Capital and Growth Equity

Venture capital and growth equity firms continue to fund early-stage storage technology companies, particularly in sodium-ion, flow batteries, iron-air, and software platforms. The investment thesis for these firms is that the 450 GWh annual market projected for 2026 is just the beginning — the International Energy Agency projects that annual storage deployment must reach approximately 1.5 TWh by 2030 to meet climate targets. This growth trajectory creates opportunities for innovative technologies and business models to capture substantial market share.

For early-stage exhibitors at energy storage trade shows, attracting VC and growth equity attention can be as important as generating customer leads. Investors attend RE+, ees Europe, and the Energy Storage Summit specifically to evaluate potential investments, and a well-positioned booth or conference presentation can generate inbound investor interest that supplements the company's formal fundraising process.

Corporate and Utility Capital

Major utilities and energy companies are making direct strategic investments in storage assets and storage technology companies. This corporate investment provides not just capital but also market access, operational expertise, and validation that can accelerate a technology company's commercial deployment. CATL's sodium-ion market entry, for example, is funded by internal capital allocation from one of the world's largest companies, providing a scale of investment that no venture-funded startup could match.

Safety and Sustainability: The Industry's Growing Imperatives

As the energy storage industry scales, safety and sustainability considerations are becoming increasingly prominent in buyer evaluations, regulatory requirements, and public discourse. Several high-profile battery storage fire incidents in recent years have heightened scrutiny of storage system safety, and the industry is responding with improved standards, testing protocols, and system designs.

Safety Standards and Testing

The UL 9540A test standard for battery storage system safety has become the de facto benchmark for evaluating thermal runaway propagation in storage installations. This standard tests whether a thermal runaway event in a single cell or module can propagate to adjacent cells, modules, or racks, and it establishes performance criteria for fire suppression, ventilation, and structural integrity. Storage equipment manufacturers who have completed UL 9540A testing and can demonstrate compliant results have a significant competitive advantage, as project developers, utilities, and insurers increasingly require UL 9540A compliance as a procurement prerequisite.

NFPA 855, the Standard for the Installation of Stationary Energy Storage Systems, provides the regulatory framework for storage system installation in the United States. Compliance with NFPA 855 is required by most local building codes and is a condition of occupancy permits and fire marshal approval. Exhibitors should be thoroughly familiar with NFPA 855 requirements and prepared to discuss how their products facilitate compliance, including fire detection, suppression, ventilation, spacing, and emergency response provisions.

Sustainability and Circularity

The energy storage industry is increasingly confronting questions about the sustainability of its own supply chain. Lithium extraction, regardless of whether it is from hard rock mines (Australia) or brine evaporation ponds (Chile, Argentina), has environmental impacts that are receiving growing public and regulatory attention. Cobalt and nickel extraction carry both environmental and human rights concerns. Even LFP chemistry, which avoids cobalt and nickel, still requires lithium and produces manufacturing waste.

Sodium-ion technology addresses some of these sustainability concerns by eliminating the most problematic supply chain elements. The growing interest in sodium-ion among ESG-focused investors and sustainability-conscious buyers reflects a recognition that the "greenness" of energy storage depends not just on the end-use application (replacing fossil fuel generation) but also on the sustainability of the supply chain that produces the equipment.

Battery recycling and second-life applications are emerging as critical elements of the storage industry's sustainability narrative. Several companies are developing hydrometallurgical and direct recycling processes that can recover 95+ percent of critical materials from end-of-life batteries. Second-life applications, which repurpose EV batteries that no longer meet automotive performance requirements for less demanding stationary storage applications, extend the useful life of battery cells and reduce the demand for new raw materials. Exhibitors with recycling capabilities or second-life integration expertise should feature these prominently, as they address a growing buyer concern and represent a potential revenue stream that traditional storage manufacturers are only beginning to explore.

"The next frontier of competition in energy storage will not be fought on cost alone. Safety track records, supply chain sustainability, circularity commitments, and end-of-life responsibility will increasingly differentiate the winners from the also-rans. The companies building these capabilities now are investing in the brand equity that will command premium pricing in a market that is rapidly commoditizing on hardware."

— Clean Energy Associates, Storage Supply Chain Outlook 2026

The Grid of the Future: What Storage Enables

Stepping back from the immediate market dynamics, it is worth considering the broader transformation that the 100 GW storage milestone represents. The electrical grid is undergoing the most fundamental change in its century-long history: from a system designed around large, centralized, dispatchable generation plants to a system built on distributed, intermittent, renewable generation balanced by flexible storage, demand response, and digital control systems.

This transformation requires not just more storage, but smarter storage. The physical batteries are necessary but not sufficient — they must be integrated with advanced forecasting systems that predict renewable generation and demand patterns, trading algorithms that optimize charge-discharge strategies across multiple revenue streams, and grid management platforms that coordinate millions of distributed energy resources in real time. The 450 GWh of storage capacity expected to be installed in 2026 will create a massive new fleet of dispatchable assets that must be operated, maintained, optimized, and eventually recycled — each of these functions representing a market opportunity for service providers, software companies, and equipment manufacturers.

The vision of a grid powered predominantly by renewable energy, balanced by storage, and managed by artificial intelligence is no longer speculative. It is the engineering plan that grid operators in Germany, California, Australia, and the UK are actively implementing. The 100 GW milestone confirms that the storage industry can manufacture, deliver, install, and interconnect the hardware at the scale required. The remaining challenges are regulatory (ensuring market designs properly value storage flexibility), financial (scaling project finance to match the deployment trajectory), and operational (building the workforce and management systems to operate a rapidly growing fleet of storage assets).

Workforce Development: The Hidden Bottleneck

One challenge that receives insufficient attention at trade shows is the workforce required to install, commission, operate, and maintain the rapidly growing fleet of storage assets. A 200 MW storage project requires skilled electricians, power systems engineers, project managers, and safety specialists who understand the unique requirements of battery storage systems. The projected 450 GWh of 2026 installations implies tens of thousands of skilled workers who must be recruited, trained, and deployed globally.

Training and certification programs are emerging to address this need, and companies offering workforce development solutions — training platforms, certification programs, simulation tools, and field service management systems — are finding growing demand from storage developers and operators. Exhibitors in this category should not underestimate the receptivity of the trade show audience: every project developer and utility executive in attendance is grappling with workforce constraints, and solutions that demonstrably accelerate workforce readiness will command attention.

Key TakeawayThe energy storage industry's 100 GW milestone represents a structural transformation in how the world manages electricity, not just a manufacturing achievement. The companies that will capture the greatest value from this transformation are those that combine hardware capability with software intelligence, supply chain resilience with sustainability credentials, and technical excellence with commercial sophistication. The trade shows that serve this industry — RE+, ees Europe, the Energy Storage Summit, and Solar & Storage Live — are where these capabilities are demonstrated, partnerships are formed, and the commercial future of energy storage is negotiated.

Looking Ahead: From 450 GWh to the Terawatt Era

The International Energy Agency's Net Zero Emissions scenario requires approximately 1.5 TWh of annual storage deployment by 2030, roughly triple the 450 GWh forecast for 2026. Achieving this trajectory requires continued cost reduction, manufacturing expansion, supply chain diversification, and policy support. Each of these requirements creates specific opportunities for companies operating in the storage ecosystem.

Cost reduction will come from manufacturing scale, chemistry innovation, and system design optimization. Sodium-ion's emergence as a viable alternative to lithium-ion introduces competitive pressure that will drive cost reduction across all chemistries. Flow battery and iron-air technology maturation will reduce the cost of long-duration storage, expanding the total addressable market by making previously uneconomic applications viable. Software optimization will squeeze more value from existing hardware, improving effective cost-competitiveness without requiring hardware cost reductions.

Manufacturing expansion is already underway at a massive scale, with battery manufacturers globally adding hundreds of GWh of annual production capacity. The geographic distribution of this manufacturing capacity is shifting as well, with new factories in Europe, India, and North America supplementing the Chinese manufacturing base that currently dominates global supply. This geographic diversification reduces supply chain risk and creates localized industrial ecosystems that support installation, maintenance, and recycling operations.

Supply chain diversification — particularly the sodium-ion scaling led by Peak Energy and CATL — reduces the industry's dependence on concentrated critical mineral supply chains. As sodium-ion, flow batteries, and iron-air technology mature, the storage industry will increasingly resemble the broader manufacturing sector in its supply chain diversity, rather than the concentrated, single-chemistry model that characterized its first decade of growth.

Policy support must evolve from deployment incentives (which have been remarkably effective at accelerating the market) to market design reform (which ensures that storage assets can participate fully in electricity markets and receive fair compensation for the services they provide). The EU's revised Electricity Market Design represents a model for this evolution, and other jurisdictions are developing similar frameworks. Exhibitors who can help their customers navigate the policy and market design landscape — through advisory services, market simulation tools, or regulatory expertise — will add value that goes beyond equipment supply.

The path from 450 GWh to the terawatt era is visible, and the technologies, companies, and market structures needed to travel it are being assembled now. The trade shows that serve the energy storage industry are where this assembly happens in real time — where the battery manufacturers meet the project developers, the software companies meet the utilities, the investors meet the innovators, and the collective vision of a storage-enabled grid takes concrete, commercial form. For every participant in this ecosystem, the question is not whether the market will reach terawatt scale, but how quickly, and whether your company will be positioned to capture its share of that extraordinary opportunity.
