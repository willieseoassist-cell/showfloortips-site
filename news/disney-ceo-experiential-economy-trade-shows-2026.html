Disney Just Put a Theme Park Boss in the CEO Chair. Here's What That Tells Exhibitors.

When the Walt Disney Company announced on February 3 that Josh D'Amaro, the 28-year company veteran who chairs Disney Experiences, would succeed Bob Iger as CEO effective March 18, it ended years of succession drama. But it also sent a signal far beyond Hollywood: the world's most powerful entertainment company just decided that the future of its business belongs to the person who runs physical, immersive, in-person experiences.

Not the streaming chief. Not the studio head. The theme park boss.

For trade show exhibitors, this is not celebrity gossip. It is a strategic data point. Disney is a $200-billion-plus company that could have chosen any leadership archetype for its next era. It chose the executive whose expertise is designing environments where people show up in person, engage with a brand through all five senses, and leave with a memory so powerful they pay to come back. If that does not sound like the job description of a great trade show exhibitor, nothing does.

The Experience Economy Has a New Standard-Bearer

The concept of the "experience economy" has been circulating in business literature since Joseph Pine and James Gilmore coined the term in 1998. The thesis is straightforward: as goods and services become commoditized, the next frontier of economic value is staged experiences. Companies that create memorable, engaging, multi-sensory encounters will command premium pricing and deeper customer loyalty.

Disney Parks has been the gold standard of this thesis for decades. Under D'Amaro's leadership, the Experiences division became Disney's most reliable profit engine, consistently outperforming the streaming and studio segments. Revenue per guest climbed as the division invested in immersive technologies, personalized digital experiences via the MagicBand system, and new destination concepts including an upcoming park in Abu Dhabi.

D'Amaro's elevation to CEO tells the market that Disney's board believes the experience-first model is not just a division strategy. It is the company strategy. And when a company of Disney's scale and influence makes that bet, it validates the approach across every industry that competes for human attention, including the trade show industry.

What Disney Gets Right That Most Exhibitors Get Wrong

Trade show exhibitors have been talking about "experiential marketing" for years. The reality on most show floors falls far short of the rhetoric. Here is where Disney's playbook exposes the gap.

Narrative Architecture

Every Disney attraction tells a story. Not a feature list, not a product spec, not a value proposition slide. A story with a beginning, a middle, and an emotional payoff. The queue itself is part of the narrative, building anticipation and context before the guest ever steps into the ride vehicle.

Most trade show booths have no narrative architecture at all. Visitors walk in, see a logo wall, are approached by a rep, and receive a generic pitch. There is no journey, no progression, no emotional arc. The exhibitors who understand Disney's approach are designing booths with a deliberate flow: an entry moment that creates curiosity, a middle section that delivers insight or interaction, and an exit experience that reinforces the brand message and captures the lead.

Environmental Storytelling

Disney Imagineers use a concept called "environmental storytelling," the practice of embedding narrative cues into the physical space so that visitors absorb the story without being explicitly told. Everything from the texture of the walls to the ambient sound to the scent in the air contributes to the experience.

On the trade show floor, this translates to sensory design. What does your booth sound like? Most exhibitors default to either silence or generic background music. What does it smell like? This is not frivolous; scent is the sense most closely tied to memory. What textures do visitors encounter? Tactile engagement with materials, products, and interactive surfaces creates stickier memories than any screen-based demo.

Capacity Management and Flow

Disney Parks are obsessive about guest flow. They study traffic patterns, optimize queue designs, and use real-time data to adjust operations throughout the day. The goal is to minimize friction and maximize the quality of each guest interaction.

Most trade show exhibitors give almost no thought to traffic flow. Booths are designed for aesthetics but not for movement. Demo stations create bottlenecks. Meeting rooms are positioned where they block the main aisle view. Staff cluster near the entrance, intimidating casual browsers. Exhibitors who adopt Disney's flow-management mindset, studying where attendees enter, how they move through the space, and where they dwell longest, will capture more qualified conversations per square foot.

The D'Amaro Playbook: Five Principles for Exhibitors

Drawing from D'Amaro's public statements, Disney's parks strategy, and the operational philosophy that earned him the top job, here are five principles exhibitors can apply immediately.

1. Invest in the Transition Moment

D'Amaro has spoken repeatedly about the importance of the "threshold experience," the moment a guest transitions from the ordinary world into the Disney environment. In parks, this is the walk through the gates. On the show floor, it is the moment an attendee crosses from the aisle into your booth space.

Make that transition feel like entering a different world. A change in flooring material, a shift in lighting, a subtle audio cue. The goal is to signal, instantly and nonverbally, that something different happens here. Exhibitors who master the threshold create a psychological commitment: once an attendee has "entered," they stay longer and engage more deeply.

2. Design for Emotional Peaks, Not Feature Completeness

Disney attractions are designed around one or two peak emotional moments. The rest of the experience supports those peaks. D'Amaro's teams do not try to pack every possible feature into a single ride; they focus on creating one unforgettable moment that guests talk about for years.

Exhibitors typically do the opposite: they try to communicate every product feature, every use case, every competitive advantage in a single booth visit. The result is information overload and zero emotional impact. Choose one story, one demonstration, one visual moment that will make attendees remember your booth above all others. Build everything else around supporting that peak.

3. Personalize at Scale

Disney's MagicBand technology personalizes the park experience for millions of guests simultaneously. Characters greet children by name. Restaurant reservations appear automatically. The system creates the illusion of individual attention within a mass-market environment.

Trade show technology now enables similar personalization. Badge scanning connected to CRM data can alert booth staff to a visitor's company, role, and past interactions before the first handshake. Digital signage can display relevant content based on the attendee's profile. Pre-show outreach can schedule personalized demos that feel custom-built rather than off-the-shelf. The technology exists. The question is whether exhibitors will deploy it with Disney-level intentionality.

4. Train Staff Like Cast Members

Disney calls its employees "cast members" because every person in the park is performing a role in the guest experience. Training is rigorous, standards are high, and the expectation is that every interaction reinforces the brand promise.

Most trade show booth staff receive a 30-minute briefing the morning of the show. They are given talking points but no training on how to read attendee body language, manage booth traffic, transition between conversations, or close with a clear next step. Exhibitors who invest in genuine staff training, not just product knowledge but performance skills, will see measurable improvements in lead quality and quantity.

5. Measure the Experience, Not Just the Output

Disney Parks measures guest satisfaction through dozens of metrics beyond revenue: wait-time satisfaction, interaction quality scores, return-visit intent, and Net Promoter Score by attraction. D'Amaro's division has built a culture where the quality of the experience is tracked as rigorously as the financial results.

Exhibitors overwhelmingly measure outputs: leads scanned, meetings held, demos given. Almost none measure the quality of the experience itself. Post-show surveys, in-booth feedback kiosks, and dwell-time analytics can all provide experience-quality data that is just as important as lead volume for optimizing future show performance.

The Bigger Lesson

Disney's board had every option available. They chose to put the experience architect in charge. In doing so, they validated a truth that the best trade show exhibitors already know: in an era of infinite digital content, overwhelming AI-generated information, and attention scarcity, the companies that win are the ones that create real-world experiences people cannot get anywhere else.

Your trade show booth is your theme park. Your attendees are your guests. The question D'Amaro's appointment should force every exhibitor to ask is this: would someone walk out of your booth and tell a colleague they have to visit it? If the answer is not an immediate yes, the Disney playbook is your blueprint for getting there.
