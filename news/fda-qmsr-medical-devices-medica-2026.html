Medical Devices

FDA’s QMSR Takes Effect February 2 — Surgical Robotics, Closed-Loop Neuromodulation, and the MedTech Landscape Heading into MEDICA 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

The medical device industry crossed a regulatory milestone on February 2, 2026, when the FDA's Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR) officially took effect. The QMSR replaces the longstanding Quality System Regulation (QSR) under 21 CFR Part 820, aligning U.S. medical device manufacturing requirements with ISO 13485:2016 — the international consensus standard used by regulatory authorities worldwide. For the more than 5,000 exhibitors who will converge on MEDICA in Düsseldorf November 16–19, and for companies exhibiting at MD&M and AdvaMed throughout 2026, this regulatory harmonization changes the compliance conversation, the competitive landscape, and the purchasing criteria that buyers bring to the trade show floor.

The QMSR: What Changed and Why It Matters at Trade Shows

The transition from QSR to QMSR is not merely a paperwork exercise. The most significant change is the explicit integration of risk management throughout the entire quality management system. Under the previous QSR, risk management was important but not systematically embedded in every process. Under the QMSR, manufacturers must demonstrate that risk management activities — as defined by ISO 14971 — are woven into design, production, post-market surveillance, and corrective action processes.

For exhibitors at medical device trade shows, this creates an immediate practical impact. Buyers evaluating new products, components, or manufacturing partners will now ask pointed questions about ISO 13485 certification, risk management documentation, and QMSR compliance status. Companies that can demonstrate full QMSR readiness will have a tangible competitive advantage over those still in transition.

The FDA has signaled that inspections conducted after February 2, 2026 will follow the revised Part 820 framework. For exhibitors, this means compliance is not a future goal — it is a present requirement that buyers and partners can verify.

Key InsightThe QMSR's incorporation of ISO 13485:2016 by reference is a major win for companies that sell internationally. Manufacturers who already maintain ISO 13485 certification for EU or other markets will find the QMSR transition straightforward. For exhibitors, this harmonization simplifies the pitch to global buyers at MEDICA and other international shows.

Surgical Robotics and the da Vinci 5 Effect

The medical device innovation pipeline entering 2026 is headlined by next-generation surgical robotics. Intuitive Surgical's da Vinci 5 system found commercial success in 2025, and its continued rollout is reshaping competitive dynamics across the surgical robotics market. For exhibitors in this space — whether selling the robots themselves, accessories, instruments, training simulators, or integration software — the da Vinci 5 has set a new benchmark that every competitor must address.

Modern Healthcare identifies surgical robotics, advanced cardiac care devices, diabetes management systems, and next-generation imaging as the key product categories to watch in 2026. Pulsed field ablation systems, which showed strong performance in 2025, are expected to continue gaining market share as clinical data matures.

Closed-Loop Neuromodulation Gains Ground

MDDI Online projects that more closed-loop neuromodulation devices will gain regulatory approval in 2026. These adaptive devices, which automatically adjust stimulation based on real-time physiological feedback, are showing improved outcomes compared to traditional open-loop systems and represent a significant advancement for treating chronic pain, epilepsy, and movement disorders. For exhibitors developing or supplying components for neuromodulation devices, the 2026 trade show circuit offers an opportunity to reach buyers who are actively evaluating next-generation platforms.

Industry Challenges: Tariffs, FDA Staffing, and Consolidation

MedTech Dive identifies four critical topics to watch in 2026. The ongoing Section 232 investigation could result in tariffs placed specifically on the medical device industry. Questions about whether the expiration of enhanced Affordable Care Act subsidies will affect device demand add another layer of uncertainty. Meanwhile, FDA staff reductions raise concerns about review timelines for new submissions.

Industry consolidation continues to reshape competitive dynamics. Larger companies are acquiring smaller innovators to fill pipeline gaps, altering how smaller companies access capital and reach the market. For exhibitors at MEDICA and MD&M, this consolidation environment means two things: acquisitive companies are actively scanning trade show floors for targets, and smaller exhibitors should prepare for M&A conversations alongside customer development discussions.

The 2026 Medical Device Trade Show Calendar

  • MD&M West / MD&M East: Focused on design, development, and manufacturing of medical devices. The audience includes engineers, quality managers, regulatory affairs professionals, and manufacturing leaders. Best for component suppliers, contract manufacturers, materials companies, and design services firms.
  • AdvaMed (The MedTech Conference): The U.S. industry association's flagship event. The audience includes C-suite executives, government affairs leaders, and strategic business development professionals. Ideal for companies seeking partnerships, M&A discussions, or policy engagement.
  • MEDICA (November 16–19, Düsseldorf): The world's largest medical trade fair. Over 5,000 exhibitors and 80,000 visitors from around the world, covering imaging, health-IT, hospital equipment, laboratory diagnostics, physiotherapy, and consumables. MEDICA is the definitive international stage for medical device companies.

Exhibitor Strategies for Medical Device Shows in 2026

  1. Lead with QMSR compliance documentation. Every buyer conversation in 2026 will include a compliance checkpoint. Have your ISO 13485 certification, QMSR compliance statement, and risk management documentation readily available at your booth. Consider creating a dedicated compliance section in your trade show materials.
  2. Demonstrate clinical outcomes, not just specifications. The medical device market is increasingly outcomes-driven. Bring clinical data, real-world evidence, and health economics analyses that demonstrate how your products improve patient outcomes and reduce total cost of care.
  3. Prepare for the tariff conversation. If Section 232 tariffs are announced before your trade show, have a clear messaging framework about how your supply chain adapts. If they have not been announced, prepare contingency scenarios that address buyer concerns about future pricing stability.
  4. Position for consolidation. If your company is a potential acquisition target, ensure your trade show presence projects the operational maturity and market traction that make you attractive. If you are an acquirer, use the show floor for systematic target identification — the concentration of innovators at MEDICA is unmatched.

The medical device industry in 2026 is defined by the convergence of regulatory harmonization, technological leap, and economic uncertainty. The QMSR has raised the compliance bar, surgical robotics and neuromodulation are redefining clinical possibilities, and consolidation is reshaping the competitive field. Exhibitors who arrive at MEDICA, MD&M, and AdvaMed with clear compliance credentials, strong clinical evidence, and strategic awareness will find an industry that is investing through uncertainty.
