When NVIDIA released its third annual State of AI in Retail and CPG survey at the NRF Big Show in January 2026, the headline number was striking but not surprising to anyone paying attention: ninety percent of retailers plan to increase their artificial intelligence budgets this year, with half of those expecting increases of ten percent or more. What made the survey genuinely newsworthy was the picture it painted beneath that headline — an industry moving from AI experimentation to AI dependency at a speed that is reshaping competitive dynamics, redefining technology investment priorities, and turning every major retail trade show in 2026 into a battleground for the next generation of autonomous retail intelligence.

The survey, which polled hundreds of retail and consumer packaged goods executives, found that 91 percent of companies are now actively using or assessing AI across their operations. That figure has climbed steadily over the three years NVIDIA has conducted the research, but the 2026 results reveal a qualitative shift that goes beyond adoption rates. Retailers are no longer asking whether AI works. They are asking how fast they can deploy AI agents that operate autonomously, make decisions in real time, and drive measurable revenue and cost outcomes without human intervention at every step. The agentic AI war — the competition among technology vendors, platform providers, and retailers themselves to build and deploy autonomous AI systems — is no longer theoretical. It is the defining contest of retail technology in 2026.

Inside the NVIDIA State of AI in Retail and CPG Survey

The Revenue and Cost Impact Breakdown

The 89 percent of retailers reporting revenue gains are seeing those gains across multiple dimensions: improved product recommendations driving higher average order values, AI-optimized pricing strategies capturing additional margin, personalized marketing increasing conversion rates, and predictive demand forecasting reducing stockouts that would otherwise cost lost sales.

The cost reduction figures are equally instructive. The 95 percent of retailers reporting lower costs through AI are achieving those savings through automation of manual processes (inventory counting, demand planning, customer service triage), optimization of logistics and fulfillment operations, reduction of shrinkage and fraud through AI-powered detection systems, and more efficient workforce scheduling driven by AI demand predictions. The 37 percent that have achieved cost reductions exceeding ten percent tend to be retailers that have deployed AI across multiple operational domains simultaneously rather than in isolated use cases.

What Agentic AI Means in a Retail Context

To understand why the 47 percent figure matters so profoundly, it helps to distinguish between the AI that retailers have been deploying for the past several years and the agentic AI that is now entering production. Traditional retail AI has been primarily analytical and advisory: machine learning models that generate demand forecasts, recommendation engines that suggest products, pricing algorithms that propose optimal price points, and computer vision systems that identify shelf conditions. In every case, a human makes the final decision and initiates the action.

Agentic AI removes the human from the decision loop for defined categories of action. An agentic pricing system does not recommend a price change and wait for a category manager to approve it — it executes the price change autonomously based on predefined parameters, real-time competitive data, and inventory levels. An agentic supply chain agent does not flag a potential stockout and suggest a replenishment order — it places the order, selects the supplier, negotiates the terms, and arranges the logistics. An agentic customer service agent does not draft a response for a human to review — it resolves the customer's issue end to end, including processing refunds, issuing replacements, and updating the customer's account.

The shift from advisory AI to agentic AI is not incremental. It is architectural. It requires different technology infrastructure, different governance frameworks, different organizational structures, and different relationships between retailers and their technology vendors. For every company exhibiting at a retail trade show in 2026, the agentic AI transition creates both existential risks and extraordinary opportunities depending on how quickly and effectively they adapt their products and go-to-market strategies.

"Agentic AI is the most significant operational shift in retail since the adoption of enterprise resource planning systems in the 1990s. It does not just automate tasks — it fundamentally changes the relationship between technology and human decision-making. Retailers that get the governance right will achieve operating efficiencies their competitors cannot match." — Industry analysis of NVIDIA's 2026 retail AI survey findings

The Strategic Priorities Driving AI Investment

NVIDIA's survey asked retailers to identify their top goals for AI investment in 2026, and the responses paint a clear picture of where the industry's strategic attention is concentrated. Process speed and efficiency ranked first, cited by 57 percent of respondents. Customer personalization tied for second at 40 percent alongside real-time data-driven decision making. These three priorities form the strategic triangle that will define retail AI investment conversations on every trade show floor this year.

Process Speed and Efficiency: The 57 Percent Priority

The dominance of process speed and efficiency as the top AI investment priority reflects the retail industry's core economic reality: margins are thin, competition is relentless, and every minute of inefficiency represents lost profit. When 57 percent of retailers cite speed and efficiency as their primary AI goal, they are talking about a broad range of operational improvements: faster inventory turns, quicker response to demand signals, more efficient labor scheduling, streamlined supply chain operations, faster checkout experiences, and reduced time-to-market for new products and promotions.

For technology exhibitors, this priority creates demand for solutions that can demonstrate measurable time and cost savings in specific operational workflows. The most effective booth conversations at Shoptalk and NRF will be those where exhibitors can walk a retail executive through a before-and-after comparison: "This process currently takes your team 40 hours per week and costs $X. With our AI-powered solution, it takes 4 hours and costs Y." Specificity and quantification win when efficiency is the priority.

Customer Personalization: The Perennial Priority, Now AI-Powered

Customer personalization has been a top retail priority for over a decade, but the NVIDIA survey data shows that AI is finally making the promise of one-to-one personalization at scale economically viable. The 40 percent of retailers prioritizing personalization are investing in AI systems that can generate individualized product recommendations, personalized pricing and promotion offers, customized marketing content, and tailored shopping experiences across digital and physical channels — all in real time and at a scale that would be impossible with human-driven personalization approaches.

The agentic AI dimension of personalization is particularly significant. When AI agents operate on behalf of consumers — as in the agentic commerce scenarios that are dominating the industry conversation — personalization shifts from the retailer to the agent. The consumer's AI agent already knows their preferences, budget constraints, brand affinities, and past purchase history. The retailer's job is no longer to figure out what the consumer wants; it is to present their products in a way that the consumer's AI agent can accurately evaluate and recommend. This inversion of the personalization paradigm creates new requirements for product data, catalog structure, and merchant API design that will be hot topics at every retail technology event in 2026.

Real-Time Data-Driven Decisions: The 40 Percent Imperative

The 40 percent of retailers prioritizing real-time data-driven decision making are responding to a competitive environment where the speed of insight directly correlates with commercial advantage. In a world where pricing, inventory, and consumer behavior change by the hour, retailers that make decisions based on yesterday's data are at a structural disadvantage compared to those that make decisions based on this minute's data. AI is the only technology that can process the volume, velocity, and variety of real-time data that modern retail generates and translate it into actionable decisions at the speed the market demands.

For exhibitors in the data analytics, business intelligence, and decision automation space, the NVIDIA survey data validates their entire category thesis. The challenge is differentiation: every analytics vendor claims real-time capabilities. The exhibitors that will stand out at Shoptalk, NRF, and Groceryshop are those that can demonstrate genuinely real-time (sub-second) decision-making in live environments, not batch processes marketed as "near real-time."

Key TakeawayThe three strategic priorities identified in NVIDIA's survey — process efficiency (57%), personalization (40%), and real-time decisions (40%) — define the conversation that every exhibitor at Shoptalk, NRF, and Groceryshop needs to be prepared for. If your product does not clearly address at least one of these three priorities, your trade show investment will underperform. Frame your booth messaging, demos, and meeting agendas around these priorities, and bring quantified evidence of your impact on each.

Physical AI: The Next Frontier at 17 Percent

One of the most forward-looking data points in the NVIDIA survey is the finding that 17 percent of retailers are now evaluating physical AI — a category that encompasses warehouse robots, autonomous store systems, computer vision-powered shelf monitoring, automated fulfillment centers, and robotic last-mile delivery. While 17 percent may seem modest compared to the 91 percent overall AI adoption rate, it represents a significant acceleration from previous years and signals that physical AI is transitioning from research-and-development curiosity to active commercial evaluation.

This transition is particularly relevant for NVIDIA because the company's hardware platforms — including its Jetson edge computing modules and Omniverse digital twin technology — are purpose-built for the kind of compute-intensive, real-time processing that physical AI requires. NVIDIA GTC 2026, scheduled for March in San Jose, will be the premier event for physical AI demonstrations, and retailers evaluating these technologies will be paying close attention to the vendor ecosystem that NVIDIA is assembling.

The warehouse robotics segment of physical AI is the most commercially mature, driven by the relentless growth of e-commerce order volumes and the persistent challenge of warehouse labor availability and cost. Companies like Symbotic, Ocado, AutoStore, and Locus Robotics have deployed robotic fulfillment systems at scale, and their presence at retail trade shows has expanded dramatically as retailers accelerate their evaluation timelines. Autonomous Store Systems and Computer Vision

The autonomous store category — systems that use computer vision and sensor fusion to eliminate traditional checkout, monitor shelf conditions, detect shrinkage, and optimize store layouts — is the most visible manifestation of physical AI in the consumer experience. Amazon's Just Walk Out technology, which was widely expected to set the industry standard, has evolved significantly since its initial rollout, and competitors including Grabango, Standard AI, Trigo, and AiFi have established their own deployments across multiple retail formats.

For exhibitors offering computer vision, sensor fusion, edge computing, or checkout-free technology, the 2026 trade show season is a critical window to capture market share before the category consolidates around a small number of platform leaders.

AI-Powered Supply Chain: From Reactive to Predictive to Autonomous

The evolution of AI in supply chain management mirrors the broader trajectory from analytical to advisory to agentic AI. First-generation AI supply chain tools provided better demand forecasts and identified optimization opportunities for human planners to evaluate. Second-generation tools added real-time monitoring and alert capabilities, flagging disruptions and recommending responses. The third generation — agentic supply chain AI — takes autonomous action: rerouting shipments when a port disruption is detected, reallocating inventory across distribution centers based on real-time demand signals, negotiating spot-market logistics rates, and adjusting safety stock levels without waiting for human approval.

For supply chain technology exhibitors, the NVIDIA survey data validates the urgency of the autonomous supply chain message. When 64 percent of retailers report worsening supply chain conditions and 51 percent are prioritizing supply chain investment, the market is ready for solutions that go beyond dashboards and alerts. Retailers want AI systems that can act, not just inform. Exhibitors at Shoptalk, Groceryshop, and NRF who can demonstrate autonomous supply chain capabilities — with appropriate governance guardrails — will find an exceptionally receptive audience.

"The supply chain challenges facing retail are not going to resolve themselves. They are structural, driven by geopolitics, climate, and the fundamental complexity of global trade. Open Source Models: The 79 Percent Consensus

One of the most strategically significant findings in the NVIDIA survey is that 79 percent of retailers view open-source AI models as moderately to extremely important for their AI strategy. This finding has profound implications for the competitive dynamics of the retail AI market and for how technology vendors position their products at trade shows in 2026.

The open-source preference reflects several converging factors in the retail industry's AI strategy. First, retailers are increasingly wary of vendor lock-in, having been burned by proprietary platforms in previous technology cycles. Open-source models provide optionality — the ability to switch providers, customize implementations, and avoid the pricing leverage that proprietary model vendors can exert. Second, open-source models allow retailers to fine-tune and customize AI systems on their own data without sharing that data with third-party model providers, addressing critical data privacy and competitive intelligence concerns. Third, the quality gap between open-source and proprietary models has narrowed dramatically, with models like Meta's Llama, Mistral, and others approaching or matching the performance of proprietary alternatives for many retail-specific tasks.

For technology vendors exhibiting at Shoptalk, NRF, and other retail events, the 79 percent open-source figure means that any product positioned as dependent on a single proprietary AI model will face skepticism from the majority of potential buyers. Exhibitors should be prepared to articulate their open-source strategy, demonstrate model flexibility, and explain how their products can leverage whichever AI models the retailer prefers — whether open-source, proprietary, or a combination.

Key TakeawayWith 79% of retailers viewing open-source AI models as important, exhibitors at every major retail trade show need a credible open-source strategy. Products built exclusively on proprietary models face a skeptical audience. Demonstrate model flexibility, explain your fine-tuning capabilities, and show how retailers can maintain data sovereignty while leveraging the best available AI models — open-source or proprietary.

The Consumer Trust Gap: 50 Percent Remain Cautious

While the NVIDIA survey paints a picture of aggressive retail AI investment, it also highlights a critical constraint on the agentic AI revolution: consumer trust. Fifty percent of consumers remain cautious about fully autonomous AI purchasing — AI agents that make buying decisions and complete transactions without human confirmation. This trust gap is not a minor inconvenience for the agentic AI narrative. It is the single most important factor that will determine how quickly autonomous commerce reaches its projected scale.

The 50 percent figure represents a roughly even split between consumers who are comfortable with AI handling significant portions of their purchasing decisions and those who want to maintain direct control. For retailers deploying agentic AI, this split requires a nuanced approach: systems that can operate autonomously for consumers who are comfortable with delegation while providing transparent oversight and approval mechanisms for those who are not. The one-size-fits-all approach to agentic commerce will not work. Retailers need configurable autonomy levels that adapt to individual consumer preferences.

Building Consumer Trust in Agentic AI

The consumer trust challenge creates specific opportunities for technology vendors in several categories. Transparency and explainability tools that help consumers understand why an AI agent made a particular recommendation or purchasing decision address the information asymmetry that drives consumer caution. Privacy-preserving AI technologies that allow personalization without invasive data collection build trust by respecting consumer boundaries. Gradual autonomy escalation systems that start with AI-as-advisor and progressively expand to AI-as-buyer as consumers build confidence provide the gentle onramp that cautious consumers need.

For exhibitors at retail technology events, the consumer trust data from the NVIDIA survey provides a valuable conversation opener. Rather than leading with the aggressive agentic AI narrative that dominates the industry press, exhibitors who acknowledge the trust gap and present solutions that address it will differentiate themselves from competitors who are exclusively focused on the technology's capabilities without addressing the human factors that determine adoption.

The trust challenge also creates opportunities in the consumer education and communication space. Retailers need tools and strategies for explaining their AI capabilities to consumers in clear, non-technical language that builds confidence rather than triggering suspicion. Content management systems, in-store digital signage, and customer communication platforms that can deliver these messages effectively will find ready buyers among retailers navigating the trust gap.

The term "agentic AI war" is not hyperbole. Understanding the combatants and their strategies is essential for any exhibitor positioning their company in this rapidly evolving landscape.

NVIDIA's position in the agentic AI war is foundational. The company's GPUs power the training and inference workloads that make agentic AI possible, and its software platforms — including NVIDIA AI Enterprise, Omniverse for digital twins, and Metropolis for intelligent video analytics — provide the development tools that retailers and their technology partners use to build AI agents. NVIDIA's role in the agentic AI war is analogous to the role Intel played in the personal computer revolution: the company does not build the applications, but everything runs on its infrastructure.

Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and the Platform Giants

The hyperscale cloud providers are each pursuing distinct strategies in the agentic AI war. Google's approach centers on its Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP) and Agent2Agent (A2A) framework, which aim to establish the interoperability standards for AI agents across the retail ecosystem. Microsoft is leveraging its Azure AI platform and Copilot infrastructure to embed agentic capabilities into the enterprise software stack that retailers already use. Amazon is building agentic AI into every layer of its retail ecosystem, from Alexa-powered shopping agents to autonomous fulfillment centers to AI-optimized marketplace operations.

For exhibitors at retail trade shows, the platform competition creates a complex positioning challenge. Retailers are evaluating which platforms to standardize on, and technology vendors need to demonstrate compatibility with multiple ecosystems without appearing uncommitted to any of them. The exhibitors that navigate this challenge most effectively will be those that position themselves as platform-agnostic enablers — solutions that deliver value regardless of which cloud, which AI model, or which agentic framework the retailer chooses.

AI-powered demand forecasting that reduces fresh product waste is one of the highest-ROI applications of AI in grocery, and exhibitors who can demonstrate measurable waste reduction will capture immediate attention from grocery executives under margin pressure.
  2. Address the cold chain with AI. Cold chain logistics — the temperature-controlled supply chain for perishable products — is one of the most complex and expensive operational challenges in grocery retail. AI solutions for cold chain monitoring, predictive maintenance of refrigeration equipment, and optimized cold chain routing are high-value offerings for the Groceryshop audience.
  3. Position for omnichannel grocery fulfillment. The continued growth of online grocery ordering, curbside pickup, and rapid delivery has created fulfillment complexity that traditional grocery operations were not designed to handle. AI-powered order routing, inventory allocation across fulfillment channels, and workforce scheduling for omnichannel operations are critical capabilities that Groceryshop attendees are actively evaluating.
  4. Connect CPG and retailer data through AI. The CPG-retailer data relationship is a perennial topic at Groceryshop, and AI adds new dimensions to this conversation. Collaborative forecasting, joint inventory optimization, and AI-powered trade promotion effectiveness analysis are areas where CPG companies and their retail partners can create mutual value. Exhibitors offering platforms that facilitate this AI-powered collaboration will find receptive audiences on both sides of the CPG-retailer relationship.
Key TakeawayGroceryshop 2026 is the critical event for AI-driven supply chain and fresh product optimization. With 64% of retailers reporting increased supply chain challenges and 51% prioritizing supply chain efficiency, the grocery industry's unique combination of perishability, thin margins, and fulfillment complexity creates urgent demand for AI solutions that go beyond general retail applications. Exhibitors who bring grocery-specific use cases and ROI data will dominate the conversation.

The Governance Challenge: Agentic AI Requires New Frameworks

As the agentic AI war accelerates, one of the most important and underappreciated challenges facing the retail industry is governance. When AI systems shift from providing recommendations to taking autonomous action, the governance framework must evolve from oversight of human decisions informed by AI to oversight of AI decisions that may not involve humans at all. This governance challenge cuts across every domain that the NVIDIA survey highlights: pricing, supply chain, customer service, personalization, and physical AI.

The governance dimension creates significant opportunities for technology vendors in the compliance, risk management, audit, and AI ethics space. Retailers need tools that can monitor AI agent behavior in real time, detect anomalies that might indicate the agent is operating outside its intended parameters, maintain audit trails of autonomous decisions for regulatory compliance, and provide kill-switch capabilities that allow human overseers to intervene when an AI agent's behavior deviates from acceptable bounds.

Regulatory Landscape and Compliance Requirements

The regulatory environment for AI in retail is evolving rapidly in 2026, with the European Union's AI Act entering full enforcement, individual U.S. states implementing their own AI transparency and fairness requirements, and industry self-regulatory frameworks emerging from organizations like the National Retail Federation and the Consumer Goods Forum. Retailers operating across multiple jurisdictions face a patchwork of compliance requirements that add complexity and cost to AI deployment.

For exhibitors in the compliance, legal technology, and AI governance space, the regulatory complexity creates a compelling value proposition. Retailers need help navigating the compliance landscape, and the exhibitor that can clearly explain the regulatory requirements, demonstrate compliance monitoring tools, and provide implementation guidance will find an audience that is both large (every retailer deploying AI needs compliance) and urgent (regulatory deadlines create time pressure that accelerates purchasing decisions).

Investment Implications: Where the Money Is Going

The NVIDIA survey's finding that 90 percent of retailers plan to increase AI budgets, with 50 percent expecting increases of ten percent or more, represents a massive expansion of the total addressable market for retail AI technology. But understanding where that money will flow requires a more granular analysis of the survey data and the competitive dynamics that are shaping retailer investment decisions.

The Three Budget Tiers

Based on the NVIDIA survey data and broader industry analysis, retail AI spending in 2026 is concentrating in three tiers:

  • Tier 1: Foundation and infrastructure (30–40% of AI budgets): This includes cloud computing costs, GPU infrastructure (increasingly NVIDIA-powered), data platform investments, and the base-layer AI/ML platform licenses that enable all downstream AI applications. Retailers are investing heavily in foundational infrastructure because they have learned that AI initiatives fail when built on inadequate data and compute foundations.
  • Tier 2: Production AI applications (40–50% of AI budgets): This is the largest spending category and includes the AI-powered applications that drive the revenue and cost outcomes the NVIDIA survey measures: demand forecasting, personalization engines, pricing optimization, supply chain planning, customer service automation, and fraud detection. The shift within this tier is from point solutions to integrated platforms that coordinate multiple AI capabilities across the enterprise.
  • Tier 3: Emerging and experimental AI (10–20% of AI budgets): This includes agentic AI deployments, physical AI evaluations, generative AI for content creation and store design, and exploratory initiatives that may not have immediate ROI but position the retailer for the next wave of competitive advantage. The 47 percent of retailers evaluating agentic AI and the 17 percent evaluating physical AI are allocating their Tier 3 budgets to these categories.

For exhibitors, understanding these budget tiers is essential for targeting the right conversations. Tier 1 conversations happen with CIOs and infrastructure teams. Tier 2 conversations happen with business leaders who own P&L responsibility for specific functions. Tier 3 conversations happen with innovation teams, digital transformation leaders, and the C-suite executives who control strategic investment pools. Matching your product to the right budget tier and the right buyer persona is the difference between productive meetings and wasted booth time.

The Consolidation Dynamic

As AI budgets expand, a countervailing force is also at work: vendor consolidation. Retailers that deployed AI through a collection of point solutions — one vendor for demand forecasting, another for personalization, a third for pricing, a fourth for customer service — are increasingly looking to consolidate toward fewer, more integrated platforms. The operational overhead of managing multiple AI vendors, each with their own data requirements, integration architectures, and contract structures, is driving demand for unified platforms that deliver multiple AI capabilities through a single relationship.

This consolidation dynamic creates opportunities for large platform vendors and existential challenges for specialized point solutions. Exhibitors at retail trade shows in 2026 need to decide whether to position as a comprehensive platform (capturing a larger share of wallet from each customer) or as a specialized best-of-breed solution (with deeper capability in a specific domain). Both strategies can work, but they require fundamentally different booth messaging, demo experiences, and sales conversations.

"The retail AI market in 2026 is experiencing a paradox: budgets are expanding while vendor counts are shrinking. Retailers are spending more on AI but buying from fewer vendors. The winners will be the platforms that can deliver breadth and depth simultaneously — or the specialists that are so clearly superior in their domain that retailers cannot achieve the same results from a platform alternative." — Analysis of retail AI investment patterns based on NVIDIA survey data

The Talent and Organizational Challenge

Behind the NVIDIA survey's technology adoption data lies a human challenge that rarely makes headlines but profoundly affects how quickly retailers can realize the benefits of their AI investments: talent. Deploying, managing, and governing agentic AI requires skills that are in short supply across the retail industry. Data scientists, ML engineers, AI ethicists, and the hybrid business-technology leaders who can bridge the gap between AI capabilities and retail operations are among the most sought-after professionals in the market.

This talent constraint creates opportunities for technology vendors that can reduce the skill requirements for AI deployment. Low-code and no-code AI platforms, managed AI services, and pre-built AI models fine-tuned for retail use cases all address the talent bottleneck by enabling retailers to deploy AI with smaller, less specialized teams. Exhibitors offering these capabilities should explicitly position them as talent solutions, not just technology solutions. When a retailer cannot hire the data scientists they need, a platform that delivers AI outcomes without requiring deep data science expertise solves a very real and very urgent problem.

Organizational Restructuring for Agentic AI

The shift to agentic AI is also forcing organizational restructuring across the retail industry. When AI agents take autonomous action in pricing, supply chain, and customer service, the human roles in those functions change fundamentally. Category managers shift from setting prices to setting the parameters within which AI agents operate. Supply chain planners shift from placing orders to designing the constraints and objectives that guide AI agent behavior. Customer service managers shift from reviewing agent scripts to monitoring AI agent interactions and refining the governance frameworks that define acceptable AI behavior.

For consulting firms, systems integrators, and change management vendors exhibiting at retail trade shows, the organizational transformation dimension of agentic AI is a massive growth opportunity. Retailers need help not just implementing the technology but reshaping their organizations to operate effectively in a world where AI agents handle an increasing share of operational decisions. This is a multi-year engagement opportunity that extends far beyond the initial technology deployment.

Looking Ahead: The 2026–2028 Trajectory

The NVIDIA survey captures a moment in time — early 2026 — but the trends it identifies have a clear trajectory that extends well beyond this year. Based on the survey data and broader industry dynamics, here is what the retail AI landscape is likely to look like by 2028:

  • Agentic AI adoption will reach 80 percent or higher. The current 47 percent figure is growing rapidly, and the combination of proven ROI, competitive pressure, and improving technology will push agentic AI from the minority to the vast majority of the industry within two years.
  • Physical AI will move from evaluation to deployment. The current 17 percent evaluation rate will translate into significant deployment by 2028 as warehouse robotics costs continue to decline, autonomous store technology matures, and the economic case for physical AI becomes undeniable in the face of rising labor costs.
  • Consumer trust in autonomous AI purchasing will increase but not reach universal acceptance. The current 50 percent caution rate will decline as consumers gain positive experiences with AI agents, but a significant minority will continue to prefer human control over purchasing decisions. Successful retailers will accommodate both preferences through configurable autonomy levels.
  • Open-source AI models will become the default for retail. The current 79 percent importance rating for open-source models will strengthen as the quality and variety of open-source options continue to improve. Proprietary models will retain advantages in specific, narrow domains, but the broad platform layer of retail AI will be predominantly open-source.
  • AI governance will become a regulated requirement. The current voluntary approach to AI governance in retail will give way to mandatory compliance requirements as regulators respond to the growing autonomy and economic impact of AI agents. Retailers that invest in governance infrastructure now will be advantaged when regulatory requirements formalize.
Key TakeawayThe NVIDIA survey's 2026 data is not an endpoint but a waypoint. The trends it identifies — accelerating AI investment, expanding agentic AI adoption, emerging physical AI, persistent consumer trust challenges, and growing open-source preference — are all on clear trajectories that will intensify through 2028 and beyond. Exhibitors should build their 2026 trade show strategy around the current data while positioning their products and messaging for the accelerated future that the data points toward.

For every company exhibiting at Shoptalk, NRF, NVIDIA GTC, Groceryshop, or any other retail technology event in 2026, the NVIDIA survey data defines the conversation that the industry is having. The exhibitors that provide clear, credible, data-backed answers to these questions will capture the attention, the meetings, and ultimately the budgets of the retail industry's most forward-looking companies.

The agentic AI war is not a future event to prepare for. It is a present reality to compete in. The 2026 retail trade show circuit is the battlefield where the winners will be determined, and the NVIDIA survey data is the intelligence briefing that every exhibitor needs to study before stepping onto the show floor. The companies that internalize this data, align their messaging to its priorities, and back their claims with production proof points will be the ones signing the largest deals in the meeting rooms behind the exhibits. The agentic era has arrived, and the trade show floor is where the industry will decide who leads it.
