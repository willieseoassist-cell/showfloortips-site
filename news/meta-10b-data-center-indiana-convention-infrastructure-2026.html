Meta's $10B Indiana Data Center Breaks Ground as Big Tech's Building Spree Collides with Convention Infrastructure

With Big Tech collectively spending $635-665 billion on infrastructure in 2026, data center construction is now directly competing with convention centers for steel, skilled labor, and electrical capacity. The trade show industry is caught in the crossfire.

Photo via Unsplash

Meta just committed $10 billion to a single data center campus in Indiana, and it is not even close to the largest check being written. Microsoft is spending $80 billion on AI data centers this year. Google has earmarked $75 billion. Amazon is pouring $100 billion into AWS infrastructure. Collectively, Big Tech is on track to spend between $635 billion and $665 billion on data center construction and AI infrastructure in 2026 alone -- a figure that dwarfs the GDP of most countries and is now creating tangible, measurable problems for an industry that few expected to get caught in the blast radius: the trade show and convention sector.

The collision is not theoretical. It is happening right now in steel markets, on electrical grid planning boards, in skilled labor pools across the American Midwest, and in the procurement offices of convention centers from Las Vegas to Chicago. The same structural steel, the same electricians, the same concrete contractors, and the same mechanical engineers that build data centers also build and maintain the convention halls where industries gather to do business. When one sector writes checks this large, every adjacent sector feels the squeeze.

The Resource War Nobody Predicted

Convention centers are infrastructure projects. The Las Vegas Convention Center's West Hall expansion, completed in 2021, required 600,000 square feet of structural steel, thousands of electrical connections, and a small army of specialized tradespeople. McCormick Place in Chicago, the largest convention center in North America, undergoes continuous renovation cycles that consume hundreds of millions in construction resources annually. The Indiana Convention Center in Indianapolis -- located in the same state where Meta is now breaking ground -- has its own $175 million expansion on the drawing board.

Now imagine those projects competing for contractors against a client willing to spend $10 billion on a single campus. Data center construction pays premium rates. Meta, Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are signing long-term contracts with general contractors, locking in steel allocations, and hiring electricians on retainer. The trickle-down effect is brutal: convention center renovation budgets are blowing up, timelines are stretching by six to eighteen months, and some venue operators are quietly shelving expansion plans altogether.

"We put out bids for an electrical upgrade last quarter and got two responses instead of the usual eight. Every qualified contractor in the region is working on data centers. The rates they quoted were 40 percent above our budget." -- Operations director at a major Midwestern convention center

CONEXPO-CON/AGG: Selling Shovels in a Gold Rush

No trade show is better positioned to capitalize on the data center construction boom than CONEXPO-CON/AGG, North America's largest construction trade show. Held every three years in Las Vegas, CONEXPO draws more than 130,000 attendees and 2,800 exhibitors showcasing everything from excavators to concrete pumps. The 2026 edition, scheduled for March, will be dominated by a single theme: who is building the infrastructure that Big Tech is buying?

Data center construction is fundamentally different from commercial real estate. These facilities require reinforced foundations designed to support the immense weight of server racks, redundant power distribution systems, liquid cooling infrastructure that did not exist at scale five years ago, and precision concrete work with tolerances measured in millimeters. Equipment manufacturers who can demonstrate data center-specific capabilities at CONEXPO will own the show floor conversation.

What CONEXPO Exhibitors Should Know

  • Data center builders are coming to CONEXPO as buyers. General contractors managing Meta, Microsoft, and Google campuses need specialized equipment for high-precision foundation work, heavy-lift operations for modular data center components, and trenching equipment for massive underground electrical conduit runs. Exhibitors who target this buyer persona with specific use cases will generate the highest-quality leads.
  • Modular construction is the headline story. Data center operators increasingly demand pre-fabricated, modular builds that compress construction timelines from 24 months to 12. Exhibitors showcasing modular construction equipment, precision crane systems, and automated material handling should anchor their demos around speed-to-deployment metrics.
  • Sustainability credentials matter more than ever. Meta and Microsoft have both committed to carbon-negative operations by 2030. Construction equipment manufacturers who can demonstrate lower emissions, electric-powered alternatives, and recycled-material compatibility will find receptive audiences among data center procurement teams.

Data Centre World: The Other Side of the Equation

While CONEXPO addresses the building of data centers, Data Centre World -- held annually in London with regional editions in Frankfurt, Singapore, and now Dallas -- addresses the operating of them. The show brings together colocation providers, cooling system manufacturers, power distribution specialists, and the software platforms that manage facility operations. In 2026, the show floor narrative has shifted from efficiency to urgency: how do you build and operate data centers fast enough to meet demand that is doubling every eighteen months?

For exhibitors at Data Centre World, Meta's Indiana project is a case study to reference and a selling opportunity to pursue. Cooling vendors should demonstrate how their systems handle the thermal loads of next-generation AI chips that draw 700 watts per GPU. Power distribution exhibitors should showcase redundancy architectures that meet the four-nines uptime requirements of AI training workloads where a single interruption can cost millions in lost computation. And everyone should be prepared to discuss the grid constraints that are becoming the single biggest bottleneck in data center deployment.

The Grid Bottleneck: Convention Centers Feel It Too

Data centers in the United States are projected to consume 5 to 8 gigawatts of new electrical capacity in 2026 alone. That demand is straining the same regional power grids that serve convention centers. Several venue operators have reported difficulties securing additional power allocations for expansion projects, with utility companies prioritizing data center connections that guarantee higher revenue per megawatt. For trade shows requiring significant temporary power -- think CES, NAB, or any event with large-scale AV and lighting rigs -- utility constraints could eventually affect what is possible on the show floor.

Construction Trade Shows: Planning Around the Disruption

The construction trade show circuit extends well beyond CONEXPO. World of Concrete (Las Vegas, January), The AHR Expo (HVAC and refrigeration), and dozens of regional building expos all serve segments of the data center construction supply chain. The $635-665 billion infrastructure wave is reshaping attendance patterns at every one of these events.

Attendee profiles are shifting. Where construction trade shows once drew primarily from commercial real estate, municipal infrastructure, and residential building, data center construction managers and procurement teams are now showing up in force. They have specific technical requirements, aggressive timelines, and budgets that dwarf typical construction projects. Exhibitors who recognize this shift and adjust their booth messaging, demo content, and sales team training accordingly will capture a disproportionate share of the opportunity.

The Convention Center Paradox

There is a profound irony in the current situation. The data center construction boom is making it harder and more expensive to build and renovate the convention centers where construction companies showcase their products. The very industry that benefits most from the building spree is being physically constrained by it. Convention center operators in Indianapolis, Phoenix, and Northern Virginia -- all data center hot spots -- report that renovation projects are running 30 to 50 percent over initial budgets, primarily due to labor and material competition from data center construction.

For trade show organizers, the implications are immediate. Show floor expansion plans may need to be delayed. Build-out costs for exhibitors using custom booth construction will rise as the same carpenters and electricians who build booths find more lucrative work on data center sites. Even temporary staffing -- the forklift operators, riggers, and general laborers who set up and tear down trade shows -- is getting harder to secure in markets where data center construction offers steady, year-round employment at higher wages.

What Trade Show Professionals Should Do Now

The data center infrastructure wave is not a temporary disruption. It is a structural shift that will persist for the rest of this decade as AI compute demand continues its exponential growth. Trade show professionals -- whether you are an exhibitor, organizer, or venue operator -- need to adapt to a world where your industry competes directly with the most capital-rich sector on the planet for basic construction resources.

  1. Lock in contractors early. If you use custom booth construction, sign contracts with your build partners 6 to 12 months in advance. The days of last-minute booth builds at reasonable rates are over in data center markets.
  2. Budget for escalation. Add 20 to 30 percent contingency to any construction-related trade show budget. Steel, electrical components, and skilled labor costs are all trending upward and show no signs of stabilizing.
  3. Target data center buyers explicitly. If your products serve any segment of the data center supply chain, update your trade show messaging now. Generic construction positioning leaves money on the table when data center procurement teams are walking the floor with urgent, specific needs.
  4. Watch the grid. For shows requiring significant temporary power, confirm utility availability during your planning phase, not during load-in. Power allocation conflicts are already emerging in data center-heavy markets.

Meta's $10 billion Indiana data center is one project among dozens. The collective impact of Big Tech's infrastructure ambitions is rewriting the economics of construction, the capacity of power grids, and the availability of the skilled labor that every industry -- including the trade show industry -- depends on. The companies and professionals who recognize this shift earliest will be the ones who navigate it most successfully.

The rest will find out the hard way when their booth contractor does not return their call.
