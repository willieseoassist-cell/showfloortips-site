933 Measles Cases and Counting: The Trade Show Industry's Next Health Crisis Is Already Here

ShowFloorTips · February 13, 2026

Measles was supposed to be a solved problem. The United States declared the disease eliminated in 2000. A quarter century later, the virus is surging back with a ferocity that public health officials have not seen in a generation — and the trade show industry, which depends on packing tens of thousands of people into enclosed convention halls, is directly in the crosshairs.

As of February 5, the CDC has confirmed 733 measles cases across 20 U.S. jurisdictions in 2026. South Carolina's outbreak alone has ballooned to 933 cases as of February 10, making it the largest single-state outbreak in decades. Ninety-two percent of all cases are outbreak-associated, meaning they are linked to known clusters rather than isolated incidents. And 859 of those 933 South Carolina cases involve unvaccinated individuals. Cases reached a 30-year high last year, and 2026 is already on pace to shatter that record before spring.

Zero deaths have been reported so far in 2026. But for an industry that learned the hardest possible lesson from COVID-19 — that infectious disease can shut down the entire exhibition ecosystem overnight — waiting for fatalities before taking action is not a strategy. It is negligence.

Why Measles and Trade Shows Are a Dangerous Combination

Measles is not COVID. In some ways, it is worse.

The measles virus is one of the most contagious pathogens known to science. A single infected person can transmit it to 12 to 18 others in an unvaccinated population — compared to COVID-19's original R0 of 2 to 3. The virus can linger in the air for up to two hours after an infected person has left a room. You do not need to shake hands, share a drink, or even be in the same booth. You just need to breathe the same air in the same enclosed space.

Now consider what a trade show is: 10,000 to 200,000 or more people packed into convention halls with recirculated air, shared restrooms, crowded food courts, and shoulder-to-shoulder aisles. Attendees fly in from across the country and around the world, spend three to five days in close contact, then scatter back to their home cities. A single measles case at a major show is not an isolated incident. It is a distribution network.

"Measles is airborne and extraordinarily contagious. Convention centers are essentially large indoor rooms with thousands of people breathing the same air. From an epidemiological perspective, a trade show during a measles outbreak is a textbook superspreader scenario."

— Dr. Peter Hotez, Dean of the National School of Tropical Medicine, Baylor College of Medicine

The timing makes this worse. Two new outbreaks have been reported in 2026, with additional ongoing outbreaks carrying over from 2025. California is now reporting its largest measles outbreak in five years, with 17 confirmed cases. The geographic spread is widening, not narrowing. And the trade show calendar is entering its busiest stretch of the year.

The Shows Most Exposed

Every major trade show scheduled between now and summer is a potential venue for measles transmission. The risk scales with size, international attendance, and geography.

CES in January already drew approximately 180,000 attendees to Las Vegas. SXSW in March will bring over 300,000 participants to Austin. NRF's Big Show in January packed 40,000-plus into New York's Javits Center. HIMSS in March will gather 45,000 healthcare professionals in Las Vegas. These are not theoretical risks. These are specific, imminent, large-scale gatherings happening in a country where measles is actively circulating in 20 jurisdictions.

But the risk is not limited to mega-shows. Any event drawing more than a few thousand people from multiple states creates transmission potential. Regional healthcare conferences, construction expos, food industry shows, and technology summits all share the same fundamental risk profile: enclosed spaces, high density, multi-day attendance, and a geographically diverse attendee base that can carry the virus across state lines.

The International Attendee Factor

Trade shows are inherently global events. Major U.S. exhibitions draw significant percentages of their attendees from countries where vaccination rates are lower than the American average. Exhibitors and buyers travel from regions experiencing their own measles outbreaks. International pavilions, a growing feature at shows like CES, SXSW, and Hannover Messe, concentrate attendees from dozens of countries into compact exhibition areas.

This is not a call for xenophobia. It is an epidemiological reality. Measles does not check passports, but it does exploit gaps in vaccination coverage regardless of where those gaps originate. And the 859 unvaccinated individuals who contracted measles in South Carolina are a stark reminder that the biggest gaps in coverage are domestic.

Measles at a Glance: What Trade Show Professionals Need to Know

  • Incubation period: 7 to 21 days. An attendee infected at a trade show may not show symptoms until they are back home, making contact tracing extraordinarily difficult.
  • Contagiousness: Infected individuals are contagious 4 days before the rash appears. They can be walking the show floor, scanning badges, and shaking hands while actively spreading the virus.
  • Airborne transmission: The virus remains viable in the air for up to 2 hours after an infected person has left the area. Convention center aisles, meeting rooms, and enclosed food courts are high-risk zones.
  • Vaccination protection: Two doses of the MMR vaccine are approximately 97% effective. One dose is about 93% effective. Unvaccinated individuals face the highest risk by a wide margin.
  • Complications: Measles can cause pneumonia, encephalitis, and death. Roughly 1 in 4 people who contract measles require hospitalization. The risk is highest for children, immunocompromised individuals, and pregnant women.

The COVID Lesson: Act Before You Are Forced To

The trade show industry lost an estimated $30 billion in direct revenue between March 2020 and mid-2021. Convention centers went dark. Exhibitors canceled contracts. Event staff were laid off by the tens of thousands. The recovery took years, and some shows never came back at all.

The industry does not want another shutdown. No one does. But the path to preventing one is not pretending the threat does not exist. It is acting proactively so that public health authorities never have to step in and make the decision for you.

Consider the scenario: A measles cluster is traced back to a major trade show. Dozens of attendees across multiple states test positive in the weeks following the event. Media coverage is intense and unflattering. The local health department launches an investigation. The next edition of that show sees a 30-40% drop in registration because corporate risk managers refuse to send employees. Exhibitors demand registration fee refunds, citing unsafe conditions. The show's insurance premiums double.

This is not a hypothetical. It is a foreseeable sequence of events that any event planner, show organizer, or convention center operator should be gaming out right now.

What Convention Centers Are Already Doing

The good news is that many convention centers made significant infrastructure investments during and after the COVID-19 pandemic that are now proving valuable in a different context. HVAC systems were upgraded across hundreds of major venues. MERV-13 and higher-grade air filters — which can capture virus-carrying aerosols — became standard in many facilities. Air exchange rates were increased. Some venues installed UV-C germicidal systems in their air handling units.

These upgrades were designed for COVID-era concerns, but they apply directly to measles and any other airborne pathogen. A convention center with high air exchange rates, proper filtration, and UV disinfection is meaningfully safer than one running legacy HVAC systems. Exhibitors and organizers should be asking their venues about these capabilities and factoring them into site selection decisions.

Beyond air quality, some convention centers are beginning to implement health screening protocols. While full-scale temperature checks and proof-of-vaccination requirements are not yet standard for trade shows — unlike the mandates that existed during COVID — several major venues have quietly updated their event planning guidelines to include health advisory templates, enhanced cleaning protocols for high-touch surfaces, and coordination plans with local health departments.

What Show Organizers Should Do Now

1. Add Health Advisories to Registration

Every show registration page should include a measles health advisory, effective immediately. This advisory should state the current CDC guidance, recommend that attendees verify their MMR vaccination status before traveling, and provide links to local vaccination providers. This is not just good public health practice. It is a liability shield. Organizers who can demonstrate that they proactively informed attendees about the risk are in a far stronger legal position than those who said nothing.

2. Coordinate with Local Health Departments

Before every major show, organizers should establish a direct line of communication with the local health department in the host city. What is the current case count in the area? Are there active outbreaks within the state? What is the department's protocol if a confirmed case is linked to the event? Having these answers in advance — rather than scrambling to find them after an incident — is the difference between a managed situation and a crisis.

3. Review Insurance Coverage

The insurance landscape for live events shifted dramatically after COVID. Many policies now include communicable disease exclusions or limitations. Organizers should review their event liability policies immediately to understand what is and is not covered in the event of an outbreak linked to their show. If your policy has gaps, the time to address them is before the show, not after.

4. Prepare a Communications Plan

If a measles case is confirmed at your show, you need a pre-drafted communications plan that can be activated within hours. Who issues the statement? What channels does it go through? How do you notify attendees? What do you tell exhibitors? What do you tell the press? Improvising this under media scrutiny is a recipe for reputational damage.

What Exhibitors Should Do at Their Booths

1. Stock Up on Hygiene Supplies

Hand sanitizer should be visible and accessible at every booth. Sanitizing wipes should be available for demo equipment, shared tablets, and touch screens. This is not theater. Measles spreads primarily through airborne droplets, but contaminated surfaces can also play a role. And visible hygiene measures signal to attendees and staff that your company takes health seriously.

2. Implement a No-Questions-Asked Sick Day Policy

If a member of your booth team develops symptoms — fever, cough, runny nose, red eyes, or a rash — they should not be on the show floor. Period. The trade show culture of "pushing through" illness is a liability when measles is circulating. Exhibitors should make it explicitly clear to their teams before the show that calling in sick will not be penalized, and that backup coverage plans are in place.

3. Verify Your Team's Vaccination Status

Before sending staff to a trade show in 2026, companies should verify that their team members have documentation of MMR vaccination or evidence of immunity. This is not a political statement. It is an occupational health measure, no different from requiring steel-toed boots on a construction site. Most adults born after 1957 who received two doses of the MMR vaccine are protected, but many may have incomplete records or may have received only one dose.

4. Rethink High-Contact Booth Activities

Shared VR headsets, interactive touch screens used by hundreds of people per day, candy bowls, and communal food stations all present elevated risk during an outbreak period. Consider whether these elements are essential to your booth strategy, and if so, implement cleaning protocols between uses. The Insurance and Liability Dimension

The legal landscape around infectious disease at events has been reshaped by COVID-era litigation. Several key principles are emerging that trade show professionals should understand.

First, duty of care. Event organizers and venue operators have a legal duty to provide a reasonably safe environment. If a measles outbreak is actively occurring in the host city and the organizer takes no precautionary measures, they may face claims of negligence. The standard is not perfection. It is reasonableness. Did you take steps that a prudent organizer would take given the known risk?

Second, assumption of risk. Health advisories and informed consent language in registration agreements can help establish that attendees were aware of and accepted a degree of risk. But this defense has limits, particularly if the organizer failed to implement basic precautions.

Third, employer liability. Companies that send employees to trade shows have a duty under OSHA's general duty clause to provide a workplace free from recognized hazards. An employer that sends an unvaccinated employee to a trade show in an area with an active measles outbreak, without providing information about the risk or offering vaccination, could face regulatory scrutiny.

Key Takeaway The 2026 measles outbreak is not a distant public health statistic. It is an active, escalating threat to every large indoor gathering in the United States, and trade shows are among the most exposed. South Carolina's 933-case outbreak, the nationwide spread across 20 jurisdictions, and the fact that 92% of cases are outbreak-associated all point to a virus that is finding concentrated, unvaccinated populations and spreading rapidly. Show organizers need health advisories on registration pages now. Exhibitors need sick-day policies and booth hygiene protocols. Convention centers need to verify that their post-COVID air filtration upgrades are operational. The industry survived COVID, but it paid a $30 billion price in the process. The cost of proactive action on measles is a fraction of that. The cost of doing nothing could be another shutdown — or worse, being the venue that launches the next national outbreak.
