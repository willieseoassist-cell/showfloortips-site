99% of New US Power Capacity in 2026 Will Be Solar, Wind, and Battery Storage -- What It Means for Energy Trade Shows

The EIA's latest data confirms the most lopsided capacity buildout in American energy history. With 69,579 MW of new renewable and battery storage capacity projected for 2026, the implications for exhibitors at RE+, Intersolar, POWERGEN, and every energy trade show are enormous -- and immediate.

Photo via Unsplash

The US Energy Information Administration has published data that should stop every energy trade show exhibitor in their tracks: 99 percent of net new US generating capacity added in 2026 will come from solar, wind, and battery storage. Not 60 percent. Not 80 percent. Ninety-nine percent. The remaining sliver -- just 3,960.7 MW of natural gas -- is almost entirely offset by 3,387 MW of coal plant retirements happening simultaneously. The fossil fuel era is not ending in some distant future scenario. It is being numerically overwhelmed right now, in real-time capacity additions, by renewables and storage technologies that are scaling faster than almost anyone predicted five years ago.

For the thousands of companies that exhibit at energy trade shows each year -- from massive utility-scale solar manufacturers to small battery management system startups -- these numbers represent a market opportunity unlike anything the industry has seen. The show floors at RE+, Intersolar North America, Solar Power International, WindEnergy Hamburg, POWERGEN, and DistribuTECH are about to become the most consequential selling environments in the energy sector. If your company makes, installs, finances, or services renewable energy or storage equipment, the demand trajectory is no longer a question. The only question is whether your trade show strategy is keeping pace with the market you are trying to serve.

The Numbers Behind the Headline

The EIA's monthly generation and capacity data, drawn from plant-level reporting through November 2025, paints a picture of acceleration rather than gradual transition. Utility-scale solar generation grew 33.9 percent year-over-year through November, a growth rate that would be remarkable in any industry but is especially striking in power generation, where infrastructure projects typically take years to plan and build. Battery storage capacity surged 49.4 percent, with operators adding 13,357 MW of new storage capacity in the first eleven months of 2025 alone. These are not pilot projects or demonstration facilities. These are grid-scale installations delivering power to millions of households and businesses.

The 2026 projections are even more dramatic. The EIA forecasts 69,579 MW of new renewable and battery storage capacity coming online this year, dwarfing the 3,960.7 MW of natural gas additions that represent virtually all new fossil fuel capacity. When you subtract the 3,387 MW of coal retirements scheduled for the same period, the net addition of fossil fuel capacity becomes negligible -- a rounding error in the context of a grid that is being fundamentally reshaped by clean energy technology.

"By November 2026, total US renewable generating capacity is projected to reach approximately 508,700 MW -- within striking distance of natural gas at 514,484 MW. The grid parity moment that was once a decade-away target is now months away."

That convergence point deserves emphasis. Renewables reaching near-parity with natural gas in installed capacity -- roughly 508,700 MW versus 514,484 MW -- would have been considered an aggressive fantasy a decade ago. Today it is a statistical inevitability based on projects already under construction or in final permitting. Renewables now account for more than 36 percent of total US generating capacity, and they provided 26 percent of the nation's electricity in 2025. The gap between capacity share and generation share reflects the intermittent nature of solar and wind, but that gap is precisely what is driving the explosive growth in battery storage -- the technology that converts intermittent generation into dispatchable power.

Why This Reshapes Every Energy Trade Show in 2026

Trade shows are mirrors of their industries. When an industry is growing at 34 percent and 49 percent annual rates across its two fastest-moving segments, the trade shows that serve that industry become pressure cookers of demand, competition, and urgency. That is exactly what is happening across the energy trade show circuit right now.

Booth Demand Is Surging

The math is straightforward. When 69,579 MW of new capacity needs to be manufactured, shipped, installed, interconnected, and maintained in a single year, the number of companies competing for contracts in that supply chain expands rapidly. Solar panel manufacturers, inverter companies, tracker system providers, battery cell producers, energy management software developers, EPC contractors, grid interconnection specialists, permitting consultants, and financing firms all need visibility with the same universe of utility buyers and project developers. The show floor is where those connections happen at scale, and the demand for booth space at the major energy shows is reflecting the industry's growth trajectory.

RE+, the largest clean energy trade show in North America, takes place November 16-19, 2026, in Las Vegas. With over 39,000 attendees and 1,350 exhibitors expected, it is the single most important selling environment for US solar, storage, and wind companies. But RE+ is not the only show feeling the surge. Intersolar North America, Solar Power International (co-located with RE+), POWERGEN International, and DistribuTECH are all experiencing increased exhibitor interest as the renewable buildout accelerates.

Solar Exhibitors: The 33.9% Growth Rate Changes Everything

Utility-scale solar is not just growing. It is growing at a rate that is straining supply chains, overwhelming permitting offices, and creating fierce competition among manufacturers for market share. The 33.9 percent year-over-year increase in generation reflects a buildout pace that demands scale -- and scale is what trade shows are designed to facilitate.

For solar exhibitors, the trade show floor in 2026 is not about educating the market. Utilities and developers know solar works. They know the economics. What they need now are answers to execution questions: which manufacturers can deliver panels on time and at the volume required for a 500 MW project? Which tracker systems perform best in high-wind regions? Which inverter platforms integrate most seamlessly with battery storage? Which EPC firms have the workforce to handle three simultaneous utility-scale installations?

The exhibitors who win at RE+ and Intersolar this year will be the ones who show up with answers to those questions -- backed by project references, delivery timelines, and bankability documentation that procurement teams can take directly into their internal approval processes. Concept-stage products and prototype demonstrations will still draw visitors, but the buyers with the largest purchase orders are shopping for proven, deployable technology that can move from contract to commercial operation in the shortest possible timeline.

Battery Storage: The 49.4% Surge Creates a New Trade Show Category

Battery storage grew 49.4 percent in capacity during the first eleven months of 2025, adding 13,357 MW of new storage to the US grid. That growth rate is transforming battery storage from a niche segment within energy trade shows into a dominant exhibition category in its own right. Five years ago, battery storage exhibitors at RE+ occupied a modest section of the show floor. Today, storage companies -- from lithium-ion cell manufacturers to flow battery startups to energy management software platforms -- command some of the largest and most prominent booth positions at every major energy show.

The reason is structural. As solar and wind capacity grows, the grid's need for storage grows proportionally. Every megawatt of intermittent generation added to the grid increases the value of every megawatt of dispatchable storage. The EIA data confirms that utilities and grid operators understand this relationship and are investing accordingly. For battery storage exhibitors, the message from the market is unambiguous: build more, deploy faster, and make sure the buyers at every major energy trade show know exactly what you can deliver.

"Battery storage capacity grew 49.4% in just eleven months, adding 13,357 MW to the US grid. Five years ago, this would have been the total installed storage capacity of most countries. Now it is a single year's additions in a single market."

The Natural Gas Footnote -- And What It Means for Fossil Fuel Exhibitors

Natural gas is adding just 3,960.7 MW of new capacity in 2026. For context, that is roughly 5.7 percent of what solar, wind, and battery storage are adding in the same period. When you net out the 3,387 MW of coal retirements, the fossil fuel sector's contribution to new grid capacity is functionally zero. This does not mean natural gas is disappearing from the grid -- it remains the single largest source of installed capacity at 514,484 MW -- but it does mean the growth story belongs entirely to renewables and storage.

For exhibitors who sell gas turbines, fossil fuel plant equipment, or coal-related services, the trade show implications are significant. Booth traffic from buyers evaluating new-build projects will increasingly skew toward renewable and storage technologies. The smart play for fossil fuel exhibitors is to pivot their messaging toward grid reliability, peaker plant modernization, hydrogen-ready turbine platforms, and the hybrid gas-plus-storage configurations that some utilities are deploying as transitional solutions. Companies that position themselves at the intersection of legacy thermal assets and emerging clean energy requirements will find a receptive audience. Companies that ignore the EIA data and continue marketing as though new gas plants are the primary growth opportunity will find their booths quieter than expected.

The Approaching Parity: 508,700 MW vs. 514,484 MW

Perhaps the most striking number in the EIA data is the projected convergence: by November 2026, total US renewable generating capacity is expected to reach approximately 508,700 MW, compared to natural gas at 514,484 MW. The gap is less than 6,000 MW -- a margin that could be erased in a single quarter at current installation rates. When renewable capacity surpasses natural gas capacity -- a milestone that now appears likely before the end of 2027 at the latest -- it will mark the most significant structural shift in the US power sector since the retirement of the coal fleet began in earnest a decade ago.

For trade show exhibitors, this approaching parity is more than a statistical milestone. It is a narrative inflection point. Renewables are no longer the "alternative" energy source. They are becoming the primary energy source by installed capacity, and the trade shows that serve the renewable sector will increasingly function as the main stage of the US energy industry rather than a specialized sub-sector event. Exhibitors at RE+ 2026 will not be presenting at a renewable energy trade show. They will be presenting at an energy trade show -- period.

Exhibitor Strategy: How to Capitalize on the Renewable Surge

The EIA data does not just describe a market. It describes a market that is moving faster than most exhibitors' trade show strategies. Here is how to align your exhibition presence with the reality of 99 percent renewable capacity additions:

  1. Book booth space now, not later. Exhibit space at RE+, Intersolar, and POWERGEN is being claimed earlier each year as the industry grows. Waiting until summer to secure your booth position means accepting whatever is left, likely in a low-traffic area far from the anchor exhibitors where the buyers spend their time. The exhibitors who generate the highest ROI are the ones who secured prime positioning months before the show.
  2. Lead with deployment data, not product specs. In a market adding 69,579 MW of new capacity in a single year, buyers are not comparison-shopping on spec sheets. They are evaluating which vendors can deliver at scale, on time, and with the bankability that lenders and investors require. Your booth messaging should center on megawatts deployed, projects completed, and delivery timelines met -- not laboratory performance data that has not been validated in the field.
  3. Prepare for storage conversations at every booth. Even if your company does not sell battery storage, your buyers are thinking about it. Solar exhibitors should be ready to discuss DC-coupled storage integration. Wind exhibitors should understand how storage changes the curtailment calculus. Grid equipment manufacturers should be prepared to explain how their products perform in high-storage-penetration environments. The 49.4 percent growth rate means storage is no longer a separate conversation -- it is embedded in every conversation.
  4. Invest in lead capture that matches the opportunity. With thousands of qualified buyers walking the show floor at RE+ alone, the difference between a successful show and a wasted investment often comes down to how efficiently you capture and process leads. Use Scannly to digitize every badge scan and conversation in real time, so your sales team can begin follow-up within hours of the show closing rather than spending weeks sorting through business cards and scribbled notes.
  5. Target international shows for global positioning. The US renewable buildout is the largest in the world, but it is not the only one. WindEnergy Hamburg provides access to the European offshore wind market. Intersolar Europe reaches solar buyers across the EU. Companies with global ambitions should build a multi-show strategy that covers both domestic and international markets, using each show's unique audience to expand their geographic footprint.

The Bottom Line for Energy Trade Show Exhibitors

The EIA's data is not a forecast or a projection of what might happen under favorable conditions. It is a description of what is already happening, based on plants under construction, projects in final permitting, and capacity additions that are contractually committed. Ninety-nine percent of net new US power capacity in 2026 will be solar, wind, and battery storage. The exhibitors who build their trade show strategy around this reality -- with the right booth position, the right messaging, and the right lead capture technology -- will be the ones who convert the largest energy transition in American history into revenue. The ones who do not will watch their competitors do it instead.
