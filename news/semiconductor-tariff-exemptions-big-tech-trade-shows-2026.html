Semiconductor Tariff Exemptions for Big Tech and TSMC's $165 Billion Expansion — What Exhibitors Need for SEMICON West, Computex, and embedded world 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

The semiconductor industry is entering 2026 under a policy regime that would have been unimaginable five years ago. The White House is actively considering tariff exemptions for Amazon, Google, and Microsoft on chips manufactured by TSMC — the Taiwanese foundry that produces the most advanced processors on Earth. Simultaneously, the administration is preparing broad semiconductor tariffs that will affect the entire chip supply chain. And at the center of this policy tension sits TSMC's jaw-dropping pledge to invest $165 billion in U.S. manufacturing expansion, the largest foreign direct investment in American semiconductor history.

The proposed exemptions would allow Big Tech companies to import up to 2.5 times their planned production capacity from TSMC's overseas fabs tariff-free during the multi-year buildout of TSMC's Arizona facilities. The logic is pragmatic: these companies need advanced chips now to fuel their AI infrastructure buildouts, and domestic production capacity will not be ready for years. But the carve-outs create a two-tier system that will reshape competitive dynamics across the semiconductor ecosystem — dynamics that will play out in full view at SEMICON West in July, Computex Taipei in June, and embedded world in March.

The Tariff Landscape: What Is Actually Happening

Understanding the semiconductor tariff landscape requires separating the policy strands that are converging simultaneously. The administration has signaled its intent to impose broad tariffs on semiconductor imports as part of its industrial strategy to accelerate domestic chip production. These tariffs, which could range from 10 to 25 percent depending on the chip category and country of origin, are designed to create economic incentives for chipmakers to manufacture in the United States rather than in Taiwan, South Korea, or China.

However, broad chip tariffs create an immediate problem: the hyperscale cloud providers and AI companies that are the largest consumers of advanced semiconductors cannot wait for U.S. fabs to come online. Microsoft, Amazon, and Google are each spending tens of billions of dollars annually on AI infrastructure, and that infrastructure requires chips that currently can only be manufactured at scale in TSMC's Taiwan facilities. Imposing tariffs on those chips would effectively tax American AI competitiveness at precisely the moment the administration is trying to accelerate it.

The proposed solution is surgical: give the biggest buyers tariff exemptions during the transition period while TSMC's Arizona fabs ramp to production. The exemptions would reportedly allow these companies to import up to 2.5 times their planned capacity from TSMC's overseas fabs without paying the new tariffs. Once domestic production reaches sufficient scale, the exemptions would phase out.

Winners and Losers in the Two-Tier System

The tariff exemptions create clear winners and potential losers. Big Tech companies with the scale and government relationships to secure exemptions get a cost advantage over smaller competitors who must either pay full tariffs or source from less advanced domestic fabs. Chip designers who rely on TSMC's most advanced process nodes — particularly AI chip companies — face a more complex decision matrix about where and how to manufacture.

  • Winners: Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and other hyperscalers with direct TSMC manufacturing agreements and the political leverage to secure exemptions. TSMC itself benefits from the certainty that its largest customers will continue purchasing during the transition.
  • Potential losers: Mid-tier cloud providers, smaller AI startups, and electronics manufacturers who lack the scale to negotiate exemptions and face tariff-inflated chip costs. Companies relying on chips from Samsung, Intel Foundry, or Chinese fabs face different but equally complex tariff scenarios.
  • Uncertain: Equipment manufacturers and materials suppliers whose business depends on where fabs are built and how quickly they ramp. A faster U.S. buildout benefits domestic equipment suppliers; a slower one benefits those with existing Asian supply chain positions.
Key TakeawayThe semiconductor tariff exemptions create a two-speed market where the biggest buyers get preferential access to the most advanced chips at pre-tariff prices, while the rest of the industry absorbs new costs or scrambles for alternatives. For exhibitors at semiconductor trade shows, the strategic question is clear: which side of that divide are your customers on, and how does your product help them navigate it?

TSMC's $165 Billion Bet on America

TSMC's $165 billion U.S. expansion commitment is the cornerstone of the entire tariff-and-exemption framework. The investment, which includes multiple fabs in Arizona manufacturing chips at the 3-nanometer and 2-nanometer process nodes, represents a fundamental shift in global semiconductor geography. For decades, the most advanced chip manufacturing has been concentrated almost exclusively in Taiwan and South Korea. TSMC's Arizona expansion begins the process of redistributing that capacity to the United States.

The scale of the investment is staggering. At $165 billion, TSMC's U.S. commitment exceeds the GDP of most countries. It will create thousands of direct manufacturing jobs and tens of thousands of supply chain and support positions. It will require new housing, new roads, new water infrastructure, and new power generation capacity in the Phoenix metropolitan area. And it will create an enormous addressable market for semiconductor equipment manufacturers, materials suppliers, construction companies, and workforce training providers.

What This Means for Trade Show Exhibitors

The convergence of tariff policy, exemption negotiations, and massive domestic manufacturing investment creates a uniquely dynamic environment for semiconductor trade show exhibitors in 2026. Every conversation on every show floor will be colored by these policy shifts, and exhibitors who understand the implications will have dramatically more productive shows than those who try to sell around the policy uncertainty.

embedded world — Nuremberg, March 10–12

embedded world is the premier European event for embedded systems, IoT, and edge computing. For semiconductor exhibitors, embedded world in 2026 is where the tariff discussion meets the European market. EU policymakers are watching U.S. semiconductor tariffs closely and considering their own industrial policy responses through the European Chips Act. Exhibitors who can position their products within the context of both U.S. and European semiconductor policy will find a deeply engaged audience. Embedded processors, microcontrollers, and specialty chips that may not require the most advanced process nodes are particularly relevant here, as they may be less affected by TSMC-centric tariff dynamics.

Computex Taipei — June 2026

Computex is ground zero for the Asian semiconductor ecosystem. In 2026, it will be the show where TSMC's customers, partners, and competitors gather to discuss how the $165 billion U.S. expansion and associated tariff policies reshape supply chains. For exhibitors, Computex offers direct access to the Taiwanese and broader Asian supply chain — the foundries, OSATs (outsourced semiconductor assembly and test providers), substrate manufacturers, and equipment companies that form the backbone of global chip production. The policy uncertainty around tariffs makes face-to-face relationship building at Computex more valuable than ever.

SEMICON West — San Francisco, July 2026

SEMICON West is the semiconductor equipment and materials industry's flagship North American event. In 2026, it will be dominated by TSMC's Arizona buildout and the equipment and materials requirements it creates. Every major equipment manufacturer — ASML, Applied Materials, Lam Research, Tokyo Electron, KLA — is scaling production to supply the new Arizona fabs, and their customers, partners, and competitors will all be at SEMICON West evaluating the competitive landscape. For exhibitors in semiconductor equipment, process chemicals, advanced materials, and fab construction, SEMICON West 2026 is the single most important event on the calendar.

"The semiconductor tariff exemptions are not just trade policy — they are industrial policy that will determine which companies have access to the most advanced chips at competitive prices for the next five to seven years. For any company whose products contain semiconductors, understanding these dynamics is now a strategic priority." — Semiconductor industry policy analyst, February 2026

Exhibitor Strategies for Semiconductor Shows in 2026

  1. Know your customer's tariff exposure. Before you set foot on a show floor, understand how the tariff exemptions and broader chip tariffs affect your target customers. A hyperscaler with TSMC exemptions faces a completely different cost structure than a mid-tier electronics manufacturer paying full tariffs. Tailor your value proposition accordingly — cost optimization messaging resonates differently with each audience.
  2. Position for the domestic buildout. TSMC's $165 billion Arizona expansion is creating a massive domestic market for semiconductor equipment, materials, construction services, and workforce development. If your products serve any of these categories, your SEMICON West booth should be explicitly framed around the Arizona buildout opportunity. Bring specifics: capacity, delivery timelines, Arizona-specific logistics capabilities, and references from existing fab construction projects.
  3. Address supply chain diversification. The tariff environment is driving every semiconductor buyer to diversify their supply chain. Companies that previously sourced exclusively from one foundry or one region are now evaluating alternatives. If your product enables supply chain flexibility — whether through multi-foundry design tools, alternative materials, or region-agnostic testing and packaging — that is a message with strong 2026 resonance.
  4. Prepare for policy conversations. Your booth staff will be asked about tariff impacts whether you prepare them or not. Brief your team on the current state of tariff policy, exemption eligibility criteria, and how your products are affected. Having a knowledgeable, confident response to tariff questions builds credibility; fumbling through policy discussions undermines it.

The density of qualified buyers is extraordinary, and missing even a single high-value contact can mean missing a significant opportunity. Exhibitor InsightThe semiconductor tariff landscape in 2026 is not a background condition — it is the central strategic issue affecting every buyer and seller in the industry. Exhibitors who treat tariff policy as someone else's problem will find their booth conversations awkward and their lead quality poor. Those who embrace the policy complexity and demonstrate how their products help customers navigate it will have the most productive shows of the year.

The Long Game: Reshoring as a Decade-Long Opportunity

TSMC's $165 billion commitment is not a one-time event. It is the beginning of a multi-decade reshoring of advanced semiconductor manufacturing to the United States. Intel is investing tens of billions in domestic fab expansion. Samsung is building advanced facilities in Texas. GlobalFoundries is expanding in New York and Vermont. The CHIPS and Science Act continues to provide federal incentives for domestic semiconductor investment. And the tariff framework being constructed now will further accelerate the economic case for domestic manufacturing.

For trade show exhibitors, this means the semiconductor show circuit is entering a period of sustained relevance and growth. The equipment orders, materials contracts, and service agreements generated by domestic fab construction will fuel exhibitor pipelines for years. Companies that establish strong show floor presences now — building brand recognition, technical credibility, and customer relationships — will be better positioned to capture their share of the reshoring investment than those who wait until domestic production is fully ramped.

The semiconductor industry's 2026 trade show calendar — embedded world in March, Computex in June, and SEMICON West in July — arrives at a moment when tariff policy, massive capital investment, and geopolitical strategy are converging to reshape the industry's competitive landscape. For exhibitors who understand these dynamics and can articulate how their products fit into the new reality, these shows offer access to the most consequential buyer audience in the global technology supply chain.
