Anthropic's AI Just Wiped $285 Billion Off Software Stocks. Exhibitors, Take Note.

On a single Tuesday in early February, a product launch from a San Francisco AI lab erased $285 billion from the global software sector. Thomson Reuters dropped 15.83%, its worst day on record. LegalZoom cratered 19.68%. RELX and FactSet suffered double-digit declines. An ETF tracking the broader software industry posted its worst session since April. The catalyst was not a recession, a regulatory crackdown, or a fraud scandal. It was a new feature called Claude Cowork.

Anthropic, the AI company behind the Claude family of models, had released a suite of workplace automation plugins designed for sales, finance, legal, data, and marketing professionals. Available to subscribers of its $100-per-month premium tier, Claude Cowork can author documents, build slide decks, produce reports, search and organize files, and pull data from tools like Zendesk and Microsoft Teams. In other words, it can do what entire categories of enterprise software were built to do, but without requiring a separate subscription for each task.

For trade show exhibitors, especially the thousands of SaaS companies that fill the floors of events like SaaStr Annual, Dreamforce, Web Summit, and HubSpot INBOUND, this is not a distant theoretical threat. It is a present and urgent strategic challenge.

Why This Selloff Matters for Exhibitors

The stock market is a forward-looking machine. When investors dumped nearly $1 trillion in software and services stocks over the course of the week, they were not reacting to lost revenue today. They were repricing the future. The implicit bet: AI-native tools will absorb market share from standalone SaaS products faster than most analysts had predicted.

That repricing has direct, tangible consequences for the trade show floor. Software companies whose stock prices fell 10-20% in a week will face immediate pressure on discretionary budgets. Trade show participation is often one of the first line items scrutinized when CFOs tighten spending. Expect to see smaller booth sizes, fewer sponsored activations, and more last-minute cancellations from companies in the legal tech, financial data, and enterprise analytics categories that took the hardest hits.

But the disruption runs deeper than budget cuts. The Anthropic selloff is accelerating a conversation that every SaaS exhibitor must now have with their booth visitors: "Why should I pay for your tool when an AI agent can do this?"

The SaaS Exhibitor's Identity Crisis

For the better part of two decades, SaaS companies have followed a reliable playbook at trade shows. Set up a booth with monitors showing the product interface. Staff it with sales development reps who demo the workflow. Hand out swag. Scan badges. Follow up with a sequence of emails offering a free trial. The value proposition was straightforward: our software solves this specific problem better than the alternative.

Claude Cowork, and the wave of AI workplace agents that will inevitably follow, threatens that proposition at its core. If a general-purpose AI can produce reports, organize data, generate slide decks, and integrate with existing business tools, the "specific problem" a standalone SaaS product solves may no longer require a standalone product.

This does not mean every SaaS company is doomed. It means the ones that survive on the show floor will be those that can articulate a new kind of value: depth that generalist AI cannot match.

"The SaaS companies that will win are not the ones that do tasks. They are the ones that hold proprietary data, enforce regulatory compliance, and embed into workflows so deeply that ripping them out would be more expensive than any AI subscription." — Industry analyst consensus emerging from the selloff week

What Strong Exhibitors Are Doing Differently

The smartest SaaS exhibitors are already pivoting their booth narratives. Here is what separates the companies that will grow through this disruption from those that will shrink:

  • Leading with data moats. Companies like Bloomberg Terminal exhibitors and specialized compliance platforms are emphasizing their proprietary data assets, the information AI cannot access or replicate because it lives behind contractual, regulatory, or technical barriers.
  • Demonstrating AI integration, not competition. The winning move is to show how your product works with AI, not in spite of it. Exhibitors at recent tech conferences have started running live demos where an AI agent uses their product's API, proving that AI makes their tool more valuable rather than redundant.
  • Shifting from feature demos to outcome stories. Nobody walking the show floor in 2026 is impressed by a product demo showing that your tool can "generate reports." They have seen an AI do that for free. The exhibitors gaining traction are telling stories about outcomes: risk mitigated, revenue captured, compliance achieved, and the specific disasters their customers avoided.

The Broader Exhibitor Ecosystem Feels the Tremor

The Anthropic-triggered selloff is not just a SaaS exhibitor problem. It ripples across the entire trade show ecosystem.

Show Organizers

Events heavily weighted toward software categories face exhibit-space risk. If 30% of your floor is populated by companies whose stock just fell 15%, some of those booths will not be renewed. Organizers should be proactively reaching out to exhibitors in affected categories to offer flexible terms, package incentives, and co-marketing support that makes staying on the floor an easier financial decision.

Booth Builders and Service Providers

Custom exhibit houses that count SaaS companies as a large share of their client base should diversify their pipelines now. The AI infrastructure companies ramping spend (covered in our separate analysis of the $650 billion capex wave) represent a growing pool of clients that need impressive booth builds. Pivot marketing efforts accordingly.

Non-Software Exhibitors

If you are a hardware manufacturer, a logistics company, or a professional services firm exhibiting at a tech-adjacent show, the software selloff is actually an opportunity. Attendees at these events are hungry for conversations about tangible products and services that AI cannot fully automate. Physical goods, complex consulting engagements, and relationship-driven services are experiencing a perception upgrade precisely because software is being commoditized. Lean into that positioning at your next show.

Five Tactical Moves for Exhibitors in the Post-Cowork Era

  1. Rewrite your booth's opening question. Ditch "What challenges are you facing with [category]?" and replace it with "How are you navigating AI disruption in [category]?" This immediately positions your team as consultants, not vendors, and opens a conversation your prospects are desperate to have.
  2. Build a live AI comparison demo. Set up a booth station where attendees can watch an AI agent attempt the task your product handles, then see your product handle the same task. Highlight the gaps: accuracy in edge cases, compliance requirements, integration depth, audit trails. Let the comparison speak for itself.
  3. Create an AI-readiness assessment. Offer a quick diagnostic tool, a tablet-based questionnaire or interactive kiosk, that evaluates how prepared a prospect's organization is for AI disruption. This generates high-quality leads from people who are thinking seriously about the exact problem you solve, and it positions your company as a thought leader.
  4. Arm your booth staff with the selloff narrative. Your sales reps need to speak fluently about the Anthropic news and what it means for your product category. Brief them on the specific stocks that fell, the reasons why, and your company's counter-narrative. Prospects will respect preparedness and punish ignorance.
  5. Negotiate your next booth contract now. If you are exhibiting at a software-heavy show later this year, the organizer is worried about floor occupancy. This is the moment to negotiate favorable terms: better placement, reduced rates, bundled marketing packages. Your leverage is real and time-limited.

The Long View

The $285 billion selloff was a single day. The structural shift it signals will play out over years. AI workplace agents will get better. More software categories will feel the pressure. But trade shows will remain the place where companies differentiate themselves from commoditized alternatives, precisely because the show floor is where you prove that your product, your team, and your expertise are worth more than a general-purpose AI subscription.

The exhibitors who internalize this lesson now will not just survive the AI disruption wave. They will use it to win larger deals, attract better prospects, and build the kind of trust that no chatbot, however capable, can replicate. The show floor has always been where real business happens. In the age of AI, that truth is more valuable than ever.
