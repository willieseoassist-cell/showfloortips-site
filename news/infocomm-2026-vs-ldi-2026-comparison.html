InfoComm 2026 vs LDI 2026: Pro AV Enterprise Power Meets Live Entertainment Innovation — Which Show Fits Your Career?

The professional audiovisual and live entertainment industries revolve around two flagship tradeshows held each year in Las Vegas: InfoComm, organized by AVIXA, and LDI (Live Design International), produced by Questex. Both events draw thousands of professionals who design, install, operate, and manage technology-driven experiences. Yet the two shows serve distinct audiences, emphasize different technology verticals, and offer fundamentally different networking environments. Understanding those differences is critical before you commit time, travel budget, and professional development hours to either—or both—events in 2026.

This in-depth comparison examines every angle: dates, venue logistics, exhibitor scope, educational tracks, registration pricing, target audiences, career-building opportunities, and the practical travel considerations that ultimately determine your return on investment. Whether you are a systems integrator evaluating enterprise collaboration platforms or a lighting designer hunting for the next generation of moving fixtures, this guide will help you decide which show floor deserves your attention this year.

Show Overview and Heritage

InfoComm 2026

InfoComm is the world's most vital professional audiovisual event, attracting over 40,000 attendees every June. Run by AVIXA (the Audiovisual and Integrated Experience Association), InfoComm has grown from a niche projection and sound convention into the defining marketplace for the entire Pro AV ecosystem. The 2026 edition spans June 13 through 19, with the full conference program beginning on June 13 and the exhibition hall open June 17 through 19 at the Las Vegas Convention Center.

With more than 1,000 exhibitors filling hundreds of thousands of square feet, InfoComm is where category-leading brands launch new product lines, where systems integrators source their next-generation solutions, and where enterprise technology managers evaluate everything from LED video walls and digital signage networks to unified communications platforms and immersive XR environments. The show covers the full spectrum of professional AV: audio, video, lighting, projection, digital signage, unified communications, collaboration, streaming, and extended reality.

LDI 2026 (Live Design International)

LDI has been the annual gathering point for live entertainment technology professionals since 1988. Organized by Questex, the 2026 edition is scheduled for December 2 through 8 at the Las Vegas Convention Center, West Hall. LDI is smaller in raw exhibitor count—350+ exhibitors—but its concentration of specialized brands makes the show floor remarkably dense with innovation for anyone working in live events.

LDI focuses on the creative and technical disciplines behind live entertainment: concert touring, theater, themed entertainment, houses of worship, corporate events, broadcast production, and experiential activations. The exhibitor mix centers on audio, lighting, projection, video, staging, special effects, rigging, and XR technologies purpose-built for performance environments. The audience is a community of lighting designers, sound technicians, stage designers, production managers, and creative directors who value hands-on experience over boardroom presentations.

Exhibition Floor and Technology Focus

InfoComm: The Full AV Ecosystem

InfoComm's exhibition hall is a sprawling landscape where nearly every category of professional AV technology is represented. Walking the floor, you will encounter massive LED video walls from manufacturers competing for pixel pitch supremacy, next-generation projection systems from Christie, Epson, and Panasonic, and digital signage platforms from BrightSign, Samsung, and LG designed for corporate lobbies, retail environments, and transportation hubs. Unified communications and collaboration technology has become one of the fastest-growing segments, with Crestron, Poly, Logitech, Biamp, and QSC demonstrating room solutions that integrate seamlessly with Microsoft Teams and Zoom Rooms.

The audio segment alone spans hundreds of booths, from networked audio platforms running Dante and AES67 to commercial loudspeaker arrays from JBL Professional, Bose, and Shure. Streaming and content distribution technology features prominently, reflecting the post-pandemic shift toward hybrid meeting environments. XR (extended reality) demonstrations showcase virtual production stages and immersive experiences that blur the line between physical and digital environments.

LDI: Precision Tools for Live Performance

LDI's show floor is smaller but surgically focused. Every exhibitor caters to the demands of live performance: moving lights, LED fixtures, haze and fog machines, rigging hardware, trussing systems, staging decks, pyrotechnic controllers, and audio consoles built for front-of-house and monitor engineering. Brands like ETC, Martin by Harman, Robe, Clay Paky, and GLP occupy prominent positions, often building elaborate demo environments where visitors can see fixtures perform under show-realistic conditions.

Audio exhibitors at LDI lean toward touring and installation sound: line arrays from L-Acoustics, d&b audiotechnik, and Meyer Sound share floor space with console manufacturers like DiGiCo, Yamaha, Allen & Heath, and SSL. Special effects companies demonstrate everything from confetti cannons and laser systems to projection-mapped scenic elements. The staging and rigging section draws technical directors who need to evaluate load-rated hardware, chain hoists, and fall-arrest systems. Extended reality and virtual production have also carved a growing niche at LDI, reflecting the live entertainment industry's adoption of real-time rendering and LED volume stages.

Education and Professional Development

InfoComm's Structured Curriculum

AVIXA has built InfoComm's education program into one of the most robust professional development platforms in the AV industry. The 2026 conference includes multi-day courses and workshops starting four days before the exhibition opens. Highlights include:

  • CTS Certification Prep: Intensive review sessions for the Certified Technology Specialist exam, along with CTS-D (design) and CTS-I (installation) specialty tracks. AVIXA offers on-site testing so attendees can complete their certification during the week.
  • AI Accelerator: A dedicated program exploring how artificial intelligence is reshaping AV system design, space management, content creation, and predictive maintenance.
  • Pro Summit: An executive-level track aimed at business owners and senior managers focused on industry trends, workforce development, and growth strategies.
  • Women's Breakfast: A networking and professional development event that has become one of InfoComm's most valued community-building programs.

LDI's Practitioner-Focused Sessions

LDI education spans four days of 30- to 60-minute sessions on entertainment design and technology. The format favors rapid knowledge transfer: real-world case studies from touring productions, deep-dive technical workshops on console programming, fixture profiling, and network configuration for entertainment systems. Topics commonly include:

  • Lighting programming and visualization techniques for touring and theater
  • Audio system design for festivals, arenas, and houses of worship
  • Scenic automation and motion control safety
  • Video engineering for live broadcast and IMAG
  • Business of entertainment: freelance career management, production budgeting
  • Emerging technology: virtual production workflows, real-time rendering, LED volume calibration

Where InfoComm education leans toward certification-driven, structured curricula suited for the enterprise AV market, LDI sessions deliver the practical, hands-on wisdom that production professionals need to solve real problems on tour buses and in loading docks.

Registration and Budget Planning

Cost is one of the clearest differentiators between these two events. InfoComm offers a tiered registration model that scales from free exhibit-hall-only passes (when registered early) up to the full Premier Pass at $999 to $1,249, which unlocks the complete conference curriculum, certification prep, Pro Summit, and special programs. The Essentials pass ($699–$899) provides access to a curated subset of sessions and is the sweet spot for professionals who want more than the show floor but do not need every workshop. Exhibit hall access ranges from free to $399 depending on registration timing.

LDI typically offers complimentary exhibit hall access with standard registration, making it significantly more accessible for budget-conscious attendees. Education sessions are generally included without an additional premium, which means you can attend four full days of programming and walk the show floor without spending anything beyond travel and accommodations. For freelancers, independent designers, and smaller production companies, this cost structure makes LDI an easier event to justify to management or fund out of pocket.

Budget Tip: If you plan to attend both shows in 2026, register early for InfoComm to lock in the lowest pass rates, and use the six-month gap between June and December to evaluate what you learned at InfoComm before finalizing your LDI education plan. This staggered approach maximizes your professional development while spreading the financial commitment across two fiscal quarters.

Who Should Attend Which Show?

Attend InfoComm 2026 If You Are:

  • An AV systems integrator sourcing enterprise solutions for corporate, education, or government clients
  • An IT or facilities manager evaluating unified communications and collaboration room technology
  • A consultant pursuing or maintaining CTS, CTS-D, or CTS-I certification
  • A digital signage or streaming professional looking for content management and distribution platforms
  • A business owner or executive who needs the Pro Summit and AI Accelerator strategic programming
  • A manufacturer representative launching products to the broadest possible Pro AV audience

Attend LDI 2026 If You Are:

  • A lighting designer working in concert touring, theater, themed entertainment, or broadcast
  • A sound technician or audio engineer designing systems for live events, houses of worship, or festivals
  • A stage designer or technical director sourcing rigging, staging, and scenic automation solutions
  • A production manager or freelancer building industry connections in the live entertainment community
  • A special effects professional evaluating pyrotechnics, atmospheric effects, and laser technology
  • A creative director exploring virtual production, LED volume workflows, and immersive technologies for live environments

Attend Both If You Are:

  • A rental and staging company that serves both corporate and live entertainment clients
  • A manufacturer or distributor with products that cross enterprise and entertainment markets
  • A technology consultant whose practice spans AV integration and live production
  • A career-minded professional seeking the broadest possible industry exposure and networking reach

Networking and Community Value

InfoComm's networking environment reflects its scale. With over 40,000 attendees, the show generates an enormous volume of business connections, but those connections tend to cluster around enterprise AV purchasing decisions. The AVIXA-organized receptions, the Women's Breakfast, and the Pro Summit create structured networking opportunities that are especially valuable for integrators and consultants building client relationships. The show's size means you can encounter decision-makers from Fortune 500 companies alongside boutique AV design firms.

LDI's networking experience is fundamentally different. The live entertainment community is tight-knit, and LDI functions as the annual reunion for many professionals who spend the rest of the year on tour or in production. The smaller floor creates natural opportunities for extended conversations at exhibitor booths, and the shared creative language of the entertainment industry accelerates relationship building. After-hours events, manufacturer-hosted demos, and the proximity of the West Hall to Las Vegas entertainment venues create a social atmosphere that many attendees consider LDI's greatest strength.

Travel and Logistics

Both shows take place at the Las Vegas Convention Center, which simplifies the logistical comparison. Flights to Harry Reid International Airport (LAS) are abundant and competitive. The key differences come down to timing and on-the-ground support.

InfoComm's June dates place you in Las Vegas during peak summer heat. Temperatures routinely exceed 100 degrees Fahrenheit, which makes the walk between convention center halls and nearby hotels genuinely uncomfortable. The upside is that AVIXA provides complimentary shuttle service between official hotels and the convention center, and the onPeak housing portal offers negotiated group rates that can meaningfully reduce accommodation costs compared to booking independently.

LDI's December dates bring mild Las Vegas winter weather (highs in the mid-50s to low-60s Fahrenheit), making the convention center commute far more pleasant. However, December hotel rates can be unpredictable due to holiday events and the National Finals Rodeo, which traditionally takes place in early December and fills hotels on the east side of the Strip. Book accommodations early and compare rates through Questex's official hotel block against third-party options.

Pro Tip: For InfoComm, take advantage of the onPeak housing system as soon as registration opens. Popular hotels sell out within weeks. For LDI, check whether the National Finals Rodeo dates overlap with the show and book accordingly, as hotel prices can spike during that week.

The Verdict: Which Show Deserves Your 2026 Calendar?

InfoComm 2026 and LDI 2026 are not competitors. They are complementary pillars of a professional AV and live entertainment ecosystem that requires different tools, different training, and different communities depending on where your career lives. InfoComm is the essential event for anyone operating in the enterprise AV world: systems integrators, IT managers, UC specialists, digital signage professionals, and anyone pursuing AVIXA certification. Its scale, exhibitor diversity, and structured education program are unmatched.

LDI is the indispensable show for the live entertainment community: lighting designers, sound engineers, stage technicians, production managers, and creative directors who need to see, touch, and hear the equipment that will power their next production. Its practitioner-focused education, cost-effective registration model, and intimate networking culture make it the heartbeat of an industry built on creativity and technical precision.

If your work spans both worlds—and increasingly, it does as corporate events adopt entertainment-grade production values and live venues integrate enterprise-level AV infrastructure—attending both shows in 2026 gives you a competitive advantage that no single event can deliver. Register early, plan your floor strategy, and make every hour on the show floor count.
