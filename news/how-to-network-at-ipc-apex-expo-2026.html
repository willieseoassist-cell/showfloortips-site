How to Network at IPC APEX EXPO 2026: The Complete Guide for Electronics Manufacturing Professionals

IPC APEX EXPO is the electronics manufacturing industry's most concentrated networking opportunity. Here is how to work the standards meetings, technical sessions, exhibit hall, and Anaheim after-hours scene to build relationships that last well beyond the show floor.

Photo via Unsplash

IPC APEX EXPO 2026 runs March 17-19 at the Anaheim Convention Center, and if you work in electronics manufacturing -- whether you design PCBs, assemble boards, supply components, or sell equipment to the people who do -- this is your single best networking opportunity of the year. With more than 5,000 attendees, 450+ exhibitors, and dozens of IPC standards committee meetings running in parallel, the show packs an extraordinary density of decision-makers into three days and one venue.

But density is only useful if you have a plan. IPC APEX EXPO is not CES, where you wander a massive floor and stumble into conversations. It is a focused, technical show where the most valuable connections happen in committee rooms, during session Q&As, and over dinner at restaurants most attendees never discover. This guide will show you exactly how to work each layer of the event so you leave Anaheim with a contact list that actually converts.

Start Before the Show: IPC Standards Committee Meetings

The highest-value networking at IPC APEX EXPO does not happen on the show floor. It happens in the IPC standards committee meetings that run throughout the week, including sessions that begin before the official expo opens. These committees -- covering everything from IPC-A-610 acceptability standards to J-STD-001 soldering requirements to the emerging IPC-2581 data transfer standard -- are where the industry's most influential engineers and quality managers gather to debate the rules that govern electronics manufacturing worldwide.

If you are not already on a committee, you should be. IPC committee membership is open to any IPC member company, and joining a committee before APEX EXPO gives you an immediate reason to introduce yourself to people who shape billion-dollar procurement decisions. The conversations in these rooms are technical, specific, and candid in a way that show floor interactions never are. You will hear engineers from Apple, Lockheed Martin, Flex, and Jabil discuss real manufacturing challenges and propose real solutions. When you contribute meaningfully to that discussion, you earn credibility that no booth banner can replicate.

"I have closed more business from connections made in IPC standards committee meetings than from any booth I have ever staffed. Those rooms contain the people who write the specs that drive purchasing decisions." -- VP of Sales, mid-size EMS provider

How to Prepare for Committee Meetings

  • Register for specific committees in advance. Check the IPC APEX EXPO schedule for committee meeting times and rooms. Popular committees like IPC-A-610 and J-STD-001 fill up, and arriving early ensures you get a seat at the table rather than standing along the wall.
  • Read the current revision of the standard. Nothing kills your credibility faster than asking a question that the document already answers. Skim the most recent revision, note any sections relevant to your work, and prepare one or two substantive comments.
  • Introduce yourself during breaks, not during sessions. Committee meetings have structured agendas. The networking happens in the five-minute breaks between agenda items and in the hallway immediately afterward. Position yourself near the door and catch people as they step out.

Technical Sessions: Networking Disguised as Education

IPC APEX EXPO's technical conference features more than 100 sessions across multiple tracks, including assembly and reliability, design, factory of the future, and high-reliability electronics. For networking purposes, these sessions serve as pre-filtered rooms. Attend a session on advanced packaging techniques and you are sitting next to the exact engineers and managers who care about advanced packaging techniques. The targeting is automatic.

The networking technique is simple. During the Q&A portion, ask a thoughtful question -- not a softball, not a sales pitch disguised as a question, but a genuine technical inquiry that demonstrates you understand the topic. After the session, approach the speaker and reference your question. Then turn to the person sitting next to you and ask what they thought of the presentation. These two moves -- public question, private follow-up -- generate more meaningful connections in 20 minutes than four hours of badge-scanning on the show floor.

Session Strategy: The Two-Track Approach

Split your technical session schedule between your core expertise and one adjacent area you want to learn. If you are a PCB designer, attend two design sessions and one assembly session. If you sell soldering equipment, attend one soldering session and one inspection or test session. The cross-pollination sessions are where you meet people outside your existing network -- the connections that open doors to new markets and new opportunities. Your core sessions reinforce your expertise with peers who already speak your language.

Working the Exhibit Hall: Quality Over Quantity

The IPC APEX EXPO exhibit hall is compact compared to mega-shows like Productronica or SEMICON West, and that compactness is an advantage. With roughly 450 exhibitors, you can walk the entire floor in two hours and then spend the rest of your time having real conversations rather than rushing from aisle to aisle. The key is knowing how to approach exhibitors in a way that generates genuine dialogue instead of a polite brush-off.

For Attendees Visiting Booths

  • Skip the front-of-booth greeters. The most valuable people in any booth are the engineers and product managers, not the badge scanners stationed at the entrance. Walk past the welcome desk, look for the demo stations in the back, and ask a specific technical question about the equipment or process being shown. That question is your entry point to a real conversation.
  • Bring a specific problem. The best exhibitor conversations start with "We are struggling with X on our line -- how would your system handle that?" This immediately elevates you from anonymous attendee to qualified prospect, and the exhibitor will give you their full attention and their direct contact information.
  • Visit on Tuesday afternoon. The show floor is most crowded on opening day (Tuesday morning). By Tuesday afternoon, the initial rush has subsided, and exhibitors have more time for substantive conversations. Wednesday morning is also excellent for detailed demos, as exhibitors are energized but not yet fatigued.

For Exhibitors Working the Booth

  • Staff with engineers, not just salespeople. IPC APEX EXPO attendees are overwhelmingly technical. A conversation that starts with specifications and process capabilities builds trust faster than a conversation that starts with pricing and lead times. Have at least one process engineer in the booth at all times.
  • Run live demos on a schedule. Announce demo times on your booth signage and social media. Scheduled demos create foot traffic spikes and give you a reason to invite specific attendees to return at a particular time -- which is a natural follow-up touchpoint.
  • Capture context, not just contact info. When you scan a badge, immediately note what the attendee asked about and what problem they mentioned. A follow-up email that says "Great talking about your wave soldering defect rates -- here is the case study I mentioned" converts at five times the rate of a generic "Thanks for visiting our booth" message.

After Hours in Anaheim: Where the Real Deals Happen

Anaheim's dining and bar scene has evolved significantly from the days when your only options were chain restaurants on Harbor Boulevard. The Anaheim Packing District, a 10-minute ride from the convention center, has become the unofficial after-hours gathering spot for IPC APEX EXPO attendees who know where to go. Here are the spots where you will find industry conversations continuing over food and drinks.

  • The Packing House (Anaheim Packing District). This converted 1919 citrus packing facility now houses two dozen food vendors and a full bar. The communal seating and open layout make it easy to run into people you met on the show floor. Go between 6 and 8 PM on Tuesday for the highest density of APEX attendees.
  • Blind Rabbit (inside The Packing House). A speakeasy-style cocktail bar hidden behind a bookshelf. Reservations are difficult but not impossible -- book two weeks ahead through their website. Ideal for a small-group dinner meeting with three or four key contacts. The intimate setting forces real conversation.
  • Anaheim Brewery. A block from the Packing District, this craft brewery has a relaxed taproom that is perfect for casual post-show drinks. Groups of 6-10 can grab a long table without a reservation. The noise level is moderate enough for business conversation.
  • The Ranch Restaurant & Saloon. Located on South Harbor, this upscale steakhouse is where the larger exhibitor dinners tend to land. If you are hosting clients or prospects, this is the venue that impresses without feeling ostentatious. Reserve the private dining room for groups of 12-20.
  • Napa Rose (Disney's Grand Californian). Yes, it is inside a Disney property. No, it is not a theme park restaurant. Napa Rose holds a AAA Four Diamond rating and one of the best wine lists in Orange County. Use it for your most important prospect dinner. The contrast between the Disney exterior and the sophisticated interior becomes an icebreaker.

Post-Show Follow-Up: The 72-Hour Rule

Everything you did at IPC APEX EXPO -- the committee meetings, the technical sessions, the exhibit hall conversations, the dinners -- is wasted if your follow-up is slow or generic. The electronics manufacturing industry is small enough that people remember who followed up promptly and who disappeared after the show.

  1. Send personalized emails within 72 hours. Reference the specific conversation you had, the problem they described, or the session you attended together. Attach any resource you promised to share. The longer you wait, the more your connection fades into the blur of badge scans and handshakes.
  2. Connect on LinkedIn with a custom message. Do not use LinkedIn's default connection text. Write two sentences that remind the person where you met and what you discussed. This small effort distinguishes you from the 50 other connection requests they received that week.
  3. Schedule a follow-up call before the next IPC event. IPC holds regional workshops and webinars throughout the year. Use the next upcoming event as a reason to reach out again: "I saw you are on the agenda for the IPC workshop in June -- want to connect beforehand to continue our conversation about X?" This creates a rhythm of interaction that builds relationships over time, not just over three days in Anaheim.
  4. Share your notes with your team. If you attended committee meetings or technical sessions, write a brief summary and distribute it internally. This positions you as the company's IPC expert and ensures the intelligence you gathered gets used, not forgotten.

IPC APEX EXPO 2026 is three days long. The relationships you build there, if you approach the show with intention and follow through with discipline, will generate business for years. The electronics manufacturing industry rewards people who show up prepared, contribute genuinely, and follow up consistently. The show floor is where the introductions happen. Everything after that is up to you.
