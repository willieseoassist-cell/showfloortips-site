World Defense Show 2026 Expands 58% as AI Autonomy, Unmanned Systems, and Space Dominate the Floor — What It Means for Sea-Air-Space, AUSA, and Space Symposium

By ShowFloorTips Research · February 11, 2026

Three days ago, World Defense Show 2026 opened its doors in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, and the numbers alone tell a story of extraordinary momentum. The show has expanded 58% over its previous edition, drawing 925 exhibitors from 80 countries across 273,000 square meters of exhibition space. That is not incremental growth. That is the kind of expansion that signals a fundamental shift in where the global defense industry convenes, how it prioritizes emerging technologies, and which buyers command the attention of the world's largest contractors.

For exhibitors in the defense and aerospace sector, World Defense Show 2026 is not simply another event on the calendar. It is a leading indicator. The themes dominating Riyadh this week -- artificial intelligence, autonomous systems, unmanned platforms across every domain, and the militarization of space -- are the same themes that will define the exhibitor experience at Sea-Air-Space 2026 in National Harbor this spring, AUSA 2026 in Washington this October, and Space Symposium 2026 in Colorado Springs. What happens on this floor in Riyadh reverberates through every major defense trade show for the rest of the year.

This article breaks down the five transformative themes at World Defense Show 2026, analyzes the strategic implications for exhibitors, and provides a concrete action plan for companies preparing to exhibit at the major U.S. and international defense shows in the months ahead.

The Five Themes Reshaping the Defense Trade Show Floor

1. AI-Driven Autonomy Moves from Concept to Centerpiece

If there is a single defining narrative at World Defense Show 2026, it is the mainstreaming of artificial intelligence and autonomous systems as operational defense capabilities rather than research curiosities. The show's newly created Future Defense Lab -- a dedicated exhibition zone focused entirely on AI, autonomous systems, robotics, quantum computing, and advanced materials -- represents an institutional acknowledgment that these technologies have crossed the threshold from theoretical to deployable.

BAE Systems is among the most prominent exhibitors showcasing AI-driven autonomy solutions in Riyadh. Their booth demonstrates how AI integrates across command-and-control architectures, enabling faster decision cycles, predictive maintenance for complex platforms, and autonomous threat assessment. This is not a science project. BAE is presenting operational systems designed for near-term procurement by sovereign militaries that are actively modernizing their capabilities.

The AI theme at World Defense Show extends far beyond a single exhibitor or zone. Across the 273,000-square-meter floor, companies from dozens of countries are presenting AI applications that range from logistics optimization and supply chain prediction to real-time battlefield intelligence fusion. The common thread is autonomy: the capacity for systems to operate with reduced human supervision, make decisions at machine speed, and adapt to dynamic environments without manual reprogramming.

For exhibitors preparing for the 2026 defense trade show season, the message from Riyadh is unambiguous. AI autonomy is no longer a differentiator -- it is a baseline expectation. Defense buyers arriving at Sea-Air-Space, AUSA, and Space Symposium later this year will expect every serious exhibitor to articulate how artificial intelligence enhances their platform, system, or service. Companies that treat AI as an optional booth talking point rather than a core capability narrative risk being categorized as legacy suppliers.

Key Takeaway AI autonomy has moved from R&D briefings to procurement conversations. Exhibitors at every defense show in 2026 must demonstrate operational AI capabilities, not conceptual ones. If your booth cannot answer the question "How does AI make this faster, cheaper, or more survivable?" you will lose ground to competitors who can.

2. The Unmanned Systems Zone: Aerial, Ground, and Maritime

World Defense Show 2026 introduces a dedicated Unmanned Systems Zone for the first time, and the scope of what it contains illustrates how comprehensively unmanned platforms have penetrated every warfighting domain. The zone showcases autonomous platforms spanning aerial drones, ground vehicles, and surface vessels, alongside AI-driven swarming concepts that coordinate multiple unmanned assets simultaneously.

The aerial segment remains the most mature, with exhibitors presenting everything from small tactical surveillance drones to large, long-endurance unmanned combat aerial vehicles. Saudi Arabian Military Industries (SAMI) is a headline presence, showcasing more than 60 national products including a futuristic UAV concept that signals Saudi Arabia's ambitions to become a UAV manufacturer and exporter, not merely a consumer. The SAMI booth alone is worth extended analysis for any exhibitor tracking the competitive landscape in unmanned aviation.

But the real story in the Unmanned Systems Zone is the expansion into ground and maritime domains. Autonomous ground vehicles -- ranging from logistics carriers to armed reconnaissance platforms -- are being presented by exhibitors from the United States, China, Turkey, South Korea, and the UAE. Maritime unmanned surface vessels, which were barely visible at defense shows three years ago, now occupy a significant portion of the zone, reflecting the rapid escalation of unmanned systems in naval operations worldwide.

The swarming concepts on display deserve particular attention from exhibitors. Multiple companies are demonstrating AI-driven coordination of drone swarms -- dozens or hundreds of unmanned platforms operating as a unified force with decentralized decision-making. These systems blur the line between individual platform sales and integrated network solutions, creating both challenges and opportunities for defense exhibitors accustomed to selling discrete hardware products.

3. The Naval Domain Goes Autonomous

World Defense Show 2026 introduces a dedicated Naval Zone alongside the Unmanned Systems Zone, and the convergence of these two areas tells a story that every maritime defense exhibitor needs to internalize before Sea-Air-Space 2026 opens in National Harbor this spring. Naval warfare is undergoing a structural transformation driven by unmanned surface vessels, autonomous underwater vehicles, and AI-integrated fleet management systems.

The Naval Zone in Riyadh features exhibitors from shipbuilding nations including South Korea, France, Germany, Italy, and the United Kingdom, alongside Saudi Arabia's own expanding naval industrial base. What distinguishes this year's naval exhibits from previous defense shows is the degree to which unmanned and autonomous systems have been integrated into conventional naval platforms. It is no longer a matter of a manned frigate and a separate unmanned vessel operating independently. The exhibits in Riyadh show integrated task forces where manned and unmanned vessels share sensor data, coordinate maneuvers through AI-driven mission planning, and allocate tasks based on real-time threat assessment.

For companies preparing to exhibit at Sea-Air-Space 2026 -- the U.S. Navy's premier exposition, organized by the Navy League -- the naval autonomy theme at World Defense Show provides a preview of buyer expectations. Sea-Air-Space attendees include flag officers, fleet commanders, program managers, and acquisition officials who are actively funding unmanned maritime programs. Exhibitors who arrive at National Harbor with only traditional naval platforms and no autonomous integration story will find themselves answering uncomfortable questions from the very buyers they are trying to impress.

"The defense trade show floor in 2026 looks fundamentally different from even two years ago. Unmanned systems are not a niche pavilion anymore -- they are the floor. Every serious exhibitor is integrating autonomy into their narrative, and every serious buyer is asking about it. If you are still exhibiting the same manned platform with no autonomy roadmap, you are exhibiting last decade's capability." — Senior defense industry analyst covering Middle East procurement

4. Space Domain Arrives as a Dedicated Exhibition Category

The addition of a Space Domain zone at World Defense Show 2026 marks another first for the show and reflects the accelerating militarization and commercialization of space. The zone features satellite communications providers, space situational awareness companies, launch vehicle manufacturers, and emerging space-based defense systems. The inclusion of space as a standalone category -- rather than a subsection of an aerospace pavilion -- signals that defense procurement agencies worldwide now treat space as a distinct operational domain requiring dedicated investment.

Saudi Arabia's own space ambitions under Vision 2030 are a driving force behind the zone's creation. The Kingdom has established the Saudi Space Agency and is investing in satellite manufacturing, launch infrastructure, and space-based surveillance capabilities. For international exhibitors in the space domain, the Saudi market represents a significant greenfield opportunity: a sovereign nation with substantial capital, clear strategic ambitions, and an urgent need for technology transfer partnerships.

The space theme at World Defense Show has direct implications for exhibitors planning to attend Space Symposium 2026 in Colorado Springs. Space Symposium, organized by the Space Foundation, is the premier gathering of the military, intelligence, civil, and commercial space communities. The exhibitor profiles overlap significantly with those in the World Defense Show Space Domain zone, and the technology conversations are continuous. Companies that refine their space messaging in Riyadh will have a significant advantage when they set up in Colorado Springs.

Key space-domain topics generating buyer interest at World Defense Show include resilient satellite communications (particularly in contested environments), space-based ISR (intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance), anti-satellite countermeasures, and the integration of space-derived data into multi-domain command-and-control architectures. These same topics will dominate Space Symposium and will appear prominently in the aerospace pavilions at AUSA and Sea-Air-Space.

5. The Geopolitical Exhibitor Landscape: A Multipolar Floor

One of the most strategically significant aspects of World Defense Show 2026 is the composition of its exhibitor base. The show floor is genuinely multipolar in a way that no Western defense exhibition achieves. Alongside the expected presence of major Western contractors -- Lockheed Martin highlighting the F-35, THAAD, and PAC-3 missile defense systems; BAE Systems with its AI autonomy portfolio -- the show features more than 50 Chinese defense firms participating under organizations including CATIC and NORINCO. Russian state export agency Rosoboronexport is making its international show debut at this edition, adding another major defense exporter to a floor that already includes Turkish, South Korean, Indian, Brazilian, and Emirati companies.

Lockheed Martin's presence in Riyadh is substantial and strategically calculated. The F-35 remains the world's most capable fifth-generation fighter, and THAAD and PAC-3 represent the premier missile defense systems available for export. By showcasing these platforms at World Defense Show, Lockheed Martin is engaging directly with Gulf state procurement officials who control some of the largest defense budgets on the planet. The company's booth is designed to reinforce the message that interoperability with the U.S. defense ecosystem delivers capabilities that no alternative supplier can match.

The Chinese presence, however, represents an increasingly credible alternative narrative. With more than 50 firms exhibiting, China is presenting a comprehensive defense industrial offering that includes combat aircraft, armored vehicles, naval vessels, air defense systems, drones, and -- critically -- integrated C4ISR (command, control, communications, computers, intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance) solutions. For nations seeking defense modernization without the political conditions that sometimes accompany Western arms transfers, Chinese exhibitors offer a compelling value proposition.

Rosoboronexport's participation, despite ongoing geopolitical tensions, underscores the reality that World Defense Show occupies a unique position as a genuinely non-aligned marketplace. Saudi Arabia's willingness to host exhibitors from every major defense-exporting nation reflects its own strategic posture: a buyer that maintains relationships across geopolitical blocs to maximize its procurement options and industrial partnerships.

For exhibitors at U.S. defense shows later in 2026, the geopolitical dynamics on display in Riyadh provide critical competitive intelligence. Companies that exhibit at Sea-Air-Space, AUSA, or Space Symposium without understanding what their Chinese, Turkish, and Korean competitors presented in Riyadh are operating with an incomplete picture of the competitive landscape.

Saudi Arabia's Strategic Transformation: From Consumer to Industrial Partner

Beneath the exhibition floor statistics and technology showcases lies a strategic narrative that every defense exhibitor must understand: Saudi Arabia is executing a deliberate, well-funded transformation from a defense consumer into a defense industrial partner. This shift, driven by the Kingdom's Vision 2030 diversification program, has profound implications for how international companies engage with Saudi procurement and what they should expect at World Defense Show and beyond.

Saudi Arabian Military Industries (SAMI) is the institutional embodiment of this transformation. SAMI's showcase at World Defense Show 2026 features more than 60 nationally produced defense products -- an extraordinary expansion from even five years ago, when Saudi Arabia manufactured almost none of its own defense equipment. The products span land systems, naval platforms, ammunition, aerospace components, and -- most notably -- unmanned aerial vehicles. SAMI's futuristic UAV concept is not merely a technology demonstrator; it is a statement of intent that Saudi Arabia plans to compete in the global UAV export market.

For international exhibitors, this transformation creates a new strategic calculus. The era of simply selling finished systems to Saudi Arabia is evolving into an era of industrial partnerships, technology transfer agreements, joint ventures, and co-production arrangements. Saudi procurement officials are increasingly evaluating not just the capability of a system but the willingness of the supplier to localize production, transfer intellectual property, and invest in Saudi workforce development.

This shift has direct implications for exhibitor strategy at every defense show in 2026. Companies that can articulate a credible industrial partnership story -- "Here is how we will build this in Saudi Arabia, train Saudi engineers, and create Saudi jobs" -- will have a competitive advantage over companies that can only articulate a product story. This is true at World Defense Show, and it is increasingly true at shows like AUSA and Sea-Air-Space, where Gulf state delegations attend in significant numbers.

"The Kingdom is not simply buying defense capability anymore. It is building defense capability. Every major contract now includes offset requirements, localization targets, and technology transfer provisions. If your pitch is 'buy our product,' you are already behind. The pitch must be 'build our product together.'" — Defense procurement consultant advising Gulf Cooperation Council nations

Live Demonstrations: Why Static Displays Are No Longer Sufficient

World Defense Show 2026 features live demonstrations across a 2,700-meter runway and land track -- a capability that most defense exhibitions simply cannot replicate. These live demonstrations allow exhibitors to move beyond static booth displays and show their platforms in operational conditions: vehicles maneuvering across terrain, aircraft performing tactical demonstrations, weapons systems engaging targets, and unmanned platforms executing autonomous missions.

The live demonstration format at World Defense Show reflects a broader trend in defense trade shows: buyers increasingly demand to see platforms in action, not just in renderings and specifications. The era of selling major defense systems entirely from a booth display is fading. Decision-makers who control multi-billion-dollar procurement budgets want to see the vehicle drive, the aircraft fly, and the missile launch before they sign a letter of intent.

For exhibitors at shows that lack live demonstration facilities -- including Sea-Air-Space and Space Symposium -- the lesson is to invest more heavily in simulation, virtual reality demonstrations, and high-quality video content captured at events like World Defense Show. If you demonstrated a live capability in Riyadh, that footage becomes your most valuable sales asset for the rest of the year. Film everything. Produce professional-grade content. Use it at every subsequent show.

AUSA 2026, held at the Walter E. Washington Convention Center in Washington, D.C., does not offer a runway or land track, but it does feature indoor capability demonstrations and increasingly sophisticated simulation environments. Exhibitors who participate in live demonstrations at World Defense Show should plan to bring that same experiential approach -- adapted for an indoor environment -- to the AUSA floor.

Exhibitor Strategy: How to Translate World Defense Show Themes into 2026 Show Season Success

The themes dominating World Defense Show 2026 are not unique to Riyadh. They are the defining themes of the entire 2026 defense trade show season. Here is how to apply the insights from World Defense Show to your exhibitor strategy at the three most important U.S. defense shows this year.

Sea-Air-Space 2026 (Spring, National Harbor, Maryland)

Sea-Air-Space, organized by the Navy League of the United States, is the nation's premier naval exposition. It draws senior Navy, Marine Corps, and Coast Guard leadership alongside international naval delegations. Based on what World Defense Show 2026 has revealed, here is how to position your Sea-Air-Space booth for maximum impact.

  • Lead with unmanned maritime: The Naval and Unmanned Systems Zones at World Defense Show confirm that autonomous surface and subsurface vessels are at the top of every navy's priority list. At Sea-Air-Space, your booth should dedicate prominent space to unmanned maritime platforms, manned-unmanned teaming concepts, and autonomous mission management systems. If unmanned is a secondary message at your booth, you are misreading the market.
  • Demonstrate AI integration in naval C2: Sea-Air-Space attendees include program managers who are actively funding AI-driven command-and-control programs. Show how your systems integrate AI for faster decision cycles, sensor fusion, and predictive logistics. Prepare live demonstrations or high-fidelity simulations that let visitors interact with your AI capabilities.
  • Address the multipolar competitive landscape: International delegations at Sea-Air-Space are increasingly aware of Chinese and Turkish naval offerings. Prepare competitive intelligence materials that articulate why your platform offers superior capability, interoperability, and lifecycle support compared to alternatives being marketed at shows like World Defense Show.
  • Pre-schedule meetings with Gulf delegations: Saudi, Emirati, and other Gulf naval officials who attended World Defense Show will also send delegations to Sea-Air-Space. These are active buyers with substantial budgets. Use the World Defense Show contact lists and intelligence to arrange meetings in advance.
  • Capture leads digitally from day one: The Navy League exhibition floor generates enormous foot traffic. Use a digital lead capture app to record every meaningful conversation, tag prospects by program and priority, and enable 48-hour follow-up after the show closes.

AUSA 2026 (October, Washington, D.C.)

The Association of the United States Army Annual Meeting and Exposition is the largest land-warfare trade show in the world. AUSA 2026 will draw more than 30,000 attendees and 700+ exhibitors to the Walter E. Washington Convention Center. Here is how World Defense Show themes translate to the AUSA floor.

  • Ground autonomy is the headliner: The autonomous ground vehicles on display at World Defense Show -- from logistics carriers to armed reconnaissance platforms -- preview exactly what AUSA attendees will demand to see. The U.S. Army's Robotic Combat Vehicle program, autonomous logistics initiatives, and counter-UAS systems are all active procurement priorities. Position your AUSA booth to address these programs directly.
  • AI for multi-domain operations: AUSA is the primary venue for the U.S. Army's multi-domain operations concept, which relies heavily on AI-driven sensor fusion, autonomous decision aids, and cross-domain data integration. The AI themes at World Defense Show are a direct preview. Exhibitors who can demonstrate AI capabilities that support multi-domain operations will receive disproportionate attention from senior Army leadership.
  • Invest in simulation and immersive experiences: World Defense Show's live demonstration capability creates an experiential standard that AUSA exhibitors must match through alternative means. Invest in high-fidelity simulation environments, augmented reality overlays, and interactive touchscreen displays that let AUSA visitors experience your platform's capabilities without a runway.
  • Prepare industrial partnership narratives: The Saudi industrial partnership model revealed at World Defense Show is relevant at AUSA because Gulf state delegations attend AUSA in large numbers. Additionally, the U.S. Army itself is increasingly interested in allied industrial partnerships and co-production arrangements. Prepare materials that address industrial cooperation, not just product capability.
  • Counter-UAS is non-negotiable: The proliferation of drone threats demonstrated by the swarming concepts at World Defense Show means that every exhibitor with any counter-UAS capability must feature it prominently at AUSA. This is the single most urgent operational requirement for ground forces worldwide, and AUSA is where the U.S. Army makes its counter-UAS investment decisions.

Space Symposium 2026 (Colorado Springs, Colorado)

Space Symposium, organized by the Space Foundation, is the premier event for military, intelligence, civil, and commercial space professionals. The Space Domain zone at World Defense Show provides a roadmap for Space Symposium exhibitor strategy.

  • Resilient communications dominate: The space exhibits at World Defense Show emphasize resilient satellite communications -- the ability to maintain connectivity in contested and degraded environments. Space Symposium attendees from the U.S. Space Force, National Reconnaissance Office, and allied space agencies are actively seeking these solutions. Make resilient comms your lead message.
  • Space-based ISR and data integration: The convergence of space-based intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance with AI-driven data analytics is a top priority for every major space customer. At Space Symposium, demonstrate how your satellite or sensor system integrates with multi-domain command architectures and delivers actionable intelligence at machine speed.
  • Engage commercial space innovators: Space Symposium has a strong commercial space component that World Defense Show does not fully replicate. Use Space Symposium to build relationships with commercial launch providers, small satellite manufacturers, and space-based data analytics companies that are increasingly relevant to defense customers.
  • Present space-domain competitive intelligence: The Chinese space-related exhibits at World Defense Show provide valuable intelligence about competitor capabilities. Space Symposium attendees -- particularly those from the intelligence and policy communities -- are deeply interested in understanding the competitive landscape. Prepare briefing materials that contextualize your capabilities relative to adversary and competitor offerings.
  • Staff with technical depth: Space Symposium attendees are among the most technically sophisticated audiences at any trade show. Staff your booth with engineers and program managers, not just business development representatives. The conversations at Space Symposium are deeply technical, and attendees will quickly disengage from booths that cannot match their expertise level.
Key Takeaway The five dominant themes at World Defense Show 2026 -- AI autonomy, unmanned systems across all domains, naval transformation, space as a dedicated warfighting domain, and the multipolar competitive landscape -- will define buyer expectations at every major defense exhibition for the remainder of 2026. Exhibitors who align their booth strategy, messaging, and demonstration capabilities to these themes at Sea-Air-Space, AUSA, and Space Symposium will capture a disproportionate share of buyer attention and procurement opportunities.

The 60-Plus National Products: Why SAMI Matters to Every Exhibitor

Saudi Arabian Military Industries' presentation of more than 60 nationally produced defense products at World Defense Show 2026 deserves dedicated analysis because it signals a competitive dynamic that affects every international exhibitor in the defense sector. When a nation with Saudi Arabia's defense budget transitions from buyer to builder, the entire competitive landscape shifts.

SAMI's product portfolio now spans land systems, naval platforms, ammunition, aerospace components, and UAVs. For established defense exporters, this means that some product categories that were once exclusively served by imports now face domestic competition in the Saudi market. For smaller and mid-tier defense companies, it means that partnership and co-production opportunities with SAMI may be more valuable than direct export sales.

The practical exhibitor implication: when you prepare for any defense show in 2026 where Saudi delegations will be present -- including Sea-Air-Space and AUSA -- research SAMI's product portfolio carefully. Understand where SAMI competes with you, where SAMI complements you, and where a SAMI partnership could multiply your market access. The exhibitors who treat SAMI as a potential partner rather than an abstract competitor will have a significant strategic advantage.

Booth Design Lessons from the 273,000-Square-Meter Floor

The sheer scale of World Defense Show 2026 -- 273,000 square meters of exhibition space -- creates design challenges that are relevant to any large defense trade show. Here are the booth design principles that are separating successful exhibitors from forgettable ones on the Riyadh floor.

Verticality and Visibility

On a floor this large, low-profile booths disappear. The most successful exhibitors at World Defense Show are using vertical elements -- suspended platforms, elevated display screens, towering hardware mockups -- to create visibility from three or four aisles away. At AUSA, where the convention center floor is dense and crowded, vertical design elements are equally critical. Invest in overhead signage, raised platforms, and tall display structures that pull attendees toward your booth from a distance.

Demonstration Zones over Display Walls

The booths generating the most sustained traffic at World Defense Show are those with interactive demonstration areas where visitors can engage with systems rather than simply look at them. Touchscreen command-and-control simulators, drone piloting stations, VR headsets showing platform capabilities from an operator's perspective -- these experiential elements keep visitors in the booth longer and generate deeper conversations than static posters or product brochures.

Dedicated Meeting Spaces

Defense procurement conversations require confidentiality. The most effective booths at World Defense Show include enclosed or semi-enclosed meeting rooms where classified or sensitive discussions can occur without being overheard. At Sea-Air-Space and AUSA, the same principle applies. Budget for at least one private meeting space within or adjacent to your booth, and pre-schedule meetings with priority prospects to ensure it is used at maximum capacity throughout the show.

Multilingual and Multicultural Readiness

With 80 countries represented at World Defense Show, successful exhibitors staff their booths with multilingual team members and prepare key materials in Arabic, English, French, and other languages relevant to their target markets. This lesson applies directly to Sea-Air-Space and AUSA, which draw significant international delegations. If you cannot staff multilingual team members, invest in translated digital materials accessible via QR code.

What to Watch for the Rest of 2026

World Defense Show 2026 opens the defense trade show season with a clear set of messages. As you plan your exhibition calendar for the rest of the year, keep these dynamics front of mind.

The AI arms race on the trade show floor is real. Every major defense exhibitor is now incorporating AI into their product narrative. By the time AUSA opens in October, the companies that fail to demonstrate operational AI capabilities will be perceived as behind. Do not wait until summer to develop your AI story. Start now.

Unmanned systems are not a specialty category anymore. They are mainstream. At Sea-Air-Space, the unmanned maritime conversation will dominate. At AUSA, ground autonomy will be the headline. At Space Symposium, autonomous satellite operations and space-based autonomy will be front and center. Every exhibitor needs an unmanned and autonomous systems narrative, even if their primary platform is manned.

Space is no longer an afterthought. The creation of a dedicated Space Domain zone at World Defense Show confirms that space has achieved parity with land, sea, and air as an exhibition category. Space Symposium will be the definitive event, but space themes will be prominent at every defense show in 2026.

The competitive landscape is multipolar. Chinese, Turkish, Korean, and Emirati defense companies are exhibiting world-class capabilities at World Defense Show. Western exhibitors who ignore this competitive pressure do so at their peril. Prepare competitive intelligence for every show you attend this year.

Industrial partnerships are replacing simple export sales. The Saudi model -- co-production, technology transfer, local workforce development -- is being adopted by defense buyers worldwide. Exhibitors who can offer partnership models, not just products, will win the most valuable contracts of 2026.

Key Takeaway World Defense Show 2026 is the opening statement of a defense trade show season defined by AI autonomy, unmanned systems across every domain, space as a warfighting priority, and a multipolar competitive landscape. Exhibitors who read these signals correctly and adapt their strategy for Sea-Air-Space, AUSA, and Space Symposium will be positioned to capture the most significant procurement opportunities of the year. Those who wait will be reacting to a market that has already moved.
