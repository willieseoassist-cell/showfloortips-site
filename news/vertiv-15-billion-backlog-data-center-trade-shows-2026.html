Vertiv's $15 Billion Backlog and 252% Order Growth — Why Data Center Infrastructure Dominates Trade Shows in 2026

ShowFloorTips Editorial · February 11, 2026

Vertiv Holdings just delivered a quarter that silenced every skeptic in the data center infrastructure space. The company's stock surged 22 percent after reporting a $15 billion backlog, 252 percent year-over-year order growth, and Q4 2025 sales of $2.88 billion — a 23 percent jump from the same period a year ago. Management raised its 2026 revenue guidance to a range of $13.25 billion to $13.75 billion, crushing the Wall Street consensus estimate of $12.39 billion by more than a billion dollars. These are not the numbers of a company riding a temporary wave. They are the numbers of an industry undergoing the most dramatic infrastructure buildout since the dawn of the commercial internet.

For trade show exhibitors in the data center, power management, cooling, and critical infrastructure sectors, Vertiv's results are both a signal and a roadmap. The signal is clear: enterprise and hyperscale demand for data center infrastructure is accelerating, not plateauing, and buyers are committing capital at unprecedented scale. The roadmap points directly to the shows that will define vendor selection in 2026 — Data Centre World in London, OFC in San Francisco, and Hannover Messe in Germany.

The AI Infrastructure Supercycle Is Real

Behind Vertiv's staggering numbers is a single driving force: the global AI infrastructure supercycle. Every major hyperscaler — Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, Oracle — is building data centers at a pace that has no historical precedent. Microsoft alone committed to spending over $80 billion on AI-enabled data centers in its fiscal year 2025. Amazon Web Services announced $100 billion in planned data center investments. Meta is constructing a facility so large it required its own dedicated power plant. These investments are not speculative bets on a future technology. They are responses to existing demand from enterprise customers deploying AI workloads at scale.

The result is a supply chain under extraordinary pressure. Data center operators need more power, more cooling, more racks, more connectivity, more everything — and they need it faster than the industry has ever delivered. Vertiv's 252 percent order growth reflects not just enthusiasm but urgency. Buyers are placing orders years in advance to secure capacity, and they are doing so with the understanding that lead times for critical components like power distribution units, UPS systems, and precision cooling infrastructure have extended significantly.

What Vertiv's Numbers Tell Us About Market Demand

  • Q4 2025 revenue of $2.88 billion (up 23% year-over-year) demonstrates that demand is translating into deliveries, not just promises.
  • 252% order growth means customers are committing capital at a rate that far outpaces current delivery capacity, creating a multi-year revenue pipeline.
  • $15 billion backlog provides visibility into demand that extends well into 2027 and beyond, insulating the sector from near-term economic fluctuations.
  • 2026 guidance of $13.25B–$13.75B implies management confidence in sustained execution against that backlog, with upside potential if supply chain constraints ease.
Key TakeawayVertiv's results are a proxy for the entire data center infrastructure supply chain. If you manufacture, integrate, or service any component that goes into a data center — from power cables to cooling towers to fire suppression systems — your addressable market just expanded dramatically. The trade shows on the 2026 calendar are where that demand meets supply.

The Power Problem: Data Centers' Biggest Constraint

The single largest bottleneck in the data center buildout is power. AI workloads consume dramatically more electricity than traditional computing. A single AI training cluster can draw as much power as a small town. Rack densities that were considered extreme at 20 kilowatts per rack five years ago are now standard, and leading-edge AI deployments are pushing past 100 kilowatts per rack. The International Energy Agency projects that data center electricity consumption will double between 2024 and 2028, reaching over 1,000 terawatt-hours globally — roughly equivalent to Japan's total electricity consumption.

This power challenge creates enormous opportunities for exhibitors across multiple categories. UPS manufacturers, switchgear providers, transformer companies, power cable specialists, fuel cell developers, and small modular reactor startups are all finding receptive audiences at infrastructure shows. The question is no longer whether data centers need more power — it is how to generate, distribute, and manage that power efficiently, reliably, and sustainably.

Cooling: The Second Critical Constraint

Where power is the first constraint, cooling is the second. High-density AI racks generate heat at levels that overwhelm traditional air-cooling systems. The industry is rapidly transitioning to liquid cooling solutions — direct-to-chip, immersion cooling, and rear-door heat exchangers — that can handle the thermal loads of modern GPU clusters. Vertiv has invested heavily in liquid cooling capabilities, and competitors like Schneider Electric, Rittal, and CoolIT Systems are scaling production as fast as possible.

For exhibitors in the cooling and thermal management space, the 2026 show calendar presents a rare opportunity. Data center operators are actively evaluating liquid cooling solutions, and many are making vendor selection decisions in the first half of 2026 to meet deployment timelines in late 2026 and 2027. A well-executed booth presence at Data Centre World or OFC can put your solution directly in front of the engineers and procurement leaders making those decisions.

Trade Show Strategy: Where to Be in 2026

Data Centre World — London, March 4–5

Data Centre World at ExCeL London is the premier European event for data center professionals. With over 15,000 attendees and 300+ exhibitors, it delivers concentrated access to the operators, engineers, and procurement leaders building and managing Europe's data center capacity. The timing is critical: European data center construction is accelerating as sovereignty requirements, GDPR compliance, and AI demand drive local infrastructure investment. For exhibitors targeting the European market, Data Centre World is the single most efficient use of trade show budget on the 2026 calendar.

OFC 2026 — San Francisco, March 30 – April 3

The Optical Fiber Communication Conference and Exhibition is where the networking and connectivity layer of data center infrastructure comes together. OFC attracts over 13,000 attendees focused on optical networking, fiber infrastructure, and high-speed interconnects — the plumbing that connects racks, buildings, and campuses within hyperscale data center environments. As AI workloads demand ever-higher bandwidth between GPU clusters, the optical networking market is experiencing its own demand supercycle. Exhibitors offering 800G and 1.6T optical solutions, co-packaged optics, or novel fiber technologies will find an intensely engaged buyer audience.

Hannover Messe — Germany, April 20–24

Hannover Messe is the world's largest industrial technology exhibition, and its energy and power infrastructure halls are increasingly relevant to the data center sector. With over 130,000 attendees from 150+ countries, Hannover Messe attracts the power generation, distribution, and management companies whose products are essential to data center operations. For exhibitors whose solutions span industrial and data center applications — switchgear, transformers, power monitoring, energy management systems — Hannover Messe offers unmatched scale and international reach.

Exhibitor Strategies for Data Center Shows in 2026

  1. Lead with capacity and lead time. In a supply-constrained market, buyers care about availability as much as specifications. If you can deliver faster than competitors, make that the centerpiece of your booth messaging. Production capacity, manufacturing locations, and delivery timelines are competitive differentiators in 2026.
  2. Quantify the AI workload impact. Generic infrastructure messaging will get lost in the noise. Frame your solution specifically around AI workload requirements — power per rack, cooling capacity per kilowatt, bandwidth per GPU cluster. Buyers are making decisions based on AI deployment timelines, and your product needs to speak that language.
  3. Address sustainability without sacrificing performance. European data center operators face increasing regulatory pressure around energy efficiency and carbon emissions. Exhibitors who can demonstrate both high performance and environmental responsibility — through efficient cooling, renewable energy integration, or waste heat recovery — will resonate with buyers navigating dual mandates.
  4. Bring your engineering team. Data center infrastructure purchases are technically complex, and buying decisions involve deep engineering evaluation. Staff your booth with technical experts who can engage in detailed discussions about integration, compatibility, and performance under real-world conditions. Marketing presentations will not close deals at these shows — technical credibility will.

The Bigger Picture: A Generational Infrastructure Buildout

Vertiv's results do not exist in isolation. Schneider Electric reported record data center orders. Eaton's electrical segment delivered double-digit growth driven by data center demand. Nvidia's data center revenue continues to set records quarter after quarter, and every GPU shipped requires infrastructure to power it, cool it, and connect it. The consulting firm McKinsey estimates that global data center capital expenditure will exceed $300 billion annually by 2028, up from roughly $200 billion in 2024.

This is not a bubble. It is a generational infrastructure buildout driven by the industrialization of artificial intelligence. Just as the buildout of railroads, highways, and telecommunications networks created decades-long investment cycles, the AI infrastructure buildout is creating sustained demand for physical infrastructure that will persist well into the next decade.

For trade show exhibitors in this space, the implication is strategic: invest in your show presence now. Secure premium booth locations for 2026 and 2027. Build relationships with show organizers. Develop the technical content and demonstration capabilities that data center buyers demand. The companies that establish themselves as trusted infrastructure partners during this buildout phase will benefit from those relationships for years to come.
