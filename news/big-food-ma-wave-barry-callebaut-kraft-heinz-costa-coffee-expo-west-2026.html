Big Food M&A Wave: Barry Callebaut, Kraft Heinz, and Costa Coffee Deals Reshape Expo West 2026

ShowFloorTips Research Team February 12, 2026 14 min read
Share:

The first weeks of 2026 have delivered a cascade of M&A activity across the global food and beverage sector that is fundamentally altering the competitive landscape heading into the year’s most important trade shows. Barry Callebaut is spinning off its cocoa business to sharpen its focus on chocolate and compound coatings. Coca-Cola is divesting Costa Coffee, the UK-headquartered chain it acquired for $5.1 billion in 2019. Nestlé is offloading Blue Bottle Coffee as part of a broader portfolio simplification. And Kraft Heinz is separating its higher-growth sauces and condiments division from its legacy grocery brands. Together, these moves—along with a surge in better-for-you deal activity that has reached its highest percentage of total food M&A since 2019—are redrawing the exhibitor map for Natural Products Expo West 2026, the NRA Show, SIAL, and every food industry event on the 2026 calendar.

For trade show professionals, exhibitors, and attendees, this is not abstract Wall Street maneuvering. Each transaction reshapes which brands will be on the show floor, how booth investments get allocated, what categories receive the most attention from buyers, and which emerging companies suddenly have the capital and strategic freedom to make aggressive plays for shelf space and foodservice distribution.

Barry Callebaut Spins Off Cocoa: What It Means for Ingredient Exhibitors

Barry Callebaut’s decision to spin off its cocoa sourcing and processing operations into a standalone entity is arguably the most structurally significant move in the ingredient supply chain this year. For decades, the Zurich-based company has operated as a vertically integrated giant, controlling the journey from cocoa bean to finished chocolate product. That integration gave Barry Callebaut enormous pricing power and supply security, but it also tied up capital in a commodity business subject to extreme volatility—cocoa futures have swung wildly in recent years due to West African crop disruptions, climate-related yield declines, and regulatory uncertainty around deforestation compliance in the EU.

By separating cocoa operations, Barry Callebaut is signaling to the market—and to the trade show floor—that its future identity is as a specialty chocolate and compound coatings innovator, not a commodity processor. The spin-off entity will need to establish its own commercial relationships, build its own brand with food manufacturers, and likely exhibit independently at ingredient-focused events.

Expo West Implications

At Natural Products Expo West 2026 in Anaheim, the Barry Callebaut restructuring will reverberate through the specialty chocolate and confectionery aisles. The company has been a significant ingredient supplier to natural and organic chocolate brands—many of which rely on Barry Callebaut for sustainably sourced cocoa and custom chocolate formulations. With the parent company now focused purely on value-added chocolate innovation, exhibiting brands should expect a more aggressive push for co-branding partnerships, exclusive formulation collaborations, and premium ingredient positioning from the newly streamlined Barry Callebaut.

Meanwhile, the spun-off cocoa entity will need to prove its standalone viability. Expect it to increase its trade show presence at events like SIAL and Gulfood, courting manufacturers who previously accessed cocoa through the integrated Barry Callebaut relationship. For smaller cocoa suppliers, this creates a rare competitive opening—a moment when the dominant player is distracted by organizational restructuring and procurement teams at major food companies are actively evaluating alternatives.

Key Takeaway for Exhibitors

If you are an ingredient exhibitor at Expo West or SIAL, the Barry Callebaut spin-off is your signal to have cocoa and chocolate sourcing conversations that would have been impossible six months ago. Procurement teams are revisiting supplier relationships, and your booth is where those new conversations start.

Coca-Cola Sells Costa Coffee: The Beverage Portfolio Reset

Coca-Cola’s decision to divest Costa Coffee represents a striking admission that the 2019 acquisition—made for $5.1 billion to give Coke a global hot-beverage platform—did not deliver the strategic synergies the company projected. Costa’s roughly 4,000 stores and vending operations gave Coca-Cola a presence in the coffee retail channel, but integrating a brick-and-mortar hospitality business into a distribution-and-marketing juggernaut proved more complex than anticipated. Store-level economics, barista labor management, and real estate lease obligations are fundamentally different from the asset-light model that defines Coca-Cola’s core bottling and distribution system.

For the trade show circuit, the Costa divestiture raises immediate questions about booth presence and sponsorship at beverage-focused events. Costa has been a visible exhibitor at foodservice trade shows, particularly the NRA Show and regional hospitality events in Europe and the Middle East. Under new ownership—whether a private equity buyer, a hospitality conglomerate, or a management-led buyout—Costa’s trade show strategy will likely shift. New owners typically invest heavily in visibility during their first year of ownership to signal continuity to customers and partners, making the 2026 show season a particularly important one for the Costa brand.

Parallel Move: Nestlé Exits Blue Bottle

Nestlé’s simultaneous divestiture of Blue Bottle Coffee amplifies the signal. The Swiss food giant acquired a majority stake in the premium coffee brand in 2017, betting that Blue Bottle’s artisanal positioning and direct-to-consumer model would complement Nespresso and Nescafé. Instead, Blue Bottle remained a niche player with high operating costs and limited scalability within Nestlé’s massive distribution infrastructure. The sale acknowledges that premium, craft-positioned coffee brands may perform better under ownership structures that prize brand authenticity over global scale.

For exhibitors in the specialty coffee space, both divestitures create a momentary vacuum at the top of the market. When two of the world’s largest beverage companies simultaneously retreat from premium coffee retail, it signals an opportunity for independent specialty coffee brands, emerging roasters, and coffee technology companies to claim more show floor attention. At Expo West, where specialty coffee and functional beverages are major draws, expect the Costa and Blue Bottle news to fuel conversations about whether the future of premium coffee belongs to small-scale operators rather than multinational parents.

Kraft Heinz Separates Sauces from Grocery: Two Companies, Two Show Strategies

Kraft Heinz’s announcement that it will separate its higher-growth sauces, condiments, and spreads business from its legacy grocery portfolio—which includes macaroni and cheese, processed meats, and shelf-stable meals—is the clearest example yet of the conglomerate disaggregation trend reshaping Big Food. The logic is straightforward: sauces and condiments have demonstrated stronger volume growth, better margin profiles, and more innovation potential than the mature, private-label-pressured grocery categories. By creating two distinct entities, Kraft Heinz aims to give each business the capital allocation, management focus, and investor narrative it deserves.

The trade show implications are significant. A unified Kraft Heinz presence at the NRA Show, for example, has historically spanned everything from Heinz ketchup packets to Oscar Mayer deli meats to Philadelphia cream cheese. Post-separation, the sauces company will likely invest disproportionately in foodservice-focused events where condiment innovation—limited-edition flavors, clean-label reformulations, globally inspired varieties—resonates with restaurant operators and foodservice distributors. The grocery company, meanwhile, may reduce its presence at premium-positioned shows like Expo West and concentrate on mass-market retail events and private-label conferences.

For competitors and emerging brands, the Kraft Heinz split creates openings in both channels. In sauces, the separated entity will be aggressively courting retailer and foodservice partnerships, potentially displacing smaller brands from key demo slots and sampling opportunities. But in the grocery segment, the legacy company’s reduced innovation budget could open doors for better-for-you alternatives that have been gaining shelf space incrementally and can now position themselves as the growth narrative that the legacy brands no longer deliver.

Better-for-You Deals Surge to Highest Share Since 2019

Beneath the headline transactions, a deeper structural shift is underway in food M&A: better-for-you brands are commanding the highest percentage of total food and beverage deal activity since 2019. This encompasses acquisitions, minority investments, and strategic partnerships targeting organic, plant-based, functional, low-sugar, allergen-free, and clean-label products.

The drivers are clear. Consumer demand for healthier options continues to accelerate. Retailers are dedicating more shelf space to better-for-you categories. And the financial returns have been proven—brands like Siete Foods (acquired by PepsiCo for $1.2 billion in 2024), RXBAR, and other better-for-you success stories have demonstrated that premium-priced health brands can achieve exit valuations that justify the investment.

For trade show exhibitors, the better-for-you M&A surge has three immediate consequences:

  • More well-capitalized emerging brands on the show floor. Companies that have recently received growth equity or strategic investment will use Expo West and similar events as their coming-out parties, with larger booths, more aggressive sampling programs, and higher-profile launch events.
  • Increased buyer sophistication. The investors and corporate development teams scouting Expo West aisles are more numerous and more knowledgeable than ever. They have specific acquisition criteria, and they are using trade shows as deal-sourcing platforms. If you are an emerging brand exhibiting at Expo West, the buyer walking past your booth may be holding a checkbook, not just a shopping bag.
  • Heightened competitive intensity. When capital floods into a category, the number of brands competing for attention, shelf space, and foodservice distribution contracts increases dramatically. Trade shows become critical differentiators—the brands that execute their show strategy flawlessly will attract the partnerships and investment that fuel the next stage of growth.

What These Deals Mean for Your Expo West 2026 Strategy

For Ingredient Suppliers

The Barry Callebaut spin-off and the broader better-for-you investment wave mean that ingredient suppliers have a rare opportunity to win new accounts. Procurement teams at major food companies are in flux. Established relationships are being reassessed as corporate parents change or business units are restructured. Your booth at Expo West should be staffed with technical experts who can have detailed formulation conversations—not just sales reps handing out samples. Bring case studies showing how your ingredients have enabled successful product launches in the better-for-you space, and be prepared to discuss supply chain resilience, sustainability certifications, and cost structures in granular detail.

For Emerging Brands

If you are a better-for-you brand exhibiting at Expo West 2026, understand that the buyer population on the show floor includes a disproportionate number of M&A scouts, venture investors, and corporate development executives. Your booth is not just a sales tool; it is a pitch deck in physical form. Ensure your brand story is clear, your unit economics are strong, your supply chain is scalable, and your team can articulate a credible path to $50 million or $100 million in revenue. The deals being announced today started as conversations on trade show floors 12 to 24 months ago.

For Foodservice Operators

The Kraft Heinz separation and the Costa Coffee divestiture are directly relevant to anyone attending the NRA Show in May. New corporate structures mean new commercial terms, new distribution arrangements, and potentially new product portfolios. If your restaurant or foodservice operation relies on Kraft Heinz condiments, Costa Coffee products, or any brand touched by these M&A transactions, the NRA Show is your opportunity to meet the new decision-makers, renegotiate terms, and evaluate alternatives while the competitive landscape is in flux.

The Bottom Line: M&A Reshapes the Show Floor Before It Reshapes the Shelf

The Barry Callebaut cocoa spin-off, the Coca-Cola divestiture of Costa Coffee, Nestlé’s exit from Blue Bottle, and the Kraft Heinz portfolio separation are not isolated events. They are manifestations of a structural rebalancing across Big Food—a recognition that the conglomerate model built for the 20th century does not deliver the focus, agility, or growth narratives that investors, retailers, and consumers demand in 2026.

For the trade show industry, these deals are a leading indicator. Before they reshape grocery shelves and restaurant menus, they reshape the show floor. Booth footprints change. Exhibitor lists evolve. Buyer agendas shift. The brands and categories that attract the most foot traffic, the most serious conversations, and the most follow-up meetings after the show are the ones that have read the M&A landscape correctly and positioned themselves accordingly.

The better-for-you surge to its highest deal share since 2019 is the exclamation point. Capital is flowing into the categories that Expo West was built to showcase. For exhibitors in those categories, the 2026 show season is not just another year on the calendar. It is the moment when the trade show floor becomes the most important deal-sourcing platform in the food industry—and the exhibitors who recognize that reality will be the ones writing the next round of headlines.
