World of Concrete 2026 vs KBIS 2026: Construction and Design Trade Shows Compared

ShowFloorTips · February 11, 2026

The building industry is vast, and no single trade show can capture all of it. Two of the most important events on the North American construction and design calendar occupy very different positions in that ecosystem: World of Concrete in Las Vegas and the Kitchen & Bath Industry Show (KBIS) in Orlando. They take place less than a month apart in early 2026, they both serve professionals who build and renovate the spaces where people live and work, and they both attract tens of thousands of attendees to massive convention centers. But that is roughly where the similarities end.

World of Concrete is the definitive event for the concrete, masonry, and heavy construction industries. It is loud, dusty, and industrial. KBIS is the design-forward centerpiece of Design & Construction Week, showcasing the latest in kitchen and bath fixtures, appliances, surfaces, and smart home technology. It is polished, aspirational, and style-driven. If you are a professional in the broader building and construction space trying to decide which event to attend in early 2026, or whether to attend both, this comprehensive comparison will give you everything you need to make an informed decision.

Whether you are a concrete contractor evaluating new equipment, a kitchen designer scouting product lines, a manufacturer considering where to exhibit, or a general contractor who touches both sides of the building industry, understanding what each event delivers in terms of scale, exhibitors, education, networking, and cost will help you allocate your time and budget where they will generate the highest return.

World of Concrete 2026: The Profile

World of Concrete 2026 takes place January 19 through 22, 2026, at the Las Vegas Convention Center. Now in its 51st year, World of Concrete has established itself as the largest annual international event dedicated to the commercial and residential concrete construction industries. It is the show where contractors, producers, and suppliers come together to see the newest equipment, learn the latest techniques, and do the deals that shape the year ahead.

The numbers tell a story of industrial scale. World of Concrete 2026 features more than 1,500 exhibitors spread across more than 745,000 square feet of exhibit space, encompassing both the massive indoor halls of the Las Vegas Convention Center and the sprawling outdoor demonstration areas that make this show unlike any other in the building industry. The outdoor lots are where World of Concrete truly differentiates itself: heavy equipment manufacturers fire up concrete pumps, excavators, and mixers for live demonstrations that you simply cannot replicate in a carpeted convention hall.

745,000+
Square feet of indoor and outdoor exhibit space at World of Concrete 2026 in Las Vegas

Attendance at World of Concrete 2026 is projected to reach between 58,000 and 60,000 registered professionals. While that number may seem modest compared to mega-events like CES or CONEXPO-CON/AGG, the quality of the World of Concrete audience is exceptionally high. Attendees are overwhelmingly decision-makers: concrete contractors, construction company owners, ready-mix producers, concrete pumping operators, masonry contractors, decorative concrete specialists, and construction managers. These are people who buy equipment, hire subcontractors, and specify materials. For exhibitors, the lead quality at World of Concrete is among the highest of any construction industry trade show.

The show floor is organized into clearly defined product categories. Equipment and tools dominate the central halls, with everything from hand tools and diamond blades to truck-mounted concrete pumps and laser-guided screeds. Adjacent zones cover concrete repair and restoration products, decorative concrete materials and techniques, masonry products and accessories, waterproofing systems, and construction technology including GPS grading systems, drones, and software platforms for concrete producers and contractors.

Education at World of Concrete

World of Concrete's education program is one of the most respected in the construction industry. The 2026 edition features more than 150 sessions covering topics from the highly technical to the business-critical. More than 60 new topics have been added for 2026, reflecting the rapid pace of change in concrete technology, sustainability requirements, and construction business management. Sessions are led by a combination of university researchers, industry veterans, and working contractors who bring real-world credibility that resonates with the audience.

The education tracks span concrete fundamentals, advanced techniques for decorative and architectural concrete, business management for contractors, safety and compliance, and emerging technology. For hands-on learners, World of Concrete offers extensive workshop programs where attendees can practice techniques under expert supervision. This is the kind of tactile, skills-based training that simply cannot be replicated through online courses or webinars, and it is one of the most compelling reasons to attend the show in person.

One of the most unique educational experiences at World of Concrete 2026 is the series of Hoover Dam tours. These behind-the-scenes tours of one of the most iconic concrete structures ever built give attendees a firsthand look at the engineering and construction techniques that went into creating a Depression-era marvel that remains fully operational nearly a century later. The tours sell out quickly and represent a once-in-a-career opportunity for many concrete professionals.

Signature Events and Competitions

World of Concrete is famous for its competitions, which have become cultural touchstones within the industry. The SPEC MIX BRICKLAYER 500 is the Super Bowl of masonry competitions, pitting the best bricklayers in North America against each other in a timed event where speed, precision, and craftsmanship all matter. With $125,000 in total prizes, the BRICKLAYER 500 draws enormous crowds and generates genuine excitement on the show floor. Masonry Madness expands on this competitive spirit with additional events that showcase masonry skills across different disciplines.

Decorative Concrete LIVE! is another must-see feature, presenting live demonstrations of stamping, staining, polishing, and overlay techniques that have transformed decorative concrete from a niche specialty into one of the fastest-growing segments of the construction industry. These demonstrations are led by recognized artisans who create finished pieces in real time, drawing standing-room-only crowds of contractors looking to add decorative concrete services to their businesses.

KBIS 2026: The Profile

KBIS 2026 takes place February 17 through 19, 2026, at the Orange County Convention Center in Orlando, Florida. KBIS is the annual gathering of the kitchen and bath industry, produced by the National Kitchen & Bath Association (NKBA). It is the place where designers, dealers, architects, builders, and manufacturers converge to see the products, technologies, and design trends that will define kitchens and bathrooms for the coming years.

KBIS 2026 marks the 13th year of co-location with the International Builders' Show (IBS) as part of Design & Construction Week (DCW), one of the largest annual gatherings of residential construction and design professionals in the world. The co-location is strategically important: a single registration grants access to both KBIS and IBS, creating a combined event that features more than 2,350 exhibitors, attracts over 115,000 attendees, and occupies approximately 1.2 million square feet of exhibit space. Within that combined footprint, KBIS itself accounts for more than 650 exhibitors and roughly 500,000 square feet of dedicated show floor.

The KBIS show floor is a carefully curated showcase of the latest in residential design and technology. Major appliance manufacturers debut new product lines. Fixture companies display next-generation faucets, sinks, and showerheads. Cabinetry brands present new door styles, finishes, and organizational systems. Surface material companies, from quartz and porcelain to natural stone and solid surface, compete for the attention of the designers and dealers who specify their products on residential projects across the country.

Unlike World of Concrete, where the emphasis is on industrial capability and raw performance, KBIS is an aesthetic experience. Exhibitor booths at KBIS are designed to inspire, featuring complete kitchen and bathroom vignettes that showcase products in context. The visual quality of the show floor is exceptionally high, reflecting an industry where design sensibility is as important as functional performance. Walking the KBIS show floor is essentially a guided tour through the future of residential design.

Keynote and Signature Events at KBIS

KBIS 2026 features NFL Hall of Famer and real estate entrepreneur Emmitt Smith as the keynote speaker. Smith's involvement with residential construction and design through his post-football career in real estate development makes him a fitting choice for an audience that straddles the worlds of construction and design. His keynote is expected to draw a capacity crowd and set the tone for a show that increasingly positions itself at the intersection of design, technology, and lifestyle.

The DesignBites program is one of KBIS's most popular features, giving exhibitors a fast-paced, TED-Talk-style platform to present their most innovative products to a live audience of designers, media, and buyers. Each presenter gets just a few minutes to make their case, creating an energetic and efficient way for attendees to discover new products without spending hours walking the show floor. DesignBites has become a launchpad for products that go on to win industry awards and capture significant market share.

The Wine & Design panels combine product education with a more relaxed networking atmosphere, offering attendees the chance to engage with industry thought leaders and fellow professionals in a setting that encourages conversation. The Best of KBIS Awards recognize the most innovative and well-designed products exhibited at the show, with categories covering kitchen, bath, smart home, and sustainability. Winning a Best of KBIS Award is one of the most coveted distinctions in the kitchen and bath industry, providing winners with significant marketing leverage and media exposure.

Innovation Hour is a newer addition to the KBIS programming, creating a dedicated window for exhibitors to demonstrate cutting-edge technology and products that represent the leading edge of kitchen and bath innovation. From AI-powered kitchen appliances to touchless bathroom fixtures and sustainable material innovations, Innovation Hour concentrates the show's most forward-looking content into a focused session that draws an audience of early adopters and trend-seekers.

Scale and Scope: Two Very Different Footprints

Scale is one of the most immediately obvious differences between these two events, and understanding it requires looking beyond the raw numbers. World of Concrete's 745,000-plus square feet of exhibit space includes both the expansive indoor halls of the Las Vegas Convention Center and the outdoor demonstration areas where heavy equipment takes center stage. The outdoor lots are critical to the World of Concrete experience: this is where you will see concrete pumps reaching 60 meters into the sky, excavators tearing through demolition simulations, and mixer trucks navigating obstacle courses designed to showcase maneuverability and capacity. No indoor-only event can replicate this kind of visceral, large-scale product demonstration.

KBIS, by contrast, is an entirely indoor affair, but its scale is amplified enormously by the co-location with IBS at Design & Construction Week. While KBIS itself occupies approximately 500,000 square feet with 650-plus exhibitors, attendees with DCW registration have access to the combined 1.2 million square feet and 2,350-plus exhibitors spanning both shows. This co-location is a strategic masterstroke: a kitchen designer attending KBIS can walk across the hall and explore IBS exhibitors selling windows, structural products, home automation systems, and building envelope solutions, creating a one-stop experience for anyone involved in residential construction and renovation.

1,500+
Exhibitors at World of Concrete 2026, including leading brands in concrete, masonry, and construction equipment

In terms of raw attendance, Design & Construction Week's combined 115,000-plus attendees significantly outpaces World of Concrete's 58,000 to 60,000. However, raw attendance numbers can be misleading. World of Concrete's audience is highly concentrated in the concrete and masonry industries, meaning that exhibitors selling to those specific markets will find a denser concentration of their target buyers per square foot of show floor. KBIS's audience, while larger in combined terms, is more dispersed across the broader residential construction and design ecosystem.

For exhibitors deciding between the two shows, the question of scale comes down to depth versus breadth. World of Concrete offers deep penetration into a narrowly defined market. KBIS, especially within the broader DCW framework, offers broader reach across the residential building and design value chain. Neither approach is inherently superior; the right choice depends on what you sell and to whom you sell it.

Exhibitor Profiles: Who Shows Up and Why

The exhibitor base at World of Concrete reads like a who's who of the concrete and construction equipment industries. Major equipment manufacturers such as those producing concrete pumps, batching plants, finishing machines, saws, drills, and forming systems anchor the show floor. Alongside them are suppliers of concrete admixtures, fiber reinforcement, curing compounds, sealers, and repair products. The decorative concrete segment has grown significantly in recent years, bringing in manufacturers of stamps, stains, dyes, overlays, and polishing equipment. Tool manufacturers, safety equipment suppliers, and construction technology companies round out the exhibitor mix.

What makes World of Concrete's exhibitor base unique is the heavy emphasis on working demonstrations. This is not a show where products sit behind glass cases. Exhibitors bring their equipment to life, pouring concrete, laying brick, cutting and polishing surfaces, and demonstrating the real-world performance of their products under conditions that approximate actual jobsite use. For buyers, this makes product evaluation dramatically more effective than simply reading specifications or watching marketing videos.

KBIS exhibitors, by contrast, come from the design-driven side of the building industry. The show floor features the world's leading kitchen appliance brands, from refrigeration and cooking to dishwashing and ventilation. Plumbing fixture manufacturers display the latest faucets, sinks, and shower systems. Cabinetry companies present new styles, finishes, and storage solutions. Surface material manufacturers, including makers of quartz, porcelain, natural stone, and engineered surfaces, compete for the attention of designers and dealers in elaborately designed booth environments that function as aspirational showrooms.

The exhibitor dynamic at KBIS also benefits from the IBS co-location. Attendees who register for DCW can visit IBS exhibitors selling structural products, insulation, windows and doors, roofing, siding, and home technology systems. This creates cross-selling opportunities that are unique to Design & Construction Week: a builder visiting IBS to source windows might discover a new cabinet line at KBIS, or a kitchen designer attending KBIS might find a structural panel system at IBS that informs their next renovation project.

Networking Opportunities: Hard Hats vs Design Minds

The networking culture at these two events could not be more different, and understanding those differences is critical to maximizing your return on attendance.

World of Concrete networking is direct, practical, and rooted in the culture of the construction trades. Conversations happen at equipment demonstrations, in education session hallways, and at the various competitions and live events that draw crowds throughout the show. The attendee base is predominantly composed of business owners, project managers, and senior estimators who are comfortable making purchasing decisions quickly. Deals at World of Concrete often progress from initial conversation to signed purchase order within the same show, particularly for equipment and materials with well-established performance benchmarks.

The competitions at World of Concrete, particularly the SPEC MIX BRICKLAYER 500, serve a networking function that goes beyond entertainment. These events draw crowds of contractors, manufacturers, and industry leaders into a shared space with a shared focus, creating natural conversation starters and relationship-building opportunities. The after-hours scene in Las Vegas needs no introduction: the city's dining, entertainment, and hospitality infrastructure provides unlimited options for client entertainment and relationship building.

Key TakeawayWorld of Concrete networking is transaction-oriented and trades-focused, with deals often closing on the show floor. KBIS networking is relationship-driven and design-focused, with connections leading to specification and product partnerships that develop over time. Match your networking style to the event that aligns with your sales cycle.

KBIS networking is more design-community oriented. The show attracts kitchen and bath designers, interior designers, architects, showroom owners, and dealers whose purchasing decisions are influenced by aesthetics, brand alignment, and design philosophy as much as by price and performance specifications. The Wine & Design panels create structured networking opportunities in a more relaxed, social atmosphere. The NKBA's industry connections provide a built-in professional network that facilitates introductions and relationship-building throughout the event.

The Emmitt Smith keynote at KBIS 2026 adds a high-profile networking anchor to the event. Keynote events at trade shows serve as gathering points where attendees from across the show come together, creating pre-event and post-event networking opportunities that would not otherwise exist. Smith's dual credibility as a sports icon and real estate entrepreneur makes him a conversation topic that bridges the gap between the design-focused KBIS audience and the builder-focused IBS audience.

Side Events and After-Hours Comparison

Las Vegas and Orlando offer very different after-hours experiences, and for many attendees, the host city's entertainment and dining options are a meaningful factor in event selection. Las Vegas is Las Vegas: world-class restaurants, entertainment options at every price point, and a hospitality infrastructure built to accommodate the convention industry at massive scale. World of Concrete's January timing means pleasant weather, lower hotel rates compared to peak convention season, and a city that is fully geared up to welcome trade show attendees.

Orlando offers a more family-friendly and moderate after-hours experience. The Orange County Convention Center is surrounded by the International Drive corridor with its abundance of restaurants, hotels, and entertainment options. For attendees who prefer to combine their business trip with a family visit to central Florida's theme parks, Orlando's location is a significant advantage. Design & Construction Week's February timing means warm weather, which is a welcome change for attendees traveling from northern climates in the middle of winter.

Education Programs: Skills vs Design Thinking

Education is a core pillar of both events, but the content, format, and audience for each show's programming could not be more different.

World of Concrete's education program is deep, technical, and skills-based. With more than 150 sessions and over 60 new topics in 2026, the program covers concrete technology at a level of detail that serves both newcomers and 30-year veterans. Session topics range from concrete mix design and admixture chemistry to decorative concrete techniques, concrete repair methodologies, and the latest in construction safety standards. The hands-on workshops are a particular strength: attendees can learn to stamp concrete, polish floors, apply waterproofing systems, and operate new equipment with direct guidance from expert instructors.

The education pricing at World of Concrete reflects the depth of the content. Exhibits-only registration ranges from $105 to $135, providing access to the show floor, outdoor demonstrations, and the competition events. Education packages range from $125 to $305, depending on the number and type of sessions selected. For many attendees, the education program alone justifies the cost of attending the show, particularly for contractors seeking continuing education credits or looking to add new services to their businesses.

KBIS's educational programming takes a different approach, emphasizing design trends, business strategy, and product innovation rather than hands-on technical training. Continuing Education Credits

Both events offer continuing education units (CEUs) that are recognized by relevant industry certifications. World of Concrete sessions can contribute to various contractor licensing requirements, particularly in jurisdictions that mandate continuing education for concrete and masonry work. KBIS sessions contribute to NKBA certification maintenance and are recognized by various design and architectural credentialing bodies. For professionals who need CEUs to maintain their licenses or certifications, both events provide efficient opportunities to earn credits while simultaneously learning valuable new skills and knowledge.

Cost Comparison: What Will You Actually Spend?

The total cost of attending either event involves registration, travel, accommodation, meals, and incidental expenses. The registration cost differential between the two shows is one of the most significant practical differences.

World of Concrete registration is straightforward and paid. Exhibits-only passes range from $105 to $135, with early registration discounts available for those who commit well in advance. Education packages layer additional costs on top: individual seminar tickets, multi-session bundles, and all-access education passes range from $125 to $305. For a contractor who wants full access to both the show floor and the education program, total registration cost will typically fall in the $250 to $400 range, depending on the specific sessions selected.

KBIS registration offers a compelling value proposition, particularly for NKBA members. Members who register early, starting in September, can obtain complimentary registration that includes access to both KBIS and IBS show floors. After the early registration window, tiered pricing applies, but the base cost remains lower than World of Concrete's paid registration structure. For non-members and late registrants, pricing increases, but even at the highest tiers, KBIS registration is generally competitive with or less expensive than World of Concrete.

Travel and Accommodation Costs

Las Vegas and Orlando are both major convention cities with extensive hotel inventories, competitive airline service, and well-developed transportation infrastructure. However, there are meaningful cost differences between the two.

  • Airfare: Las Vegas and Orlando are both served by major airlines and low-cost carriers. Airfare costs are generally comparable from most U.S. cities, though Las Vegas tends to have slightly more competitive pricing due to the volume of leisure and convention traffic the city attracts year-round.
  • Hotels: World of Concrete's January dates in Las Vegas typically coincide with lower hotel rates compared to major convention weeks later in the year. A quality business hotel within reasonable distance of the LVCC can be found for $120 to $250 per night during WOC week. Orlando hotels during DCW in February are similarly priced, though the I-Drive corridor near the OCCC offers a wider range of budget options than the Las Vegas Strip.
  • Ground transportation: Las Vegas offers the advantage of a newly expanded convention center with improved monorail and shuttle access. Orlando's convention center is well-served by ride-hailing services and hotel shuttles, but many attendees find that renting a car provides more flexibility for navigating the sprawling I-Drive area.
  • Meals and entertainment: Las Vegas dining ranges from affordable casual options to world-class restaurants with premium pricing. Orlando offers generally lower meal costs, particularly for casual dining along International Drive. Both cities offer extensive options for client entertainment, though Las Vegas's hospitality infrastructure is more elaborate and more oriented toward business entertainment.
Key TakeawayWorld of Concrete has higher registration costs but potentially lower travel expenses due to Las Vegas's competitive hotel and flight pricing in January. KBIS offers lower or free registration for NKBA members but requires a separate assessment of Orlando travel costs. For budget-conscious attendees, KBIS at DCW often delivers more total show floor access per dollar spent.

The live demonstrations allow side-by-side comparison of competing products under realistic conditions.
  • Masonry contractors and suppliers: The masonry segment at World of Concrete has grown significantly, and the SPEC MIX BRICKLAYER 500 competition has become a centerpiece event that elevates the entire masonry community. If masonry is your business, WOC is your show.
  • Ready-mix producers and concrete pumping operators: WOC's outdoor demonstrations of mixers, pumps, and delivery equipment are unmatched at any other event. If you operate a fleet of concrete delivery or pumping equipment, the ability to see new models in action is worth the trip alone.

  • Construction equipment buyers: From hand tools to heavy machinery, WOC's show floor offers the broadest selection of concrete and masonry construction equipment available at any single event.
  • Construction technology companies: GPS-guided equipment, drone survey systems, concrete monitoring sensors, and fleet management software all have a growing presence at WOC, reflecting the industry's accelerating adoption of technology.

Industry Focus: Where Each Event Excels

The industry focus of each event reflects the fundamental division within the broader building industry between structural construction and finish-level design.

World of Concrete excels in everything related to the concrete and masonry trades. This includes not just the obvious categories like equipment and materials, but also the rapidly evolving areas of concrete sustainability, including low-carbon cement formulations and carbon capture in concrete production. The decorative concrete segment continues to grow as contractors recognize the profit margins and demand growth in stamped, stained, and polished concrete for both residential and commercial applications. Concrete repair and restoration is another growth area, driven by the aging infrastructure across North America and the increasing demand for building rehabilitation rather than demolition and new construction.

KBIS excels in the design-driven, consumer-facing side of the building industry. Kitchen design trends, from minimalist European aesthetics to bold color statements, are unveiled at KBIS months before they reach mainstream consumer awareness. Smart kitchen technology, including connected appliances, voice-activated fixtures, and integrated kitchen management systems, has a strong presence at KBIS that reflects the industry's movement toward technology-enhanced living spaces. The bathroom segment is equally dynamic, with wellness-oriented products like steam showers, soaking tubs, and chromotherapy lighting systems gaining significant floor space.

The Design & Construction Week Advantage

One of KBIS's most significant strategic advantages is its co-location with IBS at Design & Construction Week. This partnership, now in its 13th year, creates a combined event that is dramatically larger and more comprehensive than either show would be on its own. For exhibitors, the co-location means access to a combined audience of 115,000-plus attendees who represent the full spectrum of residential construction and design. For attendees, it means that a single trip to Orlando provides exposure to both the design-forward world of KBIS and the construction-focused world of IBS.

The practical benefits of the co-location are substantial. A single registration provides access to both show floors. Education programming from both KBIS and IBS is available to all DCW attendees. Networking events draw participants from both shows, creating cross-pollination between the design and construction communities that rarely happens at standalone events. For manufacturers whose products serve both audiences, such as window and door companies, flooring manufacturers, and home technology providers, DCW provides an unmatched platform for reaching both designers and builders in a single event.

World of Concrete, by contrast, operates as a standalone event. This has its own advantages: the show's identity is unambiguous, the audience is precisely targeted, and the entire event infrastructure is designed to serve a single industry. There is no dilution of focus, no confusion about which hall to visit, and no need to navigate between co-located shows. For exhibitors and attendees whose business is squarely within the concrete and masonry industries, WOC's standalone format is a feature, not a limitation.

Registration Deep Dive: Navigating the Pricing

Understanding the registration structures for both events requires attention to timing, membership, and the specific level of access you need.

World of Concrete Registration Tiers

World of Concrete offers several registration options. The Exhibits Only pass, priced between $105 and $135 depending on registration timing, provides access to the entire indoor and outdoor show floor, the competition events, and certain free education sessions and demonstrations. This is the right choice for attendees whose primary goal is to evaluate products, meet vendors, and attend the BRICKLAYER 500 and other competitions. For attendees who want to participate in the formal education program, individual seminar tickets and bundled education passes are available at prices ranging from $125 to $305. All-access packages that combine exhibits, education, and premium networking events are also available at higher price points.

KBIS Registration Tiers

KBIS registration is structured around NKBA membership and timing. NKBA members who register in September can secure complimentary access to both the KBIS show floor and the IBS show floor as part of Design & Construction Week. This is one of the best values in the trade show industry: free registration to a combined event that would cost hundreds of dollars at comparable shows. After the September early registration window, tiered pricing applies based on membership status and registration date. Non-members and late registrants pay progressively higher fees, but even at the highest tiers, the cost remains accessible for most professionals.

The NKBA membership itself costs money, of course, and should be factored into the total cost calculation for anyone considering the free registration path. However, NKBA membership provides year-round benefits including continuing education access, industry research, professional certifications, and networking resources that extend well beyond the annual show. For kitchen and bath professionals who are not already NKBA members, the combination of membership benefits and free KBIS registration makes joining the association a straightforward value proposition.

Technology and Innovation at Both Events

Both World of Concrete and KBIS are actively showcasing the technology-driven transformation of their respective industries, though the types of technology on display differ significantly.

At World of Concrete, construction technology is increasingly prominent. GPS-guided equipment, including laser screeds and grade control systems for earthmoving, represents a mature technology category that continues to evolve with improved accuracy and ease of use. Drone technology for site surveying, progress monitoring, and volumetric measurement has moved from novelty to standard practice, and WOC's exhibitors include leading drone hardware and software providers. Concrete monitoring sensors that track temperature, humidity, and strength development in real time are gaining traction among contractors and producers seeking to optimize curing schedules and reduce the need for destructive testing. Software platforms for ready-mix producers, including batching automation, fleet management, and quality control systems, occupy a growing section of the show floor.

At KBIS, technology takes a more consumer-facing and design-integrated form. Smart kitchen appliances that communicate with each other and with the homeowner's smartphone are a major category, with major appliance brands debuting new connected product lines at the show each year. Touchless and voice-activated plumbing fixtures have moved from high-end luxury to mainstream availability, and KBIS exhibitors showcase the latest advances in sensor technology and connectivity. Kitchen and bath design software, including 3D rendering and virtual reality tools that allow designers to create photorealistic visualizations for their clients, continues to advance rapidly. Sustainability technology, including water-saving fixtures, energy-efficient appliances, and materials made from recycled or renewable feedstocks, has become a major theme at KBIS, reflecting growing consumer demand for environmentally responsible design.

KBIS is about making homes smarter, more beautiful, and more sustainable. If you care about where technology is taking the building industry, you need both perspectives."

Sustainability and Green Building

Sustainability has become a significant theme at both events, reflecting the building industry's broader movement toward reduced environmental impact and improved energy efficiency.

World of Concrete's sustainability programming addresses the concrete industry's outsized carbon footprint. Cement production accounts for approximately 8% of global CO2 emissions, making concrete one of the most carbon-intensive materials in the built environment. WOC exhibitors and education sessions increasingly focus on solutions: supplementary cementite materials that reduce cement content, carbon capture technologies that sequester CO2 in concrete products, and mix design optimization techniques that achieve required performance with lower environmental impact. For concrete producers and contractors facing growing pressure from clients, regulators, and investors to reduce their carbon footprint, WOC's sustainability content is directly actionable.

KBIS's sustainability focus is more consumer-facing, centering on water conservation, energy efficiency, and sustainable materials. WaterSense-certified fixtures, ENERGY STAR-rated appliances, and products made from recycled, reclaimed, or rapidly renewable materials are prominently featured on the show floor. The Best of KBIS Awards include a sustainability category that recognizes products demonstrating exceptional environmental performance. For designers and dealers who serve environmentally conscious clients, KBIS provides a comprehensive survey of the sustainable products available in the kitchen and bath categories.

The Verdict: Which Event Should You Choose?

Choose World of Concrete 2026 If:

  • Your business is in concrete construction, whether residential, commercial, or infrastructure, and you need to evaluate equipment, materials, and techniques in person with live demonstrations
  • You are a masonry contractor or supplier who wants to participate in the industry's premier networking and competition events, including the SPEC MIX BRICKLAYER 500
  • You need hands-on, skills-based education that cannot be replicated through online learning, particularly in decorative concrete, concrete repair, and new construction techniques
  • You want to see heavy equipment in action, not just in a catalog, and make purchasing decisions based on real-world performance demonstrations
  • You are looking to add decorative concrete services to your business and want to learn from the best artisans in the industry at Decorative Concrete LIVE!
  • You are in construction technology and want to showcase or evaluate GPS systems, drones, sensors, and software for the concrete and masonry markets
  • You want to take advantage of WOC's unique educational experiences, including the Hoover Dam tours

Choose KBIS 2026 If:

  • Your business is in kitchen and bath design, sales, or installation, and you need to stay current on the latest products, trends, and design directions from the world's leading manufacturers
  • You are a remodeling contractor who specializes in kitchen and bath renovations and wants direct access to appliance, fixture, and surface material manufacturers
  • You are an NKBA member seeking to maximize the value of your membership with free registration, CEU opportunities, and professional networking at the industry's flagship event
  • You want the combined value of Design & Construction Week, with access to both KBIS and IBS on a single registration, covering the full spectrum of residential construction and design
  • You are an appliance or fixture dealer or showroom owner who needs to evaluate new product lines, negotiate dealer agreements, and preview the trends that will drive consumer demand
  • You are interested in smart home technology, sustainable design, and the intersection of technology and residential living spaces
  • You want to attend the Emmitt Smith keynote and participate in high-profile events like DesignBites and the Best of KBIS Awards

Attend Both If:

The four-week gap between World of Concrete (January 19-22) and KBIS (February 17-19) makes attending both events logistically feasible for professionals who straddle the construction and design segments of the building industry. General contractors who handle both structural work and finish-level design selections, manufacturers whose products serve both the construction and design markets, and building industry consultants who advise clients across the full project lifecycle will find value in both events. The cost of attending both, including registration, travel, and accommodation for two separate trips, is significant but may be justified by the breadth of market intelligence and networking opportunities that the combination provides.

The Bottom Line

World of Concrete 2026 and KBIS 2026 are not competitors. They serve different segments of the building industry, attract different audiences, and deliver different types of value. World of Concrete is the essential event for concrete, masonry, and heavy construction professionals who need to see products perform, learn hands-on techniques, and connect with the trades community that builds the structural foundation of every project. KBIS is the essential event for kitchen and bath design and construction professionals who need to discover products, stay ahead of design trends, and participate in the industry's premier professional community.

The question is not which event is better. The question is which event is right for your specific role, market, and professional development needs in 2026. For many professionals in the broader building industry, the answer will be clear after reviewing the comparison above. For those who operate at the intersection of construction and design, the answer might be both.
