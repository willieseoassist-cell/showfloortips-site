Nebius Acquires Tavily to Supercharge AI Agent Tooling -- And Trade Show Floors Will Never Be the Same

A European cloud upstart just bought the search API that powers thousands of AI agents. As agentic AI proliferates across enterprise software, trade show exhibitors face a fundamental shift in how they demo, sell, and capture leads.

Photo via Unsplash

The acquisition was quiet. The implications are seismic. Nebius, the European AI cloud provider spun out of Yandex and now publicly traded on Nasdaq, has acquired Tavily, the Israeli startup behind the real-time search API that has become the backbone of thousands of AI agent applications worldwide. The deal gives Nebius a critical piece of the agentic AI stack -- the ability for AI agents to search the live web, retrieve current information, and act on it autonomously -- and it signals that the infrastructure layer powering AI agents has matured from experimental tooling to acquisition-worthy enterprise software. For trade show exhibitors, the ripple effects are already arriving on the show floor.

Tavily's search API is not a household name, but it is embedded in an extraordinary number of AI agent frameworks. LangChain, CrewAI, AutoGPT, and dozens of other agentic platforms use Tavily as their default search tool, enabling AI agents to retrieve real-time data from the web as part of multi-step reasoning chains. When an AI agent needs to check a competitor's latest pricing, verify a regulatory requirement, or pull current market data before making a recommendation, Tavily is often the engine under the hood. Nebius acquiring this capability means a single cloud provider now controls both the GPU infrastructure that runs the AI models and the search layer that connects them to live information.

AI Agents Are Already on the Show Floor

Walk the aisles of any major tech trade show in 2026 and you will encounter AI agents everywhere -- not as futuristic concepts in keynote presentations but as functioning software running live demonstrations in exhibitor booths. At SaaStr Annual, customer success platforms are showing AI agents that autonomously handle support ticket triage, escalation, and resolution. At HIMSS, healthcare IT vendors are demonstrating agents that pre-authorize insurance claims while the patient is still in the exam room. At Shoptalk, retail technology companies are showcasing agents that negotiate with suppliers, adjust pricing in real time, and reorder inventory without human intervention.

The Nebius-Tavily acquisition accelerates this trend by lowering the barrier to building production-grade AI agents. When the search infrastructure, GPU compute, and model hosting all come from a single provider with a unified API, the development time for a functional AI agent drops from months to weeks. That means more exhibitors will arrive at trade shows with working agent demos, and the competitive bar for "impressive AI demonstration" rises across the board.

"We are entering the era where an AI agent at a trade show booth can answer attendee questions more accurately than most booth staff. The companies that embrace this will dominate lead capture. The ones that resist will wonder why their badge scans are down." -- Jared Palmer, AI developer advocate

The New Trade Show Demo Paradigm

AI agents are transforming what a trade show demonstration looks like. The traditional demo -- a sales engineer walking through a scripted sequence of clicks -- is being replaced by live, unscripted interactions where attendees pose real questions and the AI agent responds in real time, pulling current data, making recommendations, and even executing actions within the software.

What Agent-Powered Demos Look Like in Practice

  • Interactive product configurators. Instead of a static pricing page, an AI agent takes the attendee's requirements, searches current component pricing, checks inventory across global warehouses, and generates a custom quote in under 30 seconds. The attendee watches the agent's reasoning process on screen, building trust through transparency.
  • Competitive analysis on demand. An attendee asks, "How does your product compare to [Competitor X]?" The AI agent searches the web for the competitor's latest feature announcements and pricing, then generates a side-by-side comparison customized to the attendee's stated needs. This is happening live in booths today.
  • Autonomous lead qualification. AI agents are beginning to handle initial lead qualification conversations, asking attendees about their use cases, budget timelines, and decision-making authority. The agent scores the lead, routes hot prospects to senior sales reps, and schedules follow-up meetings -- all before the attendee leaves the booth.

The Exhibitor Arms Race: Build, Buy, or Get Left Behind

For exhibitors, the proliferation of AI agents creates a strategic fork in the road. Companies with engineering resources are building custom agents tailored to their specific products and buyer personas. Companies without deep technical teams are buying off-the-shelf agent platforms and customizing them with their product data. And a shrinking number of companies are doing neither, relying on the same static demos and printed collateral that worked five years ago.

The data suggests this last group is making a costly mistake. Post-show surveys from major 2025 trade shows indicate that booths featuring interactive AI demonstrations captured 2.3 times more qualified leads than booths relying on traditional demo formats. As AI agents become more sophisticated in 2026, that gap will only widen.

Action Plan: Deploying AI Agents at Your Next Trade Show

You do not need a team of AI engineers to put an agent in your booth. Here is a practical path: (1) Start with a focused use case -- product Q&A, competitive comparison, or meeting scheduling. (2) Use a framework like LangChain or CrewAI with Tavily's search API (now part of the Nebius stack) to build a prototype in days, not months. (3) Load your product documentation, pricing sheets, and FAQ into the agent's knowledge base. (4) Test the agent with real questions from your sales team to identify gaps. (5) Deploy on a tablet or touchscreen kiosk at your booth with a human backup nearby for edge cases. Total cost: typically under $5,000 for a single-show deployment.

What Nebius-Tavily Means for Show Organizers

The rise of AI agents does not just affect exhibitors. Show organizers are grappling with new questions about what constitutes a "staffed booth" when AI agents handle a significant portion of attendee interactions. Badge scanning systems need to integrate with AI agent platforms so that automated conversations still generate trackable lead data. And the bandwidth requirements for show floor Wi-Fi are increasing as hundreds of booths run AI agents that make real-time API calls to cloud services.

Forward-thinking organizers at shows like Web Summit, Collision, and SaaStr Annual are already adapting. Some are offering "AI-ready" booth packages with guaranteed bandwidth, pre-configured API endpoints, and integration support for popular agent frameworks. Others are creating dedicated "AI Agent Alley" sections where startups can demonstrate their agent platforms to attendees who are specifically shopping for this technology.

The Privacy and Trust Equation

AI agents that interact with trade show attendees raise legitimate privacy questions. When an attendee has a conversation with an AI agent at a booth, who owns that data? Is the conversation recorded? Can the exhibitor use it for training future AI models? Are attendees clearly informed that they are interacting with AI rather than a human?

These questions are not hypothetical. GDPR compliance officers at European trade shows are already drafting guidelines for AI agent deployments. Show organizers in the US are updating their exhibitor agreements to address AI-mediated data collection. Exhibitors who deploy AI agents without clear disclosure and data handling policies risk regulatory penalties and, worse, the kind of attendee backlash that poisons a brand's trade show reputation for years.

The Nebius acquisition of Tavily is a deal between two companies most trade show exhibitors have never heard of. But the technology it enables -- AI agents that can search, reason, and act autonomously -- is transforming every trade show floor in the world right now. The exhibitors paying attention are already building. The ones reading this article still have time to catch up. But that window is closing with every show that passes.
